Proofs

A theorem in mathematics is a true proposition. Theorems are often stated as implications A ⇒ B , where A denotes the hypothesis of the theorem and B its conclusion. Proving such a theorem means verifying that the proposition A ⇒ B is a tautology. This section presents six standard methods for proving theorems:

  • vacuous proof;

  • trivial proof

  • direct proof

  • indirect proof (contrapositive and contradiction)

  • proof by cases

  • existence proof (constructive and nonconstructive)

Vacuous Proof

Suppose the hypothesis A of the implication A ⇒ B is false. Then the implication is true regardless of whatever the conclusion B is true or false. Thus, if the hypothesis can be shown to be false, the theorem A ⇒ B is true by default; such a proof is a vacuous proof. Vacuous proofs, though rare, are necessary to handle special cases.

Example. Consider the statement "If 1 = 3, then 5 = 4". Sinche the hypothesis is false, the proposition is vacuosly true. ■

Trivial Proof

Suppose the conclusion of the implication A ⇒ B is true. Again, the implication is true irrespective of the truth value of A. Consequently, if B can be shown to be true, such a proof is a trivial proof.

Example. Consider the statement "If 1 = 3, then Rome is in Italy". This statement is trivially true since the conclusion is true.  ■

Direct Proof

In a direct proof of the theorem A ⇒ B, we assume that the given hypothesis A is true. Using the laws of logic or previously known facts, we then establish the desired conclusion B as the final step of a chain of implications: A ⇒ B1, B1, ⇒ B2, ... , Bn, ⇒ B. Often, theorems are stated in the form of sentences, so we first rewrite them symbolically and then work with the symbols, as the next example shows:

Example. Prove directly that the product of any two odd integers is an odd integer.

Proof. Let x and y be any two odd integers. Thne there exist integers m and n such that x = 2m + 1 and y = 2n + 1. Thus,

x ⋅ y = (2m + 1) (y = 2n + 1)
= 4 mn + 2m + 2n + 1
= 2(2mn + m + n) +1
= 2k + 1

where k = 2mn + m + n is an integer. Thus, xy is also an odd integer.  ■

Inderect Proof

There are two kinds of an indirect proof of the theorem A ⇒ B:

  • proof of the contrapositive;

  • proof by contradiction.

Proof of the Contrapositive

If you ever have trouble proving A ⇒ B directly, you can use the Law of Contraposition and try to prove ¬B ⇒ ¬A instead. The only time i>A ⇒ B will be false occurs when A is true and B is false; the only time ¬B ⇒ ¬A will be false occurs when (¬B) is true and (¬A) is false, that is, B is false and A is true. Therefore the implication and its contrapositive are equivalent, so that to prove that A implies B, we are free to switch to the contrapositive.

Proof by Contradiction

Under a hypotheis A, we are trying to show that a proposition B is true. A proof by contradiction starts out by assuming that the statemsent to be proves is false. Then an argument is made that reaches a contradction. So we conclude that the original statement is true. This technique is known formally as reductio ad absurdum, expressed as

A → B ≡ [A ∧ (¬B)] → F

where F denotes a contradiction. In this method, we assume the given hypothesis A is true, but the conclusion B is false. We then argue logically and reach a contradiction F.

Example (A Not-Divisible Proof) Prove the following statement about divisibility

If n is an integer, the n2 + 2 is not divisible by 4

Proof. Assume the statement is false. Then 4 | (n<2 + 2) for some integer n. This means that n2 + 2 = 4k for some integer k. We shall consider two cases where n is even and where n is odd. If n is even, then n = 2m for some integer m. Substituting for n we obtain

4k = n2 + 2 = (2m + 1)2 + 2 = 4m2 + 4m + 3

Isolate 3 on the right side of the equation to obtain

4k - 4m2 - 4m = 3

This is a contradiction because the left side is even and the right side is odd.
Therefore, n cannot be odd.  ■

Example (Prime Numbers) Every integer greater than 1 is divisible by a prime.

Proof. Assume the statement is false. Then some integer n > 1 is not divisible by a prime. Since a prime divides iteself, n cannot be a prime So there is at least one integer d such that d|n and 1 < d < n. Assume that d is the smallest divisor of n between 1 and n. Now d is not prime, else it would be a prime divisor of n. So there is an integer such that a|d and d |n, it follows from (1.x) that a |n. But now we have a|n and 1 < a < d, which contradicts the assumption that d is the smallest such divisor of n. ■

Example (Euclid’s Theorem on Primes) There is no largest prime number.

Notice that the theorem has no explicit hypothesis. Suppose the given conclusion is false; i.e. there is a largest prime number p. So the prime number we have are 2,3,5,..,p.
Let x denote the product of all these prime numbers plus one: x = ( 2 ⋅ 3 ⋅ 5 ⋅⋅⋅ p) + 1. Clearly, x > p. When x is divided by each of the primes 2, 3, 5, ..., p, we get 1 as remainder. Therefore, x is not divisible by any of the primes and hence x must be a prime. Thus, x is a prime number greater than p.
But this contradics the assumption that p is the largest prime, so our assumption is false. In other words, there is no largest prime number. □

The statement "A if and only if B" abbreviated "A iff B" requires two proofs one for each conditional statement.
