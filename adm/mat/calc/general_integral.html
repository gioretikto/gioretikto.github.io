The general integral of the differential equation

We have seen that a second order linear differential equation can be written in the form

Ly = f

where L: C2(I) ⟶ C0(I) is a linear operator between two vector spaces of functions defined on an interval I. The equation Ly = 0 is said homogeneous equation associated to the complete equation Ly = f. The linearity of L allows to determine easily the structure of the general integral of the equation.

Theorem 11.6.1 (Structure of the general integral of a complete linear equation)

  1. The set of all solutions of the homogeneous equation Ly = 0 in the interval I is a vector space (subspace of C2(I)).

  2. The general integral of the nonhomogeneous equation is obtained is obtained by adding to the general solution of the homogeneous, a particular solution of the nonhomogeneous equation.

Proof.

  1. Let y1,y2 ∈ C2(I) solutions to the homogeneous equation in I, and let λ1, λ2 constants. Then

    L (λ1y1 + λ2y2) = λ1Ly1 + λ2Ly2 = 0

    Thus also λ1y1 + λ2y2 solves the homogeneous equation, showing that the set of solutions of the homogeneous equation is a vector space and in particular a subspace of C2(I) .

  2. Let y1 a particular solution of the nonhomogeneous equation and y0 a solution of the homogeneous equation, that is: Ly1 = f, Ly0 = 0. The by linearity

    L(y1 + y0) = Ly1 + Ly0 = f + 0 = f

    that is y1 + y0 is a solutions of the nonhomogeneous equation. Conversely, if y2 is an arbitrary solution of the nonhomogeneous equation (Ly2 = f) by linearity we have

    L (y2 − y1) = Ly2 − Ly1 = f − f = 0

    that is y2 − y1 is a solution of the homogeneous equation, that is y2 − y1 = y0 for a certain solution y0 of the homogeneous equation. Thus

    y2 = y1 + y0

    that is the general solution of the nonhomogeneous equation can be written as sum of the particular solution y1 of the nonhomogeneous equation plus a solution of the homogeneous equation. □

    Note that in proving the theorem wer used only linearity of L. This result can be extended to linear equations of order n.

    Another important property of the homogeneous equation is the following.

    Theorem 11.6.2 The vector space of the solutions of a second order linear differential equation has dimension 2.

    Proof. The proof is based on Theorem 11.5.3 (existence and unicity of the solution of the Cauchy problem for the equation in Canonical form).
    Let y1, y2 solutions of the equation

    Ly ≡ y'' + a(t) y' + b(t) y = 0

    in the interval I, and let t0 ∈ I such that

    { L y 1 = 0 y 1 ( t 0 ) = 1 y 1 ′ ( t 0 ) = 0 { L y 2 = 0 y 2 ( t 0 ) = 0 y 2 ′ ( t 0 ) = 1

    This is an arbitrary choice made to find a 2-element basis of the vector space of solutions.

    The solutions of equation 11.6.3, y1, y2 are linearly independent. Indeed the quotient y2(t) / y1(t) is zero at t = t0. Can we find a single constant k ∈ R such that either y2(t) = k y_1(t) or y_1(t) = k y_2(t)? There's no such constant? Then y_1(t) and y_2(t) are linearly independent.
