Another method, which is practical only when the degree of the polynomial under consideration is not too large, amounts to looking directly for a factorization. For instance, if f(x) is a polynomial (that we may always suppose primitive and with integer coefficients) of degree 5 and if we have previously checked that the polynomial has no rational roots, then, if f(x) is reducible, it may only decompose as the product of a degree two polynomial and a degree three one. So, writing down the factors with indeterminate coefficients and equating the coefficients of the polynomial and those of the product, we obtain a system and we are interested in its integer solutions: indeed, as we know, we may always reduce to a factorization in ‚Ñ§[x]. If the system is not compatible, then the original polynomial is irreducible.

Methods to study the irreducibility of a polynomial over ‚Ñö

  • Consider without loss of generality only primitive polynomials f(x) ‚àà ‚Ñ§[x];

  • If f(x) is of degree 2 or 3, it is irreducible over ‚Ñö if and only if it has no roots in ‚Ñö; If f(x) has degree 2 then one of its factor is a 1st degree polynomial x ‚àí a; By Ruffin's theorem if f has no root a, then it cannot be divisible by x ‚àí a.

    In the case of f(x) of degree 3, if f were reducible one of its factor would have degree 1, but again if f has no root a, it cannot be divisible by x ‚àí a.

  • Use the test for the existence of rational roots based upon Proposition 4.6.10 to conclude that f(x) is reducible: the existence of roots implies the reducibility of the polynomial;

  • If possible, especially for low degrees, look for a factorization into polynomials with integer coefficients;

  • When there is a prime p verifying the hypothesis of Eisenstein‚Äôs criterion, use it to conclude that f(x) is irreducible over ‚Ñö;

  • use transformations of the form x ‚Üí x + Œ± to be able to apply Eisenstein‚Äôs criterion;

  • consider fÃÑ(x) ‚àà ‚Ñ§p[x] rather than f(x) ‚àà ‚Ñ§[x]: if there exists a p not dividing the leading coefficient of f(x) and such that fÃÑ(x) is irreducible over ‚Ñ§p, then f(x) is irreducible over ‚Ñö.
