Orthogonality

Definition 2.1.1 (orthogonal complement). Let V1 be a k-dimensional subspace of the inner product space V. The set of all vectors v ∈ V (together with 0) that are each orthogonal to all vectors in V1 is called the orthogonal complement V1⊥ (“V1-perp”); that is

V1⊥ = {v ∈ V: v ⋅ u = 0  ∀ u ∈ V1} □

V1⊥ forms a subspace as the next theorem proves.

Theorem 2.1.2 (orthogonal complement is subspace). For every subspace V1 of an inner product space V, the orthogonal complement V1⊥ is a subspace of V. Further, the intersection V ∩ V1⊥ = {0}; that is, the zero vector is the only vector in both V and V1⊥.

Proof. Recall the Definition 1.5.1 of a subspace: we need to establish V1⊥ has the zero vector, and is closed under addition and scalar multiplication.

  • For all u ∈ V1, 0 ⋅ u = 0 and so 0 ∈ V1⊥;

  • Let u1, u2 ∈ V1⊥, then for all u ∈ V1 the dot product (u1 + u2) ⋅ u = u1u + u2 ⋅ u = 0 + 0 and so u1 + u2 ∈ V1⊥.

  • Let scalar c ∈ ℝ and v ∈ V1⊥, then for all u ∈ V the dot product (cv) · u = c(v · u) = c0 = 0 and so cv ∈ V1⊥.

Hence the definition of subspace is verified.

Now we focus on the intersection V ∩ V1⊥. Let u be any vector in both V and V1⊥. As u ∈ V1⊥, by definition u ⋅ v = 0 for all u ∈ V1⊥. But u ∈ V also, so using this for v in the previous equation gives u ⋅ u = 0; that is, |u|2 = 0. Hence vector u has to be the zero vector. That is, V ∩ V1⊥ = {0}. □
