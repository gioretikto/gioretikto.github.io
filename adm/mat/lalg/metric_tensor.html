Metric Tensor

The metric tensor defines the dot product for any space, therefore, it is needed if we want to calculate norms of vectors or any kind of angles. As we have seen in the definition of tensor, any tensor of rank (0, 2) will give a scalar from two vectors and any tensor of rank (2, 0) combines two one-forms to give a scalar. The metric tensor, is a particular (0, 2) tensor field gX and a related (2, 0) tensor field g−1X called the inverse metric tensor for which special distinction is reserved. The metric tensor is a symmetric bilinear scalar function of two vectors. That is, given vectors V and W, g returns a scalar, called the dot product:

g(V,W) = V ⋅ W

Symmetric means g(V,W) = g(W,V) ∀V,W ∈ V, and non-degenerate means g(V,W) = 0 ∀W ∈ V ⇒ V = 0 ∈ V.

The square length of a vector is the dot product of the vector with itself. For vector V:

g(V,V) = V ⋅ V = (V1e1 + V2e2 + V3e3) ⋅ (V1e1 + V2e2 + V3e3) = Vmem ⋅ Vnen = VmVn(em ⋅ en)

We must use two different indices m and n, because in the implicit summation formula Vmem, the symbol m is only a dummy index. So in order not to mix things up, we use another dummy index n for the second expression of V.
The components of the metric tensor are defined as the dot product between basis vectors

gmn = em ⋅ en

So the previous equation becomes

g(V,V) = V ⋅ V = VmVngmn

Considering the displacement vector ds = (dx, dy, dz), in rectangular Cartesian coordinates, its length depends on the geometry of the space, for this we need to know the metric tensor gmn and how it varies from place to place. The generalization of the Pythagoras theorem is

|ds|2 = gmn dXmdXn

It can be represented as a symmetric matrix with dimension equal to the number of intrinsic coordinates:

| d s 2 | = [ d x 1 d x 2 ] [ g 11 g 12 g 21 g 22 ] [ d x 1 d x 2 ]
