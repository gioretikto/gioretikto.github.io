Subspace topology

Definition 2.1.1. Let (X,τ) be a topological space with a subset A ⊆ X. The subspace topology of (X,τ) induced by A is the collection

τA = {O ∩ A: O ∈ τ}

The topological subspace induced by A is the pair (A,τA). □

Definition 2.1.2. Let X = {a, b, c, d, e, f}, τ = {X, Ø, {a}, {c, d}, {a, c, d}, {b, c, d, e, f}}, and A = {b, c, e}. Then the subspace topology on A is

τA = {Y, Ø, {c}}.  ■

Remark. The set A = [0,1) is not open in usual ℝ, but it is open in τA; indeed [0,1) = A ∩ (−1, 1) and (−1, 1) is open in ℝ. So one must pay attention when using the word "open". However there are cases when the "two" open sets coincide.

If a subset is closed in the larger set, then its intersection with the subspace yields a closed subset in the subspaces, and vice versa.

Proposition 2.1.3. Suppose that X is a topological space and A is a subspace of X. Then B ⊆ A is closed in A iff B equals the intersection of a closed set in X with A.

Proof. We begin by assuming B closed in A. By definition A − B is open in A, which by definition of the subspace topology means that

A − B = U ∩ A

where U is some open set in X. Then, by definition, X − U is closed in X. Because of this, and the set-theoretic result that

B = A ∩ (X − U)

we have shown that B equals the intersection of a closed set in X with A, as desired.

To prove the converse assume

B = V ∩ A

where V is closed in X. The definition of being closed yields that X − V is open, and in the subspace topology then, A ∩ (X − V) is necessarily open. Notice that

X − V ∩ A = A − B

Thus, A − B is open, meaning that B is closed. Hence, the result holds.  □
