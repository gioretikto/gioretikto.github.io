Differentiating composite functions

Se f è una funzione differenziabile definita in un aperto A ⊂ ℝ2 e r: [a, b] → A una curva piana regolare parametrizzata di componenti

x = x(t), y = y(t),   t ∈ [a, b],

è definita la funzione composta

F(t) = f(r(t)) = (f ◦ r)(t) = f(x(t), y(t)),  t ∈ [a, b].

Ci poniamo ora il problema di derivare la funzione F(t) così ottenuta, supponendo che siano differenziabili tanto la curva quanto la funzione.

Theorem 1.0 (Chain Rule). Let f(x,y) differentiable in open set A of ℝ2 and r(t) regular planar curve with parametrization

x = x(t),   y = y(t),   t ∈ [a,b]   (1.0)

Ten ∀t ∈ [a,b] there is a unique value f(x(t),y(t)) = F(t); the function F is a composite function, differentiable in [a,b] such that

F'(t) = fx (x(t),y(t)) ⋅ x'(t) + fy (x(t),y(t)) ⋅ y'(t)   (1.1)

or more simply

F'(t) = ∇f(r(t)) ⋅ r'(t)

this relation is also known as the chain rule for differentiating composite maps.

Proof. The increments of the functions x and y are

h = x(t + dt) − x(t);   k = y(t + dt) − y(t)

From the differentiability of f, we can write

F(t + dt) − F(t) = f(x + h, y + k) − f(x,y) = fx(x, y) h + fy(x, y) k + o[√(h2 + k2)]

From the differentiability of x and y, follows that,

h = x(t + dt) − x(t),    k = y(t + dt) − y(t)

The correspondent increments of the variable h and k are

h = x'(t) ⋅ dt + o(dt);   k = y'(t) ⋅ dt + o(dt)

Thus

F(t+dt) − F(t) = [fx⋅ x' + fy ⋅ y'] ⋅ dt + o(dt). □
