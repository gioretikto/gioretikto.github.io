Directional Derivatives

Partial derivatives measure the instantaneous rate of change of the function when moving parallel to the x- and y-axes, respectively. Directional derivatives allow us to find the rate of change f in any direction specified by a versor v = (cos θ, sin θ) at a given point p0 = (x0,y0)

By varying the real parameter t, the points of the line p = p0 + tv are obtained. This line can be represented by the parametric equations:

x = x0 + t cos θ

y = x0 + t sin θ

For a displacement t over this line, the function f is icremented by:

Δf = f (x0 + t cos θ, y0 + t sin θ) − f (x0, y0)

The rate of change is thus one-dimensional. The directional derivative along v is equal to the rate of change f over the rate of change of t, with t tending to zero.

Definition 1.6 Suppose f is C1 in a non empty open set A ∈ ℝ2 and let x be a point ∈ A, with v a unit-vector and t ∈ ℝ. The local rate of change of f along v, at x, is defined as the limit:

limt ⟶ 0 [f(x + tv) − f(x)] / t

when the limit exists, is called the directional derivative of f. Another notation for the directional derivative is Dvf (x).  □

We see that if v = i = (1, 0), then Di f = fx and if v = j = (0, 1), then Dj f = fy. In other words, the partial derivatives of f with respect to x and y are just special cases of the directional derivative.

Therefore the concept of directional derivative generalizes the notion of a partial derivative, we have

Fx(x0,y0) = Dv(x0,y0)   con v = i,   cioè θ = 0.

Fy(x0,y0) = Dv(x0,y0)   con v = j,   cioè θ = π/2.

Gradient formula

Theorem 1.2 (Gradient Formula) Suppose f is C1(A) with A a non empty open set of ℝ2. For every vector v there exists the directional derivative given by:

Dv f(x,y) = ∇f(x,y) ⋅ v

This relation allows also to find the direction along which the maximum rate of change of the function occurs. We have

Dv f(x,y) = ∇f(x,y) ⋅ v = ||∇f(x,y)|| cos θ

The directional derivative along a level curve must be zero as the function does not increase, so

∇f(x,y) ⋅ v = 0

The fact that a dot product is null indicates that v is orthogonal to the gradient vector, i.e. θ = π/2.
The directional derivative is maximized when θ = 0: thus when the v vector is parellel to the gradient and can be expressed by:

v = ∇f(x,y) / ||∇f(x,y)||

The following propositions hold:

Proposition 1.1 At points x0 where the gradient of f does not vanish, f has the greatest rate of increase, starting from x0, in the direction of the gradient, and the greatest decrease in the opposite direction.

Remember that the gradient indicates direction in the xy-plane and not a direction in the 3D space.
