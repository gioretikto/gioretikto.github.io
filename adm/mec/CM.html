The center of mass

To completely determine the motion of a system of particles one would be required to specify the position vector of each particle (or point within the body) as a functim of time. Even though this is theoretically possible in the framework of Newtonian mechanics, it is pratically impossible if the number of particles becomes very large. Furthermore, this approachs actually makes the physics of the motion inscrutable because of the large number of variables which one must keep track of. Fortunately there is a simple reduction of hte motion of a system of particles which can be performed which provides a much more intuitive conceptualization. We now show that the transxlational dynamics of a system can be treated as if the system consisted of a single particle of mass M, the mass of the system, concentrated at a point known as center of mass.

Definition. For a discrete set of particles, the center of mass position vector rcm is defined by

r cm = 1 M ( m 1 r 1 + m 2 r 2 + … + m n r n ) = 1 M ∑ i = 1 n m i r i

Here, the total mass of the set is

M = Σ miri

and ri = xii + yij + xik is the position vector of the ith particle, whose mass is mi. The components of rcm are

x cm = ∑ m i x i M y cm = ∑ m i y i M z cm = ∑ m i z i M

For symmetric system the center of mass is at the center of symmetry. For example, in a binary star system composed of equal-mass starts, the center of mass of the system will be halfway between the two star centers.
Numerically values obtained for the center of mass coordinates have meaning only relative to the origin chose.

Continuous Mass Distribution

We can extend the definition of a continuous mass distribution. An ordinary object, such as a baseball bat, contains so many particles (atoms) that we can best treat it as a continuous distribution of matter. The “particles” then become differential mass elements dm, the sums of Eq. 9-5 become integrals. Thus the center-of-mass position vector is given by

r cm = 1 M ∫ r d m

where M = ∫ dm. The components of rCM are given by

x cm = 1 M ∫ x d m y cm = 1 M ∫ y d m z cm = 1 M ∫ z d m

Evaluating these integrals for most common objects (such as a television set or a motorbike) would be difficult, so there we consider only uniform objects. Such objects have uniform density, ρ, so that dm = ρdV, where dV is the volume occupied by a mass element dm and V is the total volume of the object. Substituting dm = ρdV, in the previous equations gives

x cm = 1 M ∫ x ρ d V y cm = 1 M ∫ y ρ d V z cm = 1 M ∫ z ρ d V

where the integration takes place over the entire volume of the body (The integral is in fact a triple one).

Motion of the center of mass

To describe the dynamics of the center of mass, we define the center of mass velocity

v cm = d d t r cm = 1 M ∑ m i d r i d t

that is

v cm = 1 M ∑ m i v i

Analogously

a cm = d d t v cm = 1 M ∑ m i d 2 r i d t 2

that is

a cm = 1 M ∑ m i a i  10.1.1

We shall show that the motion of the center of mass of the system and consequently aCM is not affected by internal forces. Only an external force can alter the motion of the center of mass of a system. Internal force are exerted by particle of the system on other particle of the system. External forces instead are exerted on the particles from outside the system.
Let Fij be the internal force exerted on the jth particle by the ith particle. And let Fext,j the external force exerted on the point j by the outside of the system.

Consider a system of three point particles with mass m1, m2 and m3. The acceleration of CM is given by eq .10.1.1 that is Macm = m1 a1 + m2a2 + m3a3. The acceleration of each material point is given by Newton's second law,

m1a1 = ΣF1 = F21 + F31 + Fext1

m2a2 = ΣF2 = F12 + F32 + Fext2

m3a3 = ΣF3 = F13 + F23 + Fext3

Thus

Macm = (F21 + F31 + Fext1) + (F12 + F32 + Fext2) + (F13 + F23 + Fext3)

Based on the third law of the Newton, the internal force exerted by particle i on particle j equals in magnitude the force exerted by particle j on particle i, Fij = −Fji; All the internal forces cancel out and give out

Macm = Fext1 + Fext2 + Fext3 = ΣFext  10.1.2

The acceleration of the CM is determined solely by the external forces.
