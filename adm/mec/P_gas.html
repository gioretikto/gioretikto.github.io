Calculation of the Pressure of a Gas

For the molecules of gas in a closed box, in the absence of external forces, ail positions in the box and ail directions of velocity are equaily probable. The individual molecules all have their own instantaneuous velocity v with components vx, vy, vz and therefore magnitude

v = (vx2 + vy2 + vz2)½,  12.1.1

since the directions are equally probable Σ v = 0, and therefore the average velocity ⟨v⟩ must be zero. This is true whether we compute the average velocity of an individual molecule or over a long time period of the instantaneuous average velocity of a large number of individual molecules. The same applies if we analyze a particular direction of motion by the principle of molecular chaos with respect to the x direction of motion, exactly as many molecules of the gas will have a velocity component +vx as a component —vx; the mean value must therefore vanish, ⟨vx⟩ = 0. A value of ⟨vx⟩ differing from zero would imply a mean motion of the whole gas in the direction concerned, with this mean velocity. On the other hand ⟨vx2⟩ is not equal to zero and as a consequence the gas molecules have a mean speed ⟨v⟩. This is because although the directions cancel out the magnitudes of the molecular velocities, that is, their speeds, do not.
The mean speed (or average speed), vmean, of a sample consisting of N molecules with speeds v1, v2 vn is

v m e a n = v 1 + v 2 + … + v n N

Another way to measure the mean speed of the molecules, is the root-mean-square speed, vrms, defined as the square root the mean value of the squares of the speeds, which for sample consisting of N molecules with speeds v1, v2 vn is

v r m s = ⟨ v 2 ⟩ = v 1 2 + v 2 2 + … + v n 2 N

Root-mean-square speed is the measure of the speed of particles in a gas that is most convenient for problem solving within the kinetic theory of gases as it is related directly with the mean kinetic energy of a collection of molecules, as

⟨Ek⟩ = ½ mvrms2 = ½ m⟨v2⟩ 12.1.2

The rms speed is quite close in value to the mean speed, of the molecules.

Pressure is defined as the force exerted per unit area. In a gas, the force is equal to the rate of change of momentum of the molecules when they collide with the container walls. To calculate the pressure you need to know how many molecule collide with the wall in one second and how much change of momentum takes place with each collision. To simplify the calculation, we focus on the momentum changes occuring in only one dimension x − and one of the walls of the container shown in Fig.1.

If vx is the velocity component of a molecule in the x- direction, then the momentum in the x-direction before the collision is mvx. Since the collision is elastic, after the collision, the momentum must be −mvx, i.e, the same magnitude but in the opposite direction. Thus the change of momentum per collision is given by

change of momentum = (momentum before collision) − (momentum after collision) = mvx −(−mvx) = 2mvx

The momentum in the y- and z- directions is unchanged on collision with the wall.

Next, we need to calculate the number of molecules colliding with the wall in one second. For a container with wall area A, all the molecules that are moving toward the wall that are contained withing a volume Avx will hit, as shown in Fig.2.

If the gas contains N molecules per unit volume, then

No. molecules hitting the wall per second = ½NAvx

The factor ½ is introduced because, on average, only half the molecules will be heading towards the wall, while the other hald will be heading in the opposite direction. We can obtain an expression for the total change of momentum in one second as

rate of change of momentum = (No. molecules colliding the wall in one second) ⋅ (change of momentum on each collision)
½NAvx ⋅ 2mvx = NmAvx2

This expression assumes that all molecules are moving with the same velocity vx in the x-direction. In reality there is a range of speeds, that allow molecules to hit the wall in one second, so we reaplce vx by ⟨vx2⟩, which is its value averages over all the gas molecules. Then

rate of change of momentum = NA⟨vx2⟩/A = NA⟨vx2⟩ 12.1.3

This equation gives the force on the wall caused by all the collision in the x−diretion. Since p = F/a, the pressure on the wall is obtained by dividing by the area, A, of the wall

p = NA⟨vx2⟩/A = Nm⟨vx2⟩

Since molecules are moving randomly in all directions, it is possible to obtain an expression for ⟨vx2⟩ in terms of the total mean square velocity ⟨v2⟩. The average of a sum of terms is the sum of the averages of those terms so

⟨v2⟩ = ⟨vx2 + vy2 + ⟨vz2⟩ = ⟨vx2⟩ ⟨vy2⟩ + ⟨vz2⟩

Now we assume that the motion is completely isotropic (assumption 2), which implies that no direction is preferred. On the average, the x, y, and z directions are equivalent, so

⟨vx2⟩ = ⟨vy2⟩ = ⟨vz2⟩

which allows us to write

⟨v2⟩ = 3⟨vx2⟩

Remember that, for the purpose of this derivation, N is the number of molcules per unit volume. It is more convenient to think about the numbers of moles n, of the gas, so, as an alternative, you can write

N = nNA/V

where n is the number of moles of a gas in the total volume of the container V, and NA is the Avogadro constant.
Incorporating these last two changes in Eq. 12.1.3 leads to

p = m 3 ( n N A V ) ⟨ v 2 ⟩

By Eq. 12.1.2, we have that

pV = 2/3 ⋅ nNA ⋅ ⟨Ek⟩

This result shows that the pressure is proportional to the number of molecules per unit volume (our N) and to the average translational kinetic energy of the molecules; One way to increase the pressure inside a container is to increase the number of molecules per unit volume in the container (N). You do so when you add air to a tire.
