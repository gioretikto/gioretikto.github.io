Rocket Propulsion

A rocket obtains its thrust by ejecting gas from an engine in its tail. The rocket pushes on the gas, and the gas puches back. As a rocket moves in free space, its linear momentum changes when some of its mass is ejected out the engine in the form of exhaust gases. Because the gases are given momentum when they are ejected out of the engine, the rocket receives a compensating momentum in the opposite direction. Therefore, the rocket is accelerated as a result of the "push", or thrust, from the exhaust gases.

Let the initial mass of the rocket be M and let v its initial velocity, relative to some inertial reference frame. The rocket engines burn an amount of fuel dm. The burned fuel is expelled from the rocket exhaust tubes at a velocity u with respect to the rocket. Furthermore, we assume that the exhaust gas is ejected in a direction oppoiste to the velocity vector (u = −uv/v). The initial total momentum is

Pi = Mv

The total final momentum of the rocket plus burned fuel is

Pf = (M − dm) (v + dv) + dm(v + u)

Since the system of the rocket and the ejected fuel is isolated, we apply the law of conservation of linear momentum for system, and write

(M − dm) (v + dv) + dm(v + u) = Mv

Next we carry out the indicated multiplications and discar all second order differentials (The term dM ⋅ dv is 2nd order differential we can discard). This procedure yields

Mdv = −udM

Dividing both sides by dt gives

M dv/dt = −udM/dt  10.5.1

on the left-hand side dv/dt represents the acceleration of the rocket and M its mass, therefore the LHS is mass times acceleration. Therefore, teh RHS looks like the force on the rocket, usually called the thrust. The thrust depends on the rate at which fuel is burned (dM/dt) and on the speed with which the burned fuel is ejected, u. To generate a large thrust, a rocket engine is designed to burn fuel very rapidly (large dM/dt) and to expel it as high a speed as possible (large u).

How will the velocity of a rocket change as it consumes its fuel? We can rewrite Eq. 10.5.1 as

d v = − u d M M

In magnitude db = − dM/M.
Integrating leads to

∫ v i v f d v = − u ∫ M i M f d M M

in which Mi is the initial mass of the rocket and Mf its final mass. Evaluating the integrals then gives

vf − vi = u ln(Mi/Mf)

this equation shows that the increase in rocket speed is proportional to the exhaust speed u of the ejected gases. Therefore, the exhaust speed should be very high. Second, the increase in rocket speed is proportional to the natural logaritm of the ratio Mi/Mf. Therefore, this ratio should be as large as possible; taht is, the mass of the rocket without its fuel should be as small as possible and the rocket should carry as much fuel as possible.
