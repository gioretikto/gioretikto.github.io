The Center of gravity

The gravitational force on an extended body is the vector sum of the gravitational forces acting on the individual mass elements (the atoms) of the body. Let mi represent the mass of one of these elements, we have

Fg = Σi mig = (Σi mi)g = Mg

where Σi mi is the total mass of the body and g ther gravitational acceleration.
By summing the weights of each of the elemental parts we obtain the total weight of the body which is independent on the application point of the single forces.

However there exist torques created by the weights. The weight of each elementa part of the body will create a moment with respect to an axis. For the force Fi = mig, the component of the moment τzi is

τzi = −(xi)(mig)

where xi is the x coordinate of the ith elemental mass part and the negative sign indicated that the rotation is clockwise. By summing over all the moments produced by all the elemental mass parts we obtain

τz = Σi [−(xi)(mig)] 11.3.1

From this relation we can see that the net torque due to the weight of a body depends on how the single parts of the body are distributed in space. It is possible to find a point in the body known as center of gravity at which the entire mass or weight of the body may be considered to be applied generating the same torque of the previoud equation. If xcg is the x coordinate of such a point, the component of the moment due to the total weightm of intensity Fg = Σi mig, is

τz = −xcgFg − xcg Σi mig 11.3.2

The x coordinate of the center of gravity is determined by equating the right-hand sides of equations 11.3.1 and eq. 11.3.2:

x cg = ∑ i x i m i g ∑ i m i g
The center of gravity is the point at which the entire mass or weight of the body may be considered to be concentrated .

In vast majority of cases the value of g is the same for all parts of the body and can thus be eliminated, obtaining

x cg = ∑ i x i m i ∑ i m i

We recognize on the right-hand side of this equation the center of mass of the body. In general, there is a difference between the centers of mass and gravity of an object. Htis may be worth considering if the object is large enough for the magnitude of g to vary at different parts of the object.
