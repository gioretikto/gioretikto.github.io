Integration of Newtonw's Equation of Motion

Newton's 2nd law can be written as a second-order differential equation

md2r/dt2 = F

which is called the equation of motion of the particle of mass m. When the force F is known, the equation of motion becomes a second-order differential equation for the unknown function r(t), the path or otbit of the particle. Solving problems by integrating the equation of motion includes the forllowing operations:

  1. Start by choosing a suitable frame of reference (e.g. a coordinate sytstem) in which the solution can be greatly simplified.

  2. Integrate the equations of motion. The integration will depend upon the form of force function on the right-hand side of the equation of motion.

  3. Determine the constants of integration. The integration of the equations of motion will lead to the introduction of constants of integration; the values of integration constants are determined from the initial conditions. If the differential equation of motion is an equation with separable variables, rather than introducing integration constants, we can evaluate the definite integrals on both sides

In general, the force function may be a function of position, velocity, or time. A problem where the applied force is a function of all three variables simultaneously is difficult to solve. The problems that we shall consider, therefore, generally fall into on of the following four categories:

  1. The applied force is constant.

  2. The applied force is time dependent.

  3. The applied force is velocity dependent.

  4. The applied force is position dependent.
