Conservation of Linear Momentum

According to Newton's first law of motion “A body remains at rest or continues to move with a uniform velocity if no external force is acting on it.” Considering a particle of mass m, then if v does not vary with time then p = mv as well is constant with time. Newton's first law of motion is only a special case of Newton's second law according to which F = dp/dt, and if the applied force F = 0 then dp/dt = 0 or p = mv = a constant.

According to Newton's second law for a system of particles, expressed by Eq. 10.2.3, if the net external force Fnet acting on the system of particles is zero (the system is isolated) then dP/dt = 0, which means that

P = constant  (closed, isolated system)

In words,

If no net external force acts on a system of particles, the total linear momentum P of the system cannot change.

This result is called the law of conservation of linear momentum and is an extremely powerful tool in solving problems. Supposing that the net force acting on a system is null: let P1 and P2 be respectively the initial and final total linear momentum of the system, we have then

P1 = P2  or  dP /dt = 0  (closed, isolated system)

If Newtown's second and third law of motion hold good, the total linear momentum of the system of two particles remains constant.

Thus, the law of conservation of linear momentum can be applied to a single particle, to a system of particles or even to the centre of mass of the particles.
