get + prepositions

get away

1. to start, to go away, to escape

  • Don't ask her how she is, if she starts talking about her health you'll never get away from her

  • They say he has managed to get away for the holidays

get away with

1. to escape punishment

  • You have broken down their plans. I don't think you'll manage to get away with it

get to

get to a place: to arrive at...

  • It is very difficult to get to Jame's house in Harrington Road

get on

1. to mount, to board 2. to put on 3 to become aged

  • Let's get on the first bus that comes along

  • Don't forget to get on your coat

  • How old is she now? I don't know, but she is getting on (in years)

get on (with)

1. to make progress, to advance 2. to be on good terms

  • How are you getting on?

  • The building is getting on fast

  • I must get on with my work

  • I have always got on well with my colleagues

get off

1. to remove oneself, to alight (to come down from /a bus, etc.) 2. to escape punishment or misfortune 3. to remove smt.

  • I usually get off before the bus comes to the terminus.

  • No passenger is getting off at this bus stop

  • He should have been punished but he got off

  • He couldn't get the paint off his sleeve

  • I can't get this screw-cap off

get out

1. to dismount, to alight, to get off; 2. to take smb or smt out; 3. to cause to be out

  • When the plane landed he was the first to get out

  • I can't get the key out

  • My children like swimming so much that I can't get them out of the water

  • The position is difficult but there is no getting out of it

  • The reporters got the whole story out of him.

get into

to put on with difficulty

  • He can't get into this pullover. It's shrunk

get over

1. to cross, to climb, or be over; to cover 2. to recover (from an illness, a shock, surprise, dissapointment, etc,) 3. to convey (knowledge, information, etc.)

  • I'm sure they will get over the distance in some twenty minutes

  • Tom Sawyerget over the fence and was gone

  • He is getting over flu well

  • I can't get over his bad manners

  • We'll have to get over that difficulty somehow

  • He has a through knowledge of his subject but hte is unable to get over it to an audience

get round (around)

1. to persuade smb. in one's interest 2. to evade with impunity (law, regulations) 3. to spread

  • She knows how to get round her parents

  • He tried to get round the regulations

  • The story has got round

get through

1. to reach the end 2. to pass a test 3. to contact smb. on the telephone 4. to spend (time, money, etc.)

  • I hopoe we'll get through the pile of work on Saturday

  • He's got through the examination

  • We tried to telephone you last night, bu the line was busy and we couldn't get throigh to him

  • They have got through the money you gave him
