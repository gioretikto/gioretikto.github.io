Degree determiners: Much, many, little, few, a lot of, lots, plenty

Forme affermative: a lot of

We use a lot of/lots of/plenty of with both uncountable and plural nouns:

  • a lot of luck, a lot of friends, They've got a lot of things to do

  • lots of time, lots of people

  • plenty of money, plenty of ideas.

When we want to indicate "a large quantity of" or "a large number of", we use different words, depending on wheter we want to ask something, make a negative statement or make an affermative statement.

In affermative we only use a lot of or lots of with both uncountable and countable nouns. In questions we use much (with uncountables) and many (with countable), or a lot of with both countable and uncountable nouns.

  • Is there much money in your bank?

  • Are there many boys playing football?

  • Did you see a lot of children on the beach?

  • Have they got many things to do?

Much is unusual in positive sentence (especially in splen English)

  • We spent a lot of money (not spend much money)

Forme negative: a lot of; much; many

In negatives we use not ... much (with uncountables) and not ... many (with countables), or not ... a lot of with both countable and uncountable nouns.

  • There isn't much money in my bank

  • They haven't got many things to do

“Many people,” is correct. “Much people,” is always incorrect.

  • Many people drive too fast or A lor of people drive too fast.

“Many” should refer to multiple individuals; “much” means a large amount of some kind of stuff, commodity or substance.

  • You can have many dollars, but you have much money.

  • The beach has many pebbles, but has much gravel.

  • There are many people living in this small place, so there is much crowding.

You can say, “There were a lot of people,” or, “There was a huge crowd (of people),” or, “There were many people there,” etc.

More and Most

We use more and most instead of much/many in comparsions. We use more for "a larger number or amount" and most for "the largest number or amount".

So if you have 4 types of affected... 10%, 30%, 50%, 90%, 30, 50 and 90 are more affected than 10, but only 90 is most affective.

  • More children are being educated at home. They are spending more time with their parents

  • Most American teenagers say they have most fun when they are shopping "at the mall".

We can use more of and most of before determiners, pronouns and proper nouns.

  • I've already eaten more of the cake than I should

  • Most of those bananas were rotten.

  • I really liked it, but I can't eat any more of it

  • I had to thtrow most of them away.

  • I hope to see more of Spain during my next trip.

  • Most of Venice is under water

We can use other quantifiers before more (not most) with the meaning 'additional'.

  • I don't need much more time, just two more hours.

  • Is there any more tea? There's no more
