Determinative pronouns and adjective

Determinative pronouns are used to locate or designate a particular thing without locating it in space or time. Latin demostrative pronouns are:

ĭs (masculine), eă (feminine), ĭd (neuter), he/she/it, that, etc.

idem, eădem, idem, the same

ipse, ipsa, ipsum, he, self

Determinative pronouns can be used both as pronouns and as adjectives; in the nominative case they can also be used as personal pronouns. They thus declined:

Is, eă, id

Is can be used either as personal pronoun, or as a demostrative («that»).

When used as a pronoun is, eă, id can function as a 3rd person personal pronoun which in latin has no proper nominative form. In this case, it refers back to things/persons already mentioned in the sentence:

  • Apud Helvetios nobilissimum et ditissimus fuit Orgetorīx. Is coniūrationem nobilitātis fecit - «Among the Helvetians the noblest by far and the most wealthy was Orgetorix. - «He [Orgetorīx] laid a conspiracy of the nobilityt»

Is can be used as well as antecedent of a relative pronoun (is qui, «the one who»):

  • Is amicus est, qui iuvat in rebus adversis - «A friend is the one who come to help during hard times»

  • Ubi sum is, qui miser dico? - «Where are those whom you call miserable?»

or it can refer back to an entire proposition:

  • Quod honestum est, id solum bonum est (Cic.)- «Only what is honest is good»

As an adjective it indicates a person/thing:

  • Id ego ius iurandum patri datum usque ad hanc aetatem ita conservavi (Nep.) - «I have kept this oath given to my father until now»

Because there isn't a 3rd person non-reflexive possessive adjective in Latin (suus, -a, -um, are used only if the possessor is the subject of the sentence), the possessive genitive of is, eă, id is used instead:

  • Semper amavi, M. Brutum, propter eius summum ingenium - «I have ever loved, Marcus Brutus for his great wit»

  • Deum agnoscis ex operibus eius - «You recognise God from His works»

  • Probamus milites propter eorum audacia - «We praise soldiers for their boldness»

Idem

Idem, «the same» is composed of Is + the indeclinabile particle -dem. Similarly yo Is, in the nominative masculine singular can have the forms iidem, eidem (rare) and idem (contracted), and in the dative and ablative plural the forms iisdem, eisdem (rare) and isdem (contratta).

  • Ciconĭae nidos eosdem repĕtunt (Plin.)- «Storks return to the same nest»

  • Non eadem omnibus esse honesta atque turpia (Nep.) -«The same things are not becoming or unbecoming among all people».

Ipse

Ipse is an emphatic pronoun/adjective, used particularly in indirect speech, but never used as a reflexive (i.e. it must not be confused with the reflexive pronoun se) but just for clarity's sake; :

  • Alexander ipse prima acie pugnabat, «Alexander himself fought in first line»

  • Senects ipsa morbus est (Ter.) , «Old age, itself, is a disease»

Ipse is composed of is + the declinable particle -pse (from is-pse > ipse); unlike idem, in ipse, the pronominal part i- remains unchanged while the particles -pse, -psa, -psum are declined.
