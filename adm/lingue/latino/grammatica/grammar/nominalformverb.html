Nominal forms of verb

The Nominal forms of verb are the infinitive, participle, gerund, gerundive and supine; they are so called because they combine the verbal characteristics of tense and voice with the nominal characteristics of case, number, and gender. The nominal forms can function as

  • subject: infinitive, gerund and supine;

  • adjectives: participle and gerundive.

The most important of these verbal forms is the infinitive that we are going to anylize in its nominal and verbal characteristics. The noun infinitive can be a subject (To eat is fun.); a direct object (I like to eat.); a predicate nominative (A fun thing is to eat.); an appositive (My hope, to travel, never happened.); an object of a preposition (I want nothing but to save.)

The infinitive

When used as a substantive, the infinitive is considered neuter and singular, but retains its ability to govern an object and is modified by an adverb (not an adjective).

The Infinitive as asubject

The infinitive can be employed as the subject of the sentence:

  • with the verb sum and an neuter adjective, or a noun, or adverb as in:

    • iustum est, lex est, pulchrum est, mos est, consuetudo est, etc.

    • stultum est hoc facere

    • Errare humanum est (Sen.) -

    • Miserrimum est arbitrio alterīus vivere. (P. Syr.) -È tristissimo vivere ad arbitrio altrui.

    • Dulce et decorum est pro patria mori. (Cic.) -

  • with impersonal verbs like: decet, dedĕcet, piget, paenitet, oportet, interest, necesse est, opus est, placet, licet, etc., as in:

    • Confidere decet, timere non decet (Cic.) -

    • Homini necesse est mori. (Cic.) - That men should die is anavoidable.

  • With the verb sum adn an objective genitive (consulis, imperatoris, sapientis etc, est):

    • Est imperatoris imperare

  • with the gentive and the verb sum (genitive of pertinence)

  • When the infinitive of a copulative verb in function of subject is accompanied by a nominal predicate, this must be expressed in the accusative case:

    • Non esse cupidum pecunia est. (Cic.) - Non essere avido è una ricchezza.

    • Non esse emacem, vectigal est

    • Mario consulem fieri utile videbatur (Cic.) -

    • Quam miserum est fieri metuendo senem! (P. Syr.) - Quant’è triste diventar vecchio temendo!

Infinitive as a Nominal Predicate

The infinitive is used as nominal predical, connected with the copula, generally when the subject is a another infinitive or a neuter pronoun.

  • Docto homini vivere est cogitare (Cic.) -

  • Hoc non est vivere (Cic.) -

  • Hoc est ignoscere. (Sen.) Questo è perdonare.

  • Vivere militare est. (Sen.)- Vivere è essere soldato.

If the infinitive used as a nominal predical is also connected to a nominal part, this if found in rendered in the accusative case.

  • Hoc non est esse regem (Cic.) -

The explicative infinitive

The explicative infinitive is used to clarify a neuter pronoun or another generic noun occuring before it in the sentence:

  • Id iniustissimum est, iustitiae mercedem quaerere. (Cic.) - Questo è il colmo dell’ingiustizia, farsi pagare per la giustizia.

  • Ecce res magna, habere imbecillitatem hominis, securitatem dei. (Sen.) - Ecco una cosa grande, avere la debolezza di un uomo e l’imperturbabilità di un dio.

The infinitive as direct object

The indinitive is used a direct object of verbs like possum, volo, nolo, malo, debeo, soleo, incipio, queo, etc.) known as Verba Voluntatis.

  • Caesar urbem atque Italiam tueri non potest. (Caes.) - Cesare non può difendere Roma e l’Italia.

The infinitive stands as an object after many verbs which express an incomplete idea, and require the infinitive to complete it as - audeo; peto; duco («ritenere»); statuo, constituo, decerno («decidere», «deliberare», «stabilire»); scio, nescio («sapere», «non sapere»); festīno, propĕro, maturo («affrettarsi»); pergo, persevēro («continuare»); desĭno, desisto («cessare», «lasciare»); audeo («osare»); conor («tentare»); contendo, nitor («sforzarsi»); dubito («esitare»); doceo («insegnare»); disco («imparare»); assuesco, assuefio, consuesco («avvezzarsi», «essere solito») etc.

  • Petimus bene vivere (or.)

  • Statui ad te scribere. (Cic.)

  • Mori nemo sapiens miserum duxit. (Cic.)

When the infinitive used as direct object is itself part of a nominal predicate, this is rendered in the nominative case if it is the subject of the sentence:

  • Non possunt omnes esse patricii. (Cic.)

  • Cupio esse sapiens

The Infinitive as second term of the comparison

  • Periculosius est timeri quam despĭci.

  • Esse quam vidēri bonus malebat (Sall.)
