Distributive Numerals

Distributive numerals denote an equal number distributed among several objects, or at different times answering the question: Quotēni? = How many to each?. These Latin numerals may be translated in various ways as: bini, two each, two together, two by two, and singuli one by one.

ageri septēna iugera plēbi divisa sunt, «seven jugera to each citizen»

Distributive numerals are always used in the pluarl and are declined as the first/second declension adjectives (like bonus). The masculine genitive plural and neuter end generally in -um rathar than in-orum, with the exception of singuli which is always singolorum.

Distributive are used, in place of the cardinals, with the pluralia tantum and with nouns that in the plural have a meaning different from the singular; in this case, however, uni is used instead of singuli, and trini instead of terni:

unae nuptiae, only one marriage

trinae nuptiae, «three marriages»

binae aedes, two houses

duae aedes, «two temples»

unae litterae, «a letter» (epistle)

una littera, «a letter (of the alphabet)»

trinae litterae, «three letters» (epistle)

tres litterae, «three letters (of the alphabet)»

una castra, «a camps»

unum castrum, «a fort»

bina castra, «two camps»

duo castra, «two forts»

trina castra, «three camps»

tria castra, «three forts»

Note the difference:

  • Cardinal: tria castra: «three forts»

  • Distributive in place of Cardinal: trina castra: «three camp»

  • Distributive: terna castra: «three by three forts/camps»

Distributives are also used in multiplication, to denote the number of times that one number or quantity contains another, as:

  • bis bina, «twice two», (i.e. four)

  • ter ternae, «thrice three» (i.e. nine)

  • ter septēnis diēbus, «in thrice seven days»

  • bis seni pueri, «twice six boys»

  • ter novēnae virginēs, «thrice nine maidens»

In ordinary language, distributives occur only in the plural, but poets use them sometimes in the singular, in the sense of multiplicatives:

centum arboribus = centēna arbore, «with a tree a hundred fold»

bīnum corpus Lucr., a double body

Poets can also use distributives where pairs or sets are spoken:

bīna hasilia, «two shafts»
