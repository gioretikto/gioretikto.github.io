Ablative of Means or Instrument

The Ablative of Means or Instrument indicates the animal, person, or thing by which an action is accomplished. There are two possible constructions to express the mean whereby an action is done:

  • Ablative without preposition (simple Ablative) when the mean is an animal or thing;

    • Cornibus tauri, apri dentibus, morsu leones, se tutantur (Cic.) - Bulls with (their) horns, boars with (their) tusks, lions by biting, defend themselves

    • Sic enim Populo erat persuasum et adversas superiores, et praesentes secundas res, accidisse eius operā (Nep.)- For thus the People were persuaded, that both their former ill success, and their present good success, had happened through his means.

    • Aut vi aut fraude (Cic.) - Wrong is done in two manners, either by force (lit. with force) or by fraud (lit. with force).

    In the last example the ablative of instrument was rendered in English with the preposition by instead of with, but it has not to be confused with the ablative of agent. Parse the following sentence:

    • Deinde in conspectu eius Papinianus securi percussus est, et a militibus occisus (Historia Augusta, XIII. Caracalla) - Next, in the Emperor’s own presence, Papinian was struck with an axe by some soldiers and so slain.

      securi is the ablative of instrument, a militibus is an ablative of agent.

  • per + accusative when the mean is a person.

    • Artaxerxes, rex Persarum, legatos in Graeciam mittit, per quos iubet omnes ab armis discedere. (Iustin.)- Artaxerxes, king of Persia, sent ambassadors to Greece to command a truce.

    • Curius, ubi intelligit, quantum periculum confuli impendeat, propere per Fulviam Ciceroni dolum, qui parabatur, enunciat. (Sall.) - Curius, upon finding how great a danger the Cicero was in, immediately dispatches away Fulvia to him, to give him notice of the design

Slaves, and soldiers are often treated as instruments and the simple ablative is used:

  • Hannibal militibus castra vexavĕrat - Hannibal had devastated the camp with his soldiers.

  • Interea ea legione quam secum habebat, militibusque qui ex provincia convenerant, murum fossamque perducit (Caes.) - Meanwhile, with that legion which he had with him, and with the soldiers who had assembled from the Province, he carried along a wall and trench.
