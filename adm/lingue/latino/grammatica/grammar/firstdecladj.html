The declension of adjective

Adjectives largely follow the same inflectional patterns as nouns. They are characterized by:

  • 3 genders: masculine, feminine and neuter

  • 2 numbers: singular and plural

  • 6 cases: nominative, genitive, dative, accusative, vocative, ablative.

Latin adjectives belong to two declensional classes:

  • The first class includes: adjectives whose inflectional endings correspond to the first and second declension.

  • The second class includes: adjectives whose inflectional endings correspond to the third declension.

1st/2nd declension of adjectives

As mentioned before, the adjectives of the first/second adjectives are based on the model of the first and second declination. The vocabulary entries for adjectives give the form of the masculine, feminine and neuter nominative singular, as follows:

masculine feminine Neuter to read as meaning
bonus -a-um bonus, bona, bonum «good»
gratus -a-um gratus, grata, gratum «pleasing»
malus -a-um malus, mala, malum «bad»
magnus -a-um magnus, magna, magnum «big»
miser-ĕra-ĕrum miser, misĕra, misĕrum «poor»
piger-gra -grum piger, pigra, pigrum «lazy»

It is possible to recognize first class adjective by dictionary form that end in us, a, um or in r, in the nominative singular.

Let's see the declension of bonus, bona, bonum, «good»:

  Masculine Feminine Neuter Masculine Feminine Neuter
Nominative bon-ŭs bon-ă bon-ŭm bon-ī bon-ae bon-ă
Genitive bon-ī bon-ae bon-ī bon-ōrŭm bon-ārŭm bon-ōrŭm
Dative bon-ō bon-ae bon-ō bon-īs bon-īs bon-īs
Accusative bon-ŭm bon-ăm bon-ŭm bon-ōs bon-ās bon-ă
Vocative bon-ĕ bon-ă bon-ŭm bon-ī bon-ae bon-ă
Ablative bon-ō bon-ā bon-ō bon-īs bon-īs bon-īs

It can be noticed, that:

  • the masculine bonus is declined as nouns of the second declension ending in -us (lupus);

  • the feminine bona follows the first declension (like puella);

  • The neuter, bonum is declined as neuter nouns of the second declension (like otium).

It can be noticed, that:

  • The masculine (liber) is declined as male nouns of the second declension ending in -er (like vesper, vespĕri; piger as ager, agri);

  • The feminine (libera, pigra) follows the first declension (rosa);

  • The neuter (librum, pigrum) is decline as nueter nouns of the second declension (like ortum).

All adjective ending in er have the nominative singular equal to the vocative.

Agreement of Adjectives with Nouns

A latin noun agress with an adjective in case, gender, and number, but not in its case declension-class membership (e.g. pater bonus, "good pater"; bonus matches the singular number and nominative case of pater, but pater belongs to the third declension). Some examples are:

poëta clarus, "The famous poet"

bona puella, "The good girl"

puer malus, "A bad boy"

pīnus alta, "A tall pine tree"

Quercus magna, "A large oak"

nauta peritus, "A skilled sailor"

As an example the declension of Bona Puella, "the good girl", is as follows:

Case Singular Meaning Plural Meaning
Nominative bona puella The good girl bonae puellae The good girls
Genitive bonae puellae of the good girl bonarum puellarum of the good girls
Dative bonae puellae to/for the good girl bonis puellis to/for the good girls
Accusative bonam puellam The good girl bonas puellas The good girls
Ablative bona puella with/by/from/etc. the good girl bonis puellis with/by/from/etc. the good girls

This is how "pinus alta", "the tall pine tree", is declined:
