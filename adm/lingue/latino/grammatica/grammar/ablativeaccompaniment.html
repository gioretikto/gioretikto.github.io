Ablative of Accompaniment

The ablative case with the preposition cum can indicate the person with whom the action is done. This construction is known as Ablative of Accompaniment:

  • Laevinus in Macedonia cum Philippo et multis Graeciae populis amicitiam fecit (Eutr.)- Laevinus entered into an alliance with Philip, and several people of Greece.

  • Pompeius mare transiit cum omnibus militibus, quos secum habuit (Cic.) - Pompeius crossed the sea with all the soldiers that he had with him

  • Cum Pansa vixi in Pompeiano (Cic. Att. XIV) - I lived with Pansa at Pompeii

the ablative of accompaniment is sometimes extended to apply to inanimate objects this is known as ablative of unione:

  • Cum febri domum rediit (Cic.) - He came back home with a temperature

  • Hic magno cum pondere auri Thebas venit (Nep.) - He came to Thebes with a vast Quantity of Gold

Notas

The preposition cum, may be omitted with military terms as agmen, exercitus, copiae, comicatus, legio, manus, navis etc. accompanied by an attribute (not numeral); In this case the sense of instrument prevails on that of accompaniment (see Ablative Instrument).

  • Albani priores ingenti exercitu in agrum Romanum impetum fecere (Liv.) - The albans for first assaulted the Roman territory with a powerful army.

When this terms appear withou attribute the preposition cum cannot be omitted:

  • Hac victoria sublatus Ambiorix statim cum equitatu in Aduatucos, qui erant eius regno finitimi, proficiscitur (Caes.) - Ambiorix elated with this victory marches immediately with his army to Namur, which joined to his kingdom.
