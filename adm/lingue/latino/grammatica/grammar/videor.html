The Verb Videor

The verb videor (videris, visus sum, videri) the passive of video, means "seem", "appear" and may either be used personally, or impersonally with the infinitive.

Personal Construction

In the personal construction, videor agress with the subject of the infinitive proposition which is put in the nominative case rather than in the accusative as usual. The person to whom something "seems" or "appears" is in the dative case.

  • Videor diceptus esse - I seem to have been deceived

  • Videor mihi esse pauper - I seem to you to be poor

  • Galli hostes strenui esse videbantur (Cic.) - It seemed to the Gauls that the enemies were brave.

  • Tu mihi videris Epicharmi sententiam sequi - It seems to me that you are following the saying of Epicharmus

Impersonal construction

The verb videor is used impersonally as a third personal singular, in the following cases:

  • When videor is accompained by a neuter adjective as iustum, utile, bonum, arduum, idoneum, etc. (the subject is the whole infinitive proposition)

    • Turpe mihi ipsi videbatur in eam urbem me audere reverti, ex qua Brutus cederet (Cic.) - Even though it is a shame that I should venture to return to a city from which Brutus was retiring.

    • Tibi videtur utile esse nos colloqui (Cic.) - You think it useful for us to confer

  • when it signifies "to seem fit":

    • Visum est mihi de senectute aliquid ad te conscribere (Cic.) - It seemed best to me to write for you something on the subject of old age.

    • Ad haec quae visum est Caesar respondit (Caes.) - To this Caesar replied what seemed fit.

  • When videor is accompained by an impersonal verb such as: piget («to afflict, grieve»), pudet («to be ashemed»), paenitet («to repent»), taedet («to be tired»), miseret («to feel pity»), refert, etc:

    • Videtur te paenitere peccati tui - It seems that you're apologetic about your sins

    • Ipsius certe ducis hoc referre videtur Ut qui fortis erit sit felicissimus idem, (Iuv.) - Here it is said to be for the interest of the commander himself that he who is brave should also be most fortunate.

  • The circumlocution by means of fore/futurum esse ut (neg. ut non) plus the subjunctive, is necessary when the verb has no supine or participle future active

    • Mihi videtur fore ut te poeniteat - I think you will repent.
