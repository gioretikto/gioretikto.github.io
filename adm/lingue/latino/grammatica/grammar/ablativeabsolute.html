Ablative Absolute

The Ablative absolute is a construction made up of a noun or pronoun and a participle agreeing with it in the ablative case and it is used to introduce a subordinate proposition.

The ablative absolute can fulfill different functions: temporal, causal, conditional and concessive clauses.

The term absolute means independent, from the verb absolvo "untie, loosen", because the participle and noun can both stand apart from the main the sentence, though in logical and temporal connection with the main proposition. The present participle is used to describe circumstances, occuring at the same time as that of the verb in the main clause, whilst the perfect participle refers to a time before. The presenet participle in an ablative absolute, usually ends in -e, not in -i:

Dido eădem labente die convivia quaerit. (Verg.) - while the sun sets, Dido come back to the same banquets.

Sole oriente, fugiunt tenebrae- while the sun rising, darkness flies away

  • «Volsci, traditis armis, sub iugum missi sunt.» «The Volsci, delivered the weapons (= after the arms were delivered, having been delivered the weapons), were passed under the yoke.»
    Note: trado è un transitivo attivo.

  • «Caesar ad solis occasum naves solvit et, orta luce, Britanniam conspexit.» «Caesar at sunset set sail and at daybreak saw the Britannia»
    Nota: orior è un deponente intransitivo.

Note that the subject of the ablative absolute is generally different form subject of the main clause.

Causal:

  • Cupientibus omnibus, Caesar iussit proelium committere (Ces.) - Because everybody desired it, Caesar ordered to attack

  • Nullo adversante regnum obtinuit - since no one opposed him, he secured the throne

  • Galli consilium ceperunt ex oppido profugere, hortante Vercingetorīge. (Caes.) -

  • Dionysius, cultros metuens tonsorios, candenti carbone sibi adurebat capillum. (Cic.) -

  • Nobilium vita victuque mutato mores mutari civitatum puto - the costumes of the cities change after the standard of living of the nobility has changed

  • Rex, portis clausis, exclusus est. (Eutr.) - The king, since the doors had been closed, remained outside

Temporal:

  • Tarquinio Superbo regnante, Pythagoras in Italiam venit (Cic.) - in the reign of Tarquin, Pythagoras came to Italy

  • Urbe capta, Aeneas fugit - the city having been captured, Aeneas fled

  • Milites, hostibus victis, redierunt - The soldiers having won the enemy, returned.

  • Galli consilium ceperunt ex oppido profugere, hortante Vercingetorige - The Gauls took the decision to escape from the city, on the exhortation of Vercingetorix

  • Scelerati, conscientia obstrepente, condormire non possunt. - The wicked cannot sleep soundly because of the clamours of conscience

Conditional:

  • omnēs virtutes iacent, voluptate dominante - All virtues lie prostate, if pleaseure is master

  • Hanc adepti victoriam, in perpetuum se fore victores confidebant. (Caes.) - having won this battle , they thought to win forever

  • Amigo rogante, nonne id facies? - if a friend ask you a favour, Wouldn't you do it?

Concessive:

  • In urbem, defendente nullo, tamen armatis ascendere difficile erat - Although nobody defended it, it was difficult for soldiers to climb into the city

The ablative absolute with the present participle can be made with all verbs; Perfect participles can be used only with transtive active verbs or intransitive deponent verbs (like morior "I die", nascor "I born", proficiscor, "I set out"). When the ablative absolute cannot be used, latin resorts to the cum + subjunctive, construction.

Ablative Absolute without participle

Sometimes the participle can be replaced by a noun, pronoun or adjective in the ablative case with the function of predicate. This construction is known as nominal ablative absolute. This construction can be translated as if there was an unexpressed present participle of the verb sum (which does not existed):

  • Caesar duce- with Caesar general - being Caesar the general

  • Cleopatra regina, incolae felices erant- being Cleopatra queen

  • Cicerone consule- being Cicero consul

  • me puero- as a was a child

  • diis invitis- against the will of the gods

  • te adiutore- with your help

  • caelo sereno- being the sky cloudless

  • natura duce, under the guide of nature

  • mortuo Caesar- after the death of Caesar

  • vivo Caesare res publica salva erat, Caesar being alive the state was safe

Two other examples are:

  • Hostibus victis, civibus salvis, re placida, pacibus perfectis, bello exstincto, re bene gesta, integro exercitu et praesidiis... (Plaut.) -
    Now our foes are beaten and our citizens safe, calm the political situation, peace assured, and the war brought to a triumphant conclusion, with our army and garrisons intact

  • Tarquino rege, Pythagoras in Italiam venit - Tarquin being king Pythagoras came to Italy

Construction using only the participle

There is a peculiar construction made up only by a perfect participle, in the neuter ablative singular case, without subject.

  • Cognito vivere Ptolomaeum, navigandi in Aegyptum omissum consilium est - Learned that Ptolemy was alive, the idea of navigating to Egypt was abandoned

  • Auspicato (Plaut.) - taken the auspices
