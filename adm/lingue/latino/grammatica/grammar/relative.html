Relative Pronouns

Relative pronouns refer to a noun in a preceding clause, and relate it to a secondary clause (the relative clause). Relative pronouns can be either definite or indefinite.

The definite relative pronoun is qui, quae, quod, «who» or «which» is also used as adjective, its declension is as follows:

CASE SINGULAR PLURAL
Masculine Feminine Neuter Masculine Feminine Neuter
NOMINATIVE quī quae quŏd quī quae quae
GENITIVE cūiŭs cūiŭs cūiŭs quōrum quārum quōrum
DATIVE cuī cuī cuī quĭbus quĭbus quĭbus
ACCUSATIVE quĕm quăm quŏd quōs quās quae
ABLATIVE quō quā quō quĭbus quĭbus quĭbus

The relative pronoun agrees with the antecedent in gender and number, but it takes its case from its construction in its own relative clause. Parse the following sentences:

  • Gallia est omnis divisa in partes tres, quarum unam incolunt Belgae, aliam Aquitani, tertiam Celtae (Cic.) - «The whole of Gaul is divided into three parts, of which the Belgians inhabit one, the Aquitanians another and the third by Celts»

  • Stultum facit fortuna quem vult perdere - «Fortune makes stupid one whom she wants to destroy»

  • Quod dicis verum sane est - «What you say is quite true»

The ablative combined with the preposition cum the forms which are preferably used: quocum, quacum, quibuscum.

The relative clause can be anticipated in a sentence and placed before the main clause to add emphasis:

  • Quos ferro trucidari oportebat, eos nondum voce vulnero. (Cic.) - «Those who should have been butchered with the sword I do not yet wound them with my voice»

  • Qua mollitia sum animi, numquam illius lacrimis restitissem. (Cic.) - «Given my sensitivity, I would never have resisted his tears»

Indefinite relative pronouns

The indefinite relative pronouns are: quicumque, quaecumque, quodcumque «whoever», «whichever» and quisquis (masc. and fem.) quidquid or quicquid (neut.) «every one», «everything».

Quicumque is formed by the relative qui, quae, quod (the declinable part) and by the invariable suffix -cumque. It is also used as adjective; it declines as follows:

Masculine Feminine Neuter
NOMINATIVE quicumque quaecumque quodcumque
GENITIVE cuiuscumque cuiuscumque cuiuscumque
DATIVE cuicumque cuicumque cuicumque
ACCUSATIVE quemcumque quamcumque quodcumque
ABLATIVE quocumque quacumque quocumque
CASE SINGULAR
Masculine Feminine Neuter
NOMINATIVE quicumque quaecumque quaecumque
GENITIVE quorumcumque quarumcumque quorumcumque
DATIVE quibuscumque quibuscumque quibuscumque
ACCUSATIVE quoscumque quascumque quaecumque
ABLATIVE quibuscumque quibuscumque quibuscumque

Quisquis, «whoever», «whichever», has formed from the doubling of its ancient form quis; both elements are inflected. It is defective and only used as a pronoun and adjective in the forms:

  • quisquis, quaequae (nom. m. and f.)

  • quidquid (nom. and acc. n.)

Only as an adjective in the form:

  • quoquo (masculine ablative singular), in expressions like: quoquo modo, «in whatever way», «however»

for all the other forms, quicumque is used.

Constructions made using an indefinite pronoun, generally use the indicative tense:

  • Fugi, quicumque es! - «whoever your are run away!».

  • Quicumque is est, ei me profiteor inimicum (Cic.) - «whoever he is, I declare myself his enemy».

  • Placĕat homini, quidquid Deo placuit - «Whatever is God's pleasure should be man's pleasure».
