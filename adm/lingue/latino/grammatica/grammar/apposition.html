Apposition

Apposition (from appōnō, "I place near") is a grammatical construction in which two elements, normally a substantive or personal pronoun, are placed side by side, with one element, the apposition, serving to explain or describe the other e.g. Plato the philosopher or The philosopher Plato, The consul cicero; The two elements are said to be in apposition. A substantive in apposition always agrees in case with the substantive of which it is spoken.

The appelative title rex and the geographical names, frequently precede the proper name with which they are in apposition:

  • Philippus, rex Macedonum, decessit (Liv.) - Philip, King of Macedonia, died

  • Flumen Tiberis.

  • Insula Sicilia

  • Provincia Africa

  • Urbs Rōma, Urbs Trōia, Urbis Athenarum, "Of hte city Athens"

The agreement is also in gender and number when the apposition is a mobile noun, i.e. a noun which has a masculine and feminine form such as : inventor, inventrix, magister, magistra;

  • Athenae fuerunt invectrices omnium artium -

    The apposition of a proper name is placed after the name:

    • Athenae inventrices omnium artium fuerunt - Athens was the inventor of all arts.

    • Histortia est magistra vita - history is the teacher of life

    • Cicero ōrator, - «Cicero the orator;

    • Homerus poeta, «Homer the poet»;

    • Cato cēnsor, «Cato the censor»

    • Dionysiustyrannus Syracusanorum, “ Dionysius tyrant of the Syracusians

    A nouns may be in apposition to two or more nouns, and in such case is usually put in the plural:

    • M. Antonius, C. Cassius, tribuni plebis (Caes.) - M. Antonius, C. Cassius, tribues of the people

    The noun imperator with the meaning «commander», «general» is placed after the proper name it refers to; Instead when it signifies «imperator» is placed before it, e.g.:

    • Imperator Augustus - The Emperor Augustus

    • Scipio imperator -The commander scipio
