Predicative and attributive function of Adjectives

Adjectives and substantives can function attributively or predicatively; In both cases they agree with a noun or pronoun in the sentence. An attribute is an attachment to a noun in a sentence which would be already complete without it; e.g. in Bonus puer deambulat, "The good boy walks", puer deambulat makes a complete sense without the attribute bonus. An ajdective is used predicatively and it is said to be a complement, when itsels make up (complet) the meaning of the predicate e.g Bonus puer laetus deambulat; laetus is an adjective used as a complement. In English predicative adjectives occur after a copular verb: The pupil is smart.

«Puer laetus deambulat»
The happy boy walks around

«Puer deambulat laetus»
The boy walks happily around

We have already seen how to use ajdectives as Attributes, we shall focus on how to use them predicatively. This occurs particularly with ajedtives denoting an emotional state, as:

  • alacer («cheerfully»)

  • laetus («gladly»)

  • tristis («sadly»)

  • maestus («sorrowfully»)

  • nescius, ignarus («not knowing»)

  • tacitus («in silence», «without uttering a word»)

  • libens («willingly»)

  • invitus («unwillingly», «grudgingly»)

as you can notice, predicative adjectives are rendered in English adverbially.

Parse the following examples:

  • Socrates venenum laetus hausit (Cic.) - Socrates drank the poison gladly.

  • Sapiens nihil facit invitus, nihili dolens, nihil coactus (Cic.) - A wise man does nothing against his will, nothing grudgingly, nothing under coercion.

Adjectives are used predicatively also when indicating the order in temporal and spatial locutions as primus, princeps, secundus... («The first», «The second», ...), ultimus («The last»), superior («above»), inferior («underneath»), summus, medius, imus, etc.

  • Hannibal princeps in proelium ibat, ultimus conserto proelio excedēbat, (Livy) - Hannibal used to be the first to go to battle, and after the engagement the last to quit the field

  • Livius, qui primus fabulam docuit (Cic.) - Livius was the first to put on a play

  • in summo monte, «On top of the hill»

  • in medio colle, «in the middle hill», «half way up the hill»;

  • primo vere, «Early in the spring»;

  • media hieme, «In the middle of winter»;

  • prima luce, «Early in the morning»

These adjectives are used also attributively, usually after the noun they modify, when indicating its quality.

  • Manus extrema non accessit operibus eius (Cic.) - He did not. thoroughly finish his works (his finishing stroke was missing)

  • Amicus summus meus et popularis, Geta. (Ter.) - My great friend and countryman Geta

Other adjectives that complete the meaning of the verb as: solus, unus, omnis, singuli, praesens, absens, sciens, libens, etc., are used predicatively:

  • Homerus solus appello poeta mereo - Homer alone deserved to be called a poet
