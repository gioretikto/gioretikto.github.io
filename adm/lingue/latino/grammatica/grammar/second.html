Latin second declension - Declinatio secunda

Latin nouns of the second declension are prevantly masculine and neuter. Nouns of this declension are distinguished by the genitive singular ending -i, like:

lupus, lupi, "wolf"

servus, servi "slave"

In the nominative singular, masculine and feminine nouns end in -us, while neuter nouns end in -um. There are in addition some masculine nouns ending in the nominative singular in -er and in -ir.

The Stem of nouns of the second declension ends in o; the stem is found by dropping the ending from the full genitive plural form: lupo-rum. In the second declension, case endings to add to the stem, fall in three categories, depending on the ending of the nominative singular;

Masculine and Feminine nouns in -us

The masculine and feminine nouns with ending in the nominative singular in -us are declined as lupus, lupi, m., "wolf":

Case Singular Plural
Nominative lup-ŭs lup-i
Genitive lup-i lup-ōrum
Dative lup-o lup-īs
Accusative lup-ŭm lup-ōs
Vocative lup-e lup-ī
Ablative lup-ō lup-īs

Also in this declension we can observe some similar forms:

  • lupi can be singular genitive, plural nominative, or plural vocative;

  • lupo can be singular dative or ablative;

  • lupis can be plural dative or ablative.

This paradygm is followed by other names in -us like agnus, agni, m, "lamb", popŭlus, popŭli, m. "The People", porcus, porci, m., "'pig". For example the declension of another noun, cervus, cervi, m, "deer", is the following

Case Singular Plural
Nominative cerv-ŭs cerv-i
Genitive cerv-i cerv-ōrum
Dative cerv-o cerv-īs
Accusative cerv-ŭm cerv-ōs
Vocative cerv-e cerv-ī
Ablative cerv-ō cerv-īs

Masculine nouns in -er and -ir

The nouns ending in -er are all masculine; some names keep the stem-vowel e throughout the declension: they are declined as puer, pueri, m., "the boy", vesper, vespĕri, m., «the evening»; others instead present the e only in the singular nominative and vocative: these are declined as ager, agri, m., "the field"; liber, libri, m., "the book", aper, aperi, m., "the wild boar"; magister, magitri, m., "the master, teacher"; arbĭter, arbĭtri, m., «the arbiter». To know whether the e is retained in the stem it is necessary to learn both nominative and genitive singular.

Case Singular Plural
Nominative puer pueri
Genitive pueri puerōrum
Dative puero puerīs
Accusative puerŭm puerōs
Vocative puer puerī
Ablative puerō puerīs

ager, agri, sm - II decl.

Caso Singolare Plurale

Genitive agri agerōrum
Dative agro agerīs

Vocative agĕr agrī
Ablative agrō agrīs

As can be seen, the names in -er have the singular vocative equal to the nominative; the other case-endings are the same as those of the -us names. Other names in -er of the second declension are: socer, socĕri, m., «The father-in-law»; gener, genĕri, m., "the son-in-law";

Also the names in -ir are all masculine; they are declined as vĭr, virī, m., "the man":

Case Singular Plural
Nominative vĭr virī
Genitive viri virōrŭm
Dative viro virīs
Accusative virŭm virōs
Vocative vĭr virī
Ablative virō virīs

Also the names in -ir have the singular vocative equal to the nominative, and in the other cases they have the endings equal to those of the names in -us. The names in -ir are vir and its compounds, such as triumvir, triumviĭri, m., "Triumvir"; decemvir,decemvĭri, m., «decemvir» etc. These nouns in the genitive plural, along with the forms triumvirorum and decemvirorum, present also the form triumvĭrum and decemvĭrum.

Neuter nouns in -um

Neuter nouns ending in um, are declined as templum, templi, n., "The temple":

Case Singular Plural
Nominative templŭm templă
Genitive templī templōrum
Dativo templō templīs
Accusative templŭm templă
Vocativo templŭm templă
Ablative templō templīs

As can be noticed, neuter nouns have nominative, genetive and accusative equal in um singular and in -a, plural.

There are also some neuter nouns ending in -us, belonging to the second declension as (es.: pelăgus, pelăgi, n., "The Sea", virus, viri, n, "poison", vulgus, vulgi, n., "The people". These nouns are declined only in the singular form, whilst in the plural they are replaced but their synonyms (mare, venenum, populus).
