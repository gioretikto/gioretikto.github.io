Agreement of the Relative Pronoun

The relative pronoun agrees with the noun it refers to in the main clause, in gender and number, but it takes its case from its use in its own clause.

  • Accepi ab Aristocrito tris epistulas, quas ego lacrimis prope delevi (Cic.) - I have received three letters from Aristocritus, and almost blotted them out in tears.

  • Pompeius mare transiit cum omnibus militibus, quos secum habuit (Cic.) - Pompeius crossed the sea with all the soldiers that he had with him

When a relative pronoun refers to more than one substantive, it is put in the plural number.

  • Pater et frater tuus, qui nobiscum fuerunt, mortui sunt

  • Mater et filia, quae nobiscum fuerunt, mortui sunt

When the relative applies to more substantives of different genders, the masculine is preferred to the feminine and the feminine to the neuter.

  • Pater et mater, qui nunc sunt mortui, erant valde pii - My father and mother, who are now dead, were very pious

  • Mulier et mancipium, quae erant ad sinistram tuam, fugerunt - The woman and slave who were at your left, fled

When it comes to names of inanimate things the relative pronoun appears in the neuter gender;

  • Virtus et vitium, quae (n.) sunt contraria - (Virtus is fem. and vitium neuter.)

  • Divitiae et honores, quae (n.) prima mortales putant - Riches and honours, which mortals regard as the principal things. - (Divitiae is fem. and honores masc.)

In the case of mixed nouns of animate and inanimate things the relative is put usually in the gender of the animate.

  • Provinciae Galliae M. Fonteius praefuit, quae constat ex iis generibus hominum et civitatum, qui, ut vetera mittam, partim nostra memoria bella cum populo Romano ... (Cic.) - Marcus Fonteius was in charge of the province of Gaul, which comprises a type of men and communities which have within our own memory waged long and bitter wars with the people of Rome.

When the relative pronoun refers to a whole proposition it is neuter.

  • Sapientes contenti sunt rebus suis, quod est summum bonum - Wise men are content with their lot (own things), which is the greatest blessing.

whene the relative pronouns refers to a geographical proper name joined to its apposition, as urbs, oppidum, insula, mons etc., it does not agree with the proper name but with the apposition:

  • Mons Cebenna, qui (not quae) Arvernos ab Helviis discludit, altissima nive iter impediebat (Caes.) - Mont Cevennes, which separetes the Arvenui from the Helvii obstructed the road with deep snow

  • Oppidum Corinthus, quod dirutum est ...

With the noun flumen joined to a proper name, it may agree either with the neuter flumen or with the latter:

  • «Flumen Rhenus, qui fluit»; or «Flumen Rhenus, quod fluit»

  • Flumen est Arar, quod per fines Aeduorum et Sequanorum in Rhodanum influit (Caes.) - The Arar is a river, which flows through the territory of the Aeduans and the Sequanians into the Rhone.

  • Helvetii continentur undique natura loci; ex una parte, latissimo atque altissimo flumine Rheno, qui (or. quod) dividit Helvetium agrum a Germanis (Caes.) - The Helvetii are contained on every side by the nature of the place; out of one part, by the widest and deepest river Rheno, which divides the Helvetian land from the Germani.

Attraction of The Relative Pronoun

When the relative clause is no strictly necessary to express the meaning of the main clause but only the function of completing its sense, the relative pronoun agrees with the predicate in the relative and not with the antecedent. This phenomenon is known as attraction of the relative pronoun into the relative clause.

  • Omnes Belgae, quae (and not qui) tertia est Galliae pars, contra populum romanum coniuraverunt - All the Belgians, who are (which is) a third part of Gaul, were conspiring against the Roman people.

  • Thebae ipsae, quod (not quae) Beotiae caput est in magno motu erant (Liv.42.44.2),«Thebes itself, the capital of Boeotia, was also in a great uproar»

  • Sic est animi luce splendoremque fugientis iustam gloriam qui (not quae) est fructus verae honestissimus, repudiare (Cic.) - So it is a sign of a mind that actually avoids light and splendour to repudiate glory rightfully acquired, which is the most honourable fruit of true virtue.

When the relative clause is necessay to complete the meaning of the main clause, the relative pronoun agrees to the antecedent:

  • Video magnam partem eorum, qui in schola de pudore et continentia praecipiant argutissime, eosdem in omnium libidinum cupiditatibus vivere. (Nep. Fragm. Epist ad Cic.) - I observe a great proportion of those, who in the schools give most eloquent precepts respecting modest behaviour and temperature, live at the same time in the enjoyment of every kind of sensuality.
