Attribute

Adjectives can be used attributively or predicatively. In both cases they agree with the noun they modify, in gender, number, and case.

An attribute (from the Latin attribuo, "I assign, associate") simply qualifies the noun without the intervention of a verb. Most English adjectives, premodify the noun they modify, as in wise man, good dog, small house. In latin the orde is less strict;

  • Vir fortis - A brave man

  • Procera arbor - A tall tree

  • Bonus discipulus - A good pupil

  • Poëta clarus - The famous poet

Attribute adjectives that refere to more than a noun, generally agree to the noun next to it or with the most important. The attribute may be repeated, when the writer wishes to emphasize each noun:

«Omens agri et maria» - All lands and seas

«Agri et maria omnia» - The lands and all the seas

  • Hominis ūtilitātī agrī omnēs et maria pārent (Cic.) - All lands and seas serve the needs of mankind

  • Proelium uno animo et voce una poscunt (Liv.) - The army demanded to fight with one accord and one voice

Predicate adjectives

. Predicative adjectives, function as subject complements or object complementes. The verb sum (to be) is the most common copular verb in both English and Latin. Other Latin copular verbs are those which express to becomem, to be made, to appear.

In latin because of free word order, there can be ambiguity between the attributive and predicative function of an adjective, and has to be understood in context, e.g.:

Milites strenuus pugnat

Attributive function: “The brave soldiers fights.”

Funzione predicativa: “Il soldier fights bravely.”
