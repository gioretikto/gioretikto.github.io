Gerundive

In Latin, along with the gerund (a verbal noun) there is also the gerundive a verbal adjective. It is passive in voice and future in tense, for this reason is also known as a future passive particle. There is no English equivalent. The gerundive is declined as a 1st/2nd declension adjective, and it is formed by adding the endings -ndus, a, um to the stem of the verb. The ending of the fourth conjugations is in endus:

Conjugation Gerundive Meaning
I amandus, amanda, amandum ought to be loved, must be loved
II monedus, monenda, monendum ought to be advised, must be advised
III legendus, legenda, legendum ought to be read, must be read
IV audiendus, audienda, audiedum ought to be heard, must be heard

The gerundive can be used:

  • As an adjective to express necessity or obbligation

  • In place of the gerund when the latter would take a direct object which is not a neuter pronoun or adjective.

  • To express purpose modifying the direct object of verbs without the intermediary of the preposition ad (as occurs with gerund)

Use of the gerundive as adjective to express necessity or obbligation

As any adjective the gerundive agrees with the noun that it modifies in gender, number and case. The Gerundive can have two functions:

  • Attributive adjective: Liber legendus. (A book worth reading).

  • Predicative adjective (passive periphrastic construction): Liber legendus est (A book that must be read)

Attributive use is largely confined to verbs expressing approval/disapproval, and necessity:

  • Puer amandus - A lovable boy. (A boy to be loved)

  • Vulgus interdum non probandum oratorem probat (Cic.) - Sometime the peolple approve orators who shouldn't be approved.

When the gerundive refers to the genitive of personal pronouns, mei, tui, sui, nostri, vestri, it can end in i, even if referred to feminine or plural nouns:

Clopatra sui conservandi causa confugit (Cic.)- Cleopatra fled to safety

A recurrent expression well known to students of science, used when prooving a statement is QED, quod erat demostradum. This sentence is placed at the end of a mathematical argument to say that the required proof has been given. Demostradum is the gerundive neuter nominative singular of the the verb demostro, meaning "to proof", "to point out, show".

agenda - Derives from the gerundive of the verb agére, “to lead”, “to do”: litterarly means “things to be done”.

When used as predicative adjective, it expresses the purpose of the action; it acts as a predicative of the object or subject with verbs which signify "to give", "to send", "to entrust", "to grant", "to undertake", "to leave", "to take care" (do, trado, mitto, defero, committo , permitto, relinquo, sumo, suscipio, curo):

  • Diomedon Epaminondam pecunia corrumpendum susceperat. -"Diomedonte had assumed the task of bribing Epaminondas with money."

  • Caesar reliquum exercitum legatis in Menapois ducendum dedit - Caesar entrusted the remaining army to the lieutenants in order to be conducted in the territory of the Menapians

  • «Hunc Datames vinctum ad regem ducendum tradit Mithridati» - "Datame gives him to Mithridate chained up, in order to bring him to the king."

Other examples are:

  • Mater dedit magistro liberos educandos - The mother brought her sons to the teacher in order to be thaught

  • Patria semper servanda - People must defend their homeland.

Gerundive in the passive periphrastic

The gerundive is used as a predicative adjective with the verb sum, to express obligation and necessity and it is usually translated as "must be .." or "is to be". This is know in Latin grammar as periphrastic conjugation:

  • Monendi et obiurgandi amici saepe sunt. (Cic.) - Friends frequenlty must be advised and rebuked.

  • Carthago delenda est - Carthage must be destroyed

    delenda, is the feminine singular gerundive form of the verb dēlēre ("to destroy").

  • Poetae honorandi sunt - Poets ought to be honored.

  • Pacta sunt servanda - Agreements must be kept

  • De gustibus non est disputandum - "About tastes, it should not be disputed/discussed"

    This phrase is commonly rendered in English as "There is no accounting for taste(s).". The implication is that everyone's personal preferences are merely subjective opinions that cannot be right or wrong.

Sometime, the passive periphrastic is used impersonally i.e. no subject is specified with association to the action. In order to indicate who is the agent of the action, the dative of agent is used. In this construction, the gerundive is in the neuter singular (ending in -dum) and the verb sum in the third singular person; The verb can be either intransitive or transitive. Instransitive verbs cannot be made passive in the usual way, but we already saw, that they can have impersonal passive. In English it is usually better to translate with an active verb, making the dative agent, the subject:

  • Omnibus moriendum est - We all must die

  • Legendum est - People have to read / One has to read

  • Eundum est mihi - I must go

  • Nobis vincendum est: - We must win

Other example:

  • Tibi diu deliberandum concoquendumque est - "You must think long and meditate."

  • "Militibus simul et de navibus desiliendum et in fluctibus consistendum et cum hostibus erat pugnandum." - "The soldiers had to disembark from the ships and at the same time, take a position among the waves and fight against the enemies."

  • Nobis de proelio cogitandum est - "We must reflect on the war."

Some of the intransitive verbs can take themselves the dative. In this situation, the prepositions a/ab are used to avoid ambiguity. For example the sentence:

Tibi mihi oboediendum est

could mean: You must obey me or: I must obey you.

To remove any ambiguity, the preposition A is used:

A te mihi obediendum est - You must obey me

another example:

legibus a te parendum est - You must obey the laws

When there is no ambiguity, two datives can be present in the same sentence:

Servis oboediendum est domino (Cic.) - The slaves must obey the master

Gerund and gerundive phrase construction

Instead of a gerund accompained by a direct object in the accusative, there are cases in which latin authors employ a gerundive construction. In this construction the gerundive is active and agrees with the noun in gender, case and number:

Gerund: Cupiditas legendi epistulam

Gerundive: Cupiditas legendae epistulae

Gerund: Cupiditas bidendi aquam

Gerundive: Cupiditas bibendae aquae

Which can be both translated as "the desire of reading/drinking the letter/water". Another example is:

Gerund: Vir scientissimus regendi rem publicam (Cic.)

Gerundive: Vir scientissimus regendae rei publicae

They both mean: A very capable state man

Another example, is:

Construction with gerund:

  • Hasdrubal pugnantes obeundo pericula sustinuit (Liv.)

    gerund in the ablative case + perciula, direct object

Construction with gerundive:

  • Hasdrubal pugnantes obeundis periculis sustinuit

    gerundive in the ablative that agrees with the noun periculis

Which can be both traslated as:

Hasdrubal encouraged the fighters, taking part himself to the battle. Other example is the sentence "The desire of raeding many books", which can be rendered as:

Construction with gerund:

  • cupiditas legendi multos libros

    gerund in the genetive case + libros direct object

Construction with gerundive:

  • cupiditas legendorum multorum librorum.

    gerundive in the genitive case that agrees with the noun librorum.

The gerundive construction always replaces a gerund in the dative case, or accusative and ablative dependent on a preposition. These Constructions generally express purposes:

Dative:

  • Animis natum inventumque poema iuvandis. (Hor.) - Poetry was devised to enjoy the spirit.

  • Utilis temperandis animis - Useful for governing passions.

  • Praefectus bello gerendo - Set out to conduct war

Accusative:

  • Vivis ad confirmandam audaciam. (Cic.) - You live to strengthen you boldness.

  • Ad pacem petendam - to seek peace

  • Difficultas ad consilium capiedum - The difficulty of taking a decision

  • Ad colendo agros - for cultivating the fields

Ablative:

  • In beneficiis tribuendis (Sen.) - In granting benefits

  • Multa sunt dicta De contemnenda gloria - Many things have been said on the subject of despising glory

The genitive of Gerundive is often used to express purpose when used in conjuction with ad, causa or gratia:

  • Haec faciunt recuperandorum militum causa (Ces.) - They are doing these things to recover the soldiers

  • Praedandae Galliae causa - For the purpose of plundering Gallia

  • Hannibal ad Eumenem vincendum tale consilium cepit - Hannibal formed a plan of such a sort to conquer Eumenes

  • Vītandae suspīcionis causā (Cat.) - In order to avoid suspicion

  • Simulandī grātiā (Iug.) - In order to deceive

  • Exercendae memōriae grātiā (Cat.) - For the sake of training the memory
