Fractions

The word fraction means “part of a whole,” from the Latin fractio, meaning “to break into pieces. Fractions are expressed in Latin in a similar way to English, using cardinals and ordinals. Latin expresses fractions using the substantive pars (part), in the denominator:

If the numerator is "I", "one", it is omitted:

  • 1/3 = tertia or tertia pars, «a third»;

  • 1/4 = quarta or quarta pars, «a fourth»;

  • 1/5 = quinta pars or quinta pars, «a fifth»

  • 1/2 = «half» is espressed as dimidia (pars); added to integers, is rendered with the noun semis, semīssis, m.

    • 3 + 1/2 = tres et semisses

    • duo pedes et semisses altus = deep two feets and an half

    If the numerator is greater than "one", pars must be expressed with the cardinal number and the denominator is expressed with the ordinal:

    3/5 = tres quintae (partes), "three-fifths";

    2/7 = duae septimae (partes), «two sevenths».

    If the denominator is superior to the numerator by only one unit, then it is not be expressed:

    2/3 = duae partes, "two third"

    3/4 = tres partes, "three quarters";

    multae novem partes (Nep.), "nine tenths of the fine".

    Latin has also some special words for some fractions:

    dimidia (pars) f. = half

    semis, semissi, m. = half

    triens, trientis, m. = one third

    quadrans, quadrantis, m. = one quarter

    sextans, sextantis, m. = one sixth
