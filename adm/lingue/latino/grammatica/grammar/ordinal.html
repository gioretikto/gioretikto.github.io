Ordinals

The ordinal numbers answer the question quotus?, "in which order" denoting the order of succession in which any substantive is mentioned; as primus, "the first", secundus, "the second", etc.

All ordinals, terminate in us and are declined as first/second declension adjectives (like bonus) exceptfor prior and alter.

Ordinal numerals in Latin are generally used as in English:

  • Numa secundus rex Romae fuit - «Numa was the second king of Rome»

  • Septimus Rex a Romulo fuit Tarquinius superbus - «The seventh king after Romulus was Tarquinius superbus»

  • Aeneidos liber quartus, «the fourth book of the Aeneid».

Unlike English, however, with names that indicate "year", and "hour", Latin uses ordinals instead of cardinals.

Quot Annus?, «What year?» - Anno quadringentesimo quadragesimo quarto, "In the year four hundred and forty-four".

In the ordinal numbers from 13th to 17th the smaller number may be placed last and the conjunction et inserted or not:

13th = tertius decimus or decimus et tertius

14th = quartus decimus, or decimus et quartius

In the other ordinals one has the same disposition as the cardinals. However in 21st, 31st, 41st etc. it is preferred unus to primus; in 22nd, 32nd, 42nd, alter is preferred to secundus;

21st = unus et vicesimus or vicesimus unus

22nd = alter et vicesimus or vicesimus alter

31st = unus et tricesimus or tricesimus unus

32nd = alter et tricesimus or tricesimus alter

The ordinal numerals prior and alter are used instead of primus, and secundus when they refer to two substantives:

  • Democritus, et Heraclitus erant philosophi, quorum prior semper ridebat, alter semper flebat - «Democritus, and Heraclitus were filosi, of which the former always laughed the second always cried»

  • Tomi primi pars altera, «second part of the first volume».

  • Cornelia duos habuit filios, priorem Tiberium, alterum Gaium (Nep.) «Cornelia had two sons the first Tiberius and the second Gaius»

But if we refer to more than two people, primus and secundus are used:

  • Primus inter pares - «The first among his equals»

  • Primus non sum nec imus - «I am neither first nor last»
