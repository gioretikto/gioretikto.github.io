The Pronunciation of Latin

The Latin language, spoken in the first millennium BC in a circumscribed area of Latium, spread throughout the ancient world with the expansion of the dominion of Rome. The idiom of the conquerors became a universal language, useful for communicating across the empire, as happens today with English. Latin literature began in the middle of the third century with the adaptation of Greek plays with drama the most predominat literary genre at Rome. We call the language of thi period Early Latin. Twenty plays of Plautus and six of Terence represent a mavellous take on the latin of this time. Terence developed a more literary form of Latin in anticipation of what we call Classical latin the language of poetry and formal prose writing used if the first century BC and most recognizable in the writings of Cicero and his contemporaries.

The slow and constant evolution in the centuries of this language, growing contininuosly apart from the formal Latin of literature and administration resulted into the Vulgar Latin (Latin of the vulgus or common people) which ultimately turned about A.D. 800 into the Romance languages: french, spanish, romanian, portugues, and italian.

However, even with the rise of these vernacular languages, Latin continued during Middle ages and later to be used as as Europe's lingua franca and remained the predominant language for cultural, intellectual, religious and scientific affairs. During middle ages there was a trendency for speakers across Europe to pronounce Latin as they would their own language;

In 1528, Erasmus of Rotterdam published his dialogue De Recta Latini Graecique Sermonis Pronuntiatione ("On the Correct Pronunciation of the Latin and Greek Language"), discussing mutually unintelligible national pronunciations of Latin strongly influenced by the vernacular languages, that often led to confusion. The following is an hilarious anedocted from Erasmus' dialogue, describing the Emperor Maximial I, listenining to some speeches of welcome from orators of various countries:

"One of the speakers was a Frenchman and gave his speech in one discret Latin, but with a French accent so strong tha some Italian scholars present thought he was speaking in French; such was the laughter that the Frenchman broke off his speech in embarrrassment, and began to search an orator answering him. But even more sonourus ridicule greeted the German accent of the next speaker; Then the speaker from Denmark whoreceived an answer from a Zelandese: but it could be said that neither one nor the other had spoken Latin".

Erasmus tried to reestablish Classical pronunciation and in his "De pronuntiatione," recognizes the Italian pronunciation to be closest to the original, though he had the conviction that "c" and "g" letters must once for "hard" guttural sounds notining that Germans retained the hard c, since they say Kaiser for Caesar. Erasmus' reform was met by storng opposition and in England, France and Germany. A new reformed pronunciation of Classical Latin formulated by various Cambridge and Oxford scholars emerged in the 1870s and became accepted by the beginning of the twentieth century - it is now known as Classic or Restitūta pronunciation, «restored», or also as scientific pronunciation. Among Roman Catholic circles and Italy - Latin is still pronounced accordinig to the pre-Erasmian system, know as ecclesiatical pronounciation.

Diphthongs - Dipthonga

Two vowels united can produce a dipthong, pronounced together in one syllable. There are five diphtongs namely: ae, oe, au, eu, which are in common use and ei, ui, yi, which occur only a few words.

  • ae as i in alive; Caesar

  • au, as ow in bow; aurum

  • ei, as ey in Hey

  • oe, line oi in boil;

  • eu in two syllable, as ey in they and u in put;

  • ui, as wee, or French Oui.

Consonants

Consonanst are distinguished into mutes, semivowels, and liquids. The mutes cannot be sounded at all without the aid of a vowel. They are b, p, d, t, k, q and c and g hard. The semivowels can be imperfeclty sounded without a vowel as: f, h, j, l, m, r, s, v, c and g soft and the double consonants x and z. Four of these l, m, n, r are termed liquids (liquidae) for their smooth and flowing sound.

Besides these there are the guttural and dental aspirats h and s;

The consonants are also divided according to the principal organs by which they are pronounced, into:

  • Labials(lip-sounds): b, p (ph), f, v, m;

  • Dentals(tooth-sounds): d, t, (th), l, n, r, s;

  • Gutturals (throat-sound): g, c, k, qu, (ch);

According to the way we articulate the consononants, we can distinguish three major groups:

Occlusive consonants are determined by a closure of the vocal tract while articulating the sound. For example, while pronuncing the sound -p -b we do close the mouth completley for a moment.

Continuous consonants do not imply the complete closure of the vocal tract.

Pronunciation of Consonants

There are no silent consonants in Latin and they are always pronounced as they are written.

In general consonants have the same sounds as in English with few exceptions that require particular attention.

  • The c is pronounced like the english chance before e, i, ae, or oe; otherwise always hard as in cat

    and never as in city: hac, consul, Caeser

    • chance before e, i, ae, or oe; otherwise k as in cat

  • Also ch has always the sound of k, as chorda, chorus, machina.

  • h has not a proper sound but indicates a slight aspiration as in the english hat, honor, horator, rhetor

    • ph, p followed by h with slight aspiration

  • Q occurs always before the vowel u, the two being pronounced as kw: aqua

  • s has generally an hissing sound, as in the English thus and soft.

  • ti like pati, never as "action"

  • v is pronounced like w in woman.

  • z is as dz in adze

  • X at the beginning of a syllable, has the sound of a z as xenium; when located at the end of a syllable, that of ks, as in axis.

Ecclesiatical pronunciation

The sounds of consonants in Ecclesiastical pronunciation are mostly the same as in Classical pronunciation. A few sounds soften somewhat, though, in the later Latin pronunciation, and there is a different pronunciation for the Diphthongs.

The diphtongs ae, eo are pronounced as an e so like the e in bet.

  • aetas, caelum

  • foetidus.

  • poena

Consonants in the Ecclesiastical system

  • c, ch like

  • g, as geniu before e or i; otherwise g as in good

  • ph as f:

  • r always thrilled

  • t is pronounced as pure t except for the group ti which is pronounced as /tzi/, Iustitia, like /justitzia/.

Classical Pronunciation of Latin

The vowels were pronounced thus:

  • a, as a in father or in far.

  • e, as e in bet or ay in bay.

  • i, as i in bin or ee in been.

  • The letters i, j when placed before another vowel, such as iusto, ius, are pronounced like the english consonant y (you).

  • o, as o in done or dot.

  • u, as oo in boon
