Agreement of the Predicate with the Subject

When there is a single subject in a sentence, the verbal predicate agrees with it in number, gender and number:

  • Formicae laboriosae sunt - Ants are hard-working

When the predicate, is an adjective agrees in case, gender, and number:

  • Mater est bona - The mother is good

  • Frater est bonus- The brother is good

  • Fili sunt bonis - The sons are good

When the predicate is a noun it agrees only in case (because the noun has its own gender and number):

  • Voluptātēs sunt saepe causa malorum - Pleasures are often cause of ill

The predicate agrees in gender, number and case with the subject, when it is a Substantiva mobilia, i.e. those substantives which receive different terminations for the masculine and feminine genders like dominus, domina, filius, filia, magister, magistra:

  • Usus bonus magister est - Experience is a good teacher

  • Vita bona magistra est - Life is an inslructress.

Several subjects

When there are several subjects of different persons, but same gender, the verb (if it is a verbal predicate) is put in the plural; Likewise for a nominal predicate in which the nominal part (noun or adjective) and the copula are put in plural, as in English.

  • Ego et frater meus disputamus - I and my brother are discussing

  • Pater et filius boni sunt - The father and his son are good people

When the subjects are of different genders, the masculine is generally preferred to the feminine and the feminine is preferred to the neuter:

  • Mater et Pater fessi erant - The mother and father were tired

  • Pater mihi et mater mortui sunt (Ter. Eun. 517-18) - "My father and my mother have died"

  • Mulieres et mancipia captae sunt - The women and the slaves were captured.

When the subjects consist of inanimate objects, there are several distinction to make:

  • If the inanimate objects are of masculine gender: The predicate is masculine.

  • If the inanimate objects are of feminine gender: The predicate is either femininine or neuter.

    • Ira et avaritia imperio potentiora erant. (Liv.) - Anger and avarice were more powerful than authority

    • Nox atque praeda hostes rĕmŏrāta sunt (Sall.) - Night and plunder detained the enemy

  • If the inanimate objects are of mixed gender: The predicate is neuter:

    • Secunda res, honores, imperia, victoria fortuita sunt. (Cic. Off. II. 16) -

    • Leges cultusque pleraque sunt Sidonica (Sall.) -

    • Murus et porta de caelo tacta erant (Liv.) - "The wall and the gate had benn struck by lightning

When there are mixed subjects: inanimate objects, and persons, generally the predicate follows the gender of the name of persons:

  • Rex regiaque classis una profecti sunt - The king and the king's fleet set out together.

Predicate Agreement with only one subject

In the case of several subjects, the verbal predicate can agree with only one subject, usually the nearest to it, especially if it is the more important:

  • Mens et animus et consilium civitatis posita est in legibus (Cic.) - The spirit, the soul and the wisdom of a people are placed in its laws.

    The predicate "posita est", agrees with sententia and not to the four nouns

This is also the case when the two subjects express only one idea:

  • Senatus populusque Romanus voluit - The Senate and the Roman people wished

or when the subjects are correlated by the conjunctions like et... et; nec... nec; aut... aut; nihil... nihil etc.;

  • Nunc mihi nihil libri, nihil litterae, nihil doctrina prodest (Cic.) - Now books, letters and phylosophy give me no relief.
