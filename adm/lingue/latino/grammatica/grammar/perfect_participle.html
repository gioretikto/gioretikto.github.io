Perfect Participle

A Participle is a verbal adjective: an adjective formed from a verb. A latin verb can have three types of participles:

Participle example traslation
Present activeamans loving
Perfect passiveamatus, amata, amatum having been loved, loved
Future active amaturus, amatura, amaturum about to love

As you can see from the table, Perfect passive Participles in latin are declined as 1st/2nd - declension adjective, ending in -us, -a, -um; For regular verbs in the first conjugation (like amare), this participle is formed by adding -ātus,-āta, -ātum to the present stem; Latin vocabulary entry for verbs are given as follows:

amo, -as, -āvi, - ātum, - āre, 1a tr., «to love»

by taking the ātum form and by preposing the stem of the present am, we get the perfect passive particle for the neuter gender, am-ātum., “having been loved”. By replacing the ending um with us and a, we get the the perfect passive particle for the masculine and feminine gender. For other verbs, the vocabulary entry can be as this:

lego, legis, legi, lectum, legĕre, 3a tr., «lto read»

the form lectum is complete (not shortened); in this case it is enough to replace the desinence -um with the desinces of the adjectives of the first class: lectus, -a, -um, «read».

As an adjective, a participle must agree in number, gender, and case with the noun is modyfing. As you remember predicate adjectives are used with the verb “to be”- sum:

amātus est puer. The boy has been loved/was loved.

amātī sunt pueri The boys have been loved/were loved.

amāta erit puella. The girl will have been loved.

amātae erunt puellae.The girls will have been loved.

amātum erat consilium magistri. The teacher’s advice had been loved.

amāta erant consilia magistri. The teacher’s advices had been loved.
