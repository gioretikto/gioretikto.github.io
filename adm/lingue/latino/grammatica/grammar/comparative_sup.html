The comparative of superiority

The comparative of superiority denotes the quality in an increased degree; It is formed in English in two ways:

  • with the adverb more + adjective + than

  • by adding the endings r or er to the adjective.

In latin the comparative of majority is formed in a similar way, by adding to the positive adjectival stem the suffix:

  • -ior, for the masculine and feminine

  • -ius for the neuter

with the deletion of the thematic vowel.

  • Lepus est velocior quam canis - «The hare is faster than the dog»

  • Hoc oppidum clārius est omnibus aliīs - «This town is more famous that all the others»

  • Aanimi lineamenta pulchriora sunt quam corporis. (Cic.) «The features of the mind are fairer than those of the body».

The adjectives ending in (-eus, -ius, -uus) like:

  • idoneus, -a, -um

  • exiguus, -a, -um

  • necessarius, -a, -um

do not form the comparative by mean of inflection, but they simply use the adverb magis (more) before the adjective in the positive degree. Parse the following examples:

  • Amicus magis necessarius est quam ignis et aqua (Cic.) - «A friend is more necessary than fire and water»

  • magis idoneus, -a, -um; - «more fit»

  • magis exiguus, -a, -um; - «more paltry»

The adjective antiquus, -a, -um, is an exception, having the regular comparative of majority: antiquior.

Second term of comparison

When comparing two nouns, the second term can be expressed either with quam + case of the first term as seen before or with the ablative signifying than. The ablative is prefered in negative sentences and when the second term is a pronoun:

  • Caesar est clarior quam Maximus or Caesar est clarior Maximo - «Caesar is more famous than Augustus»

  • (with pronoun) Amemus virtutem qua nihil divinius est - We love the virtue of which nothing is more divine

  • (with pronoun) Admiror Cicero, quo nemo fuit clarior - «I admire Cicero of whom nobody was more famous»

  • Catō est Cicerōne ēloquentior or Catō est ēloquentior quam Cicero.

  • Leo est fortior quam taurus or Leo est fortior tauro.

  • Cicero fuit doctior quam Tullius/ fuit doctior Tullio.

  • Nihil est certius morte - «Nothing is more certain than death»

  • (Negative sentence) Nihil est virtute divinius - «Nothing is more divine that virtue»

With personal pronouns is also possible to find quam:

Gratior sum quam tu. «I am more grateful than you»;

It is always used the construction quam + case of the first term either when the first term is in the genitive, dative and abaltive case or when the second term is a verb or a preposition:

  • Canis similior est lupo quam vulpi - «The dog looks more like a wolf than a fox»

    • The first term is "lupo" which is in the dative case.
