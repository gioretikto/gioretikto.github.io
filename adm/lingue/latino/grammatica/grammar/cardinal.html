Cardinal Numbers

The only cardinal numbers which are declinable are:

  • unus, -a, -um, «one»;

  • duo, duae, duo, «two»;

  • tres, tria, «three»;

  • The hundreds, from duecenti, -ae, -a, «two hundred», to nongenti,-ae, -a, «nine hundred» (while centum, «a hundred», is undeclinabile);

  • milia, «migliaia».

Osservations

Unus, -a, -um, means "one", "the only one", while "alone" is rendered with solus:

  • Augustus unus restĭtit. «Only Augustus opposed».

  • Solus in ignōta terram errabam. - "I was wandering alone in an unknown land" The plural

uni, unae, una, is also used in the following cases:

  • when it means "the only one", "only":

    • Una mulier clamavit. "Only one woman shouted".

    • Uni Romani barbaris pugnarutnt. «Only the Romans fought the barbarians";

  • when it is opposed to alter: uni ... altĕri, «the ones ... the others», Eg.: Uni flebant, altĕri supplicabant -"Some were crying, the others begged";

  • with the pluralia tantum nouns:

    • una castra, "a camp";

    • una moenia, "one wall";

    Similarly to duo, it is declined, ambo, ambae, ambo ("both"): gen. amborum, ambarum, ambarum, dat. ambobus, ambabus, ambobus etc.

    The termination -o of duo and amboi in direct cases derives from the ancient dual number, which was used to indicate two people, animals or things.

    The numbers from 4 to 100 do not decline, but the numbers from 200, ducenti, -ae, -a up to 900, nongenti, are declined as first/second declension adjectivs: gen. ducentorum, ducentarum, ducentorum, dat. ducentis etc.; the genitive, however, as well as in -orum can also come out in -um: turma trecentorum or trecentum equitum, "a squadron of three hundred horsemen".

    Mille is indeclinable and used either as a substantive or as an adjective. As a substantive:

    • Mille passus, "a mile (1000 paces)"

    • Mille milites, "one thousand soldiers";

    • Agmen mille militum, "A line of a thousand soldiers";

    • Cum mille militibus, "with a thousand

    • Mille pedĭtes ceciderunt. "A thousand infantry fell".

    • Dux cum mille peditĭbus cecĭdit. - The commander fell with a thousand infantrymen.

    As an adjective, mille, is plural only, and indeclinable:

    • Mille homĭnes, "a thousand men"

    • Cum bis mille hominĭbus, "with two thousand men"

    When taken substantively mille has the nueter plural 3rd declension noun Milia or (millia). Milia properly means "thousands" adn it is declined thus:

    The numbers "two thousand", "three thousand", "four thousand" etc. are rendered as duo / tria / quattuor milia; Milia is followed by the genitive of the name to which it refers.

    • Duo milia passsuum, "2 miles" (2000 paces)

    • Ducenta milia hostium fugerunt. "Two hundred thousand enemies fled".

    • Cum sex milibus hostium - with six thousand enemies

    • Darīus tria milia militum in Hispāniam misit. - Darius sent three thousand soldiers to Spain.

    If the thousands are followed up by the hundreds, tens or units, the noun dependent on the number can be rendered either in the genitive case after milia, or at the end of the numerical expression with the same case of the number:


    Two thousand and two hundred soldiers

    • Duo milia militum et duecenti

    • Duo milia et duecenti milites

    With three thousand and two hundred soldiers

    • Cum tribus milibus militum et duecentis

    • Cum tribus milibus et duecentis militibus

    The number «one hundred thousand» is centum milia; its multiples are rendered with the numeral adverb followed up by centena milia («hundred of thousands»):

    a million = ten hundreds of thousands = 10 times one hundred thousand = decies centena milia

    trhee milion = thirty hundreds of thousands = 30 times one hundred thousand = tricies centena milia

    The cardinal numbers for 13, 16, 17, 18, 19, may be used in a separate form, as:

    • 13 = decem et tres

    • 16 = decem et sex

    In the compound numbers above 20, the smaller number may precede as in:

    22 = Duo et viginti (or viginti duo)

    23 = Tres et viginti (or viginti tres)

    The last two number of the set of ten (18, 19; 28, 29 up to 89) are usually formed by subtracting respectivly 2 and 1 from the following ten:

    18 = duodeviginti, literally "twenty minus two"

    19 = undeviginti, literally "twenty minus one"

    28 = duodetriginta, literally "thirty minus two"

    29 = undetriginta, literally "thirty minus one"

    This exception occurs with numbers between 18 and 89 but not in 8, 9, 98, 99, etc.

    From 101 up, put the largest number first without et:

    101 = centum ūnus

    152 = centum quinquāginta duo

    155 = Centum quinquāginta quinque

    In Latin there is no 0, which was introduced later on by the Arabs.
