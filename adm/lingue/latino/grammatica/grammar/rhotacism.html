Rhotacism

A fourth-century B.C., change marking the transition from Archaic to Old Latin is Rhotacism (from the Greek letter rho, denoting "r") a phenomenon whereby the intervocalic s, which is assumed to have been pronounced /z/), invariably became r. Thus mūs has the plural mūrēs (< *mūsēs), as well as:

*arbosem -> arborem

genĕsis -> genĕris

flōsn —> flōrem (Accusative, Old Latin flōsem)

*lupōsum -> lupōrum

*arbŏsem -> arbŏres

*maiosibus -> maioribus

Cicero noted in a letter that a certain Papisius Crassus changes the spelling of his name to Papirius in 340 B.C.; so that this phenomenon may traced back to that period. Further, Appius Claudius Caecus, Censor in 312 A.C., established the spellings of the names Valerius instead of Valesius and Furius instead of Fusius in the list of censors. This is an orthographic evidence that a restructuring of forms through rhotacism, acted by the middle of the fourth century, though it is well known that the censor Cato, who lived between III and II century A.C., spelled frequently arboses instead of arbores.

There are still some instances of intervocalic s, as causa, "cause", quaeso, formosa, that may derive from a reduction of the gemintae /ss/ after a long vowel or a diphthong to /s/ of cassa and quaessom formossa.

Other instances of intervocalis s are found in words borrowed by Latin from other Italic dialects or Greek which did not feature rhotacism, as rosa, 'rose', casa, 'house', caesus, 'cheese', esox, 'salmon', ecclēsia, 'assembly'.
