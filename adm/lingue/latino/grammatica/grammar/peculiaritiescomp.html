Peculiarities in the formation of comparative and superlatives

Adjectives in -er

The adjectives of the first/second declension ending in the nominative case in -er form the comparative regularly (i.e. by adding -ior, -ius to adjectival stem), while the superlative is formed by adding the terminations -er-rĭmus, -er-rĭma, -er-rĭmum (and not -issĭmus):

Positive Comparative Superlative
pig-er («nero») (gen. nigr-i) pigr-ior, -ius pig-er-rĭmus, -a, -um
pulch-er («bello») (gen. pulchr-i) pulchr-ior, -ius pulch-er-rĭmus, -a, -um
paup-er («povero») (gen. pauper-is) pauper-ior, -ius paup-er-rĭmus, -a, -um

Adjectives ending in -ĭlis

The following third declension adjectives ending in the nominative singular -ĭlis form the comparative regularly, while the superlative is formed by adding -il-lĭmus, -il-lĭma, -il-lĭmum, to the adjectival stem:

Positive Comparative Superlative
fac-ĭlis («easy») facil-ior, -ius fac-il-lĭmus, -a, -um
diffic-ĭlis («hard») difficil-ior, -ius diffic-il-lĭmus, -a, -um
sim-ĭlis («similar») simil-ior, -ius sim-il-lĭmus, -a, -um
grac-ĭlis («slender») gracil-ior, -ius grac-il-lĭmus, -a, -um
hum-ĭlis («humble») humil-ior, -ius hum-il-lĭmus, -a, -um
dissim-ĭlis («dissimilar») dissimil-ior, -ius dissim-il-lĭmus, -a, -um

Adjectives in -dĭcus, -fĭcus, -vŏlus

Compound adjectives ending in -dĭcus, -fĭcus, -vŏlus, take their comparative and superlative from forms in ens (entis) form the comparative by adding -entior, -entius and the superlative in -entissĭmus, -entissĭma, -entissĭmum:

Positive Comparative Superlative
male-dĭcus («slanderous») maledic-entior, -entius maledic-entissĭmus, -a, -um
honori-fĭcus («honorific») honorific-entior, -entius honorific-entissĭmus, -a, -um
bene-vŏlus («benevolent») benevol-entior, -entius benevol-entissĭmus, -a, -um

Also the following adjectives form similar comparatives and superlatives:

Positive Comparative Superlative
egēnus («needy») eg-entior, -entius eg-entissĭmus, -a, -um
provĭdus («provident») provid-entior, -entius provid-entĭssimus, -a, -um
valĭdus («valid») val-entior, -entius val-entĭssimus, -a, -um

Note - In reality the adjectives maledĭcus, benevŏlus, egēnus, provĭdus, valĭdus form the comparative and superlative from the present participle of corresponding verbs: maledĭcens, benevŏlens, egens, provĭdens, valens; validus has also the comparative validior, and superlative validissimus.

Adjectives ending in -ĭus, -ĕus, -ŭus

The adjectives ending in the masculine nominative singular in (‐ĭus, ‐ĕus, ‐ŭus) form the comparative and superlative

Positive Comparative Superlative
dub-ĭus («doubtful») magis dubius, -a, -um maxime dubius, -a, -um
idon-ĕus («suitable») magis idoneus, -a, -um maxime idoneus, -a, -um
ard-ŭus («arduous») magis arduus, -a, -um maxime arduus, -a, -um

Si formano invece in modo regolare i comparatives and superlatives of adjectives ending in -quus, because the first -u is not a rela vocal, but a graphic sign to indicate the q sound. For example, antiquus ha regolarmente il comparativo antiquor e il superlativo antiquisssimus.

Comparatives and superlatives with different stems from the positive degree

The following adjectives form the comparative and superlative with different stems from that of the positive degree:

Positive Degree Comparative Superlative
Bonus (good) melior (better) optimus, -a,-um (best)
Magnus (great) maior, maius (greater) maximus, -a,-um (greatest)
Malus (bad) peior, peius (worse) pessissĭmus, -a,-um (worst)
Multī (many) plūrēs (more) plurimī, -a,-um (most)
parvus (small) minor, minus smaller minimus, -a,-um smallest

Parse the following sentences:

  • Optĭmum cibi condimentum fames (Cic.) - «The best condiment in a dish is appetite»

  • Pessima ac doversa inter se mala luxuria atque avaritia, mores civitatis vexabant (Sall.) (Cic.) - «Bad and opposing vices are luxury and avarice that troubles the city's customs»

maxĭme idoneus, -a, -um; - "most fit";

The superlative denotes the greatest degree of the qualtiy, carried by a person or thing, with reference to that of another object; thus it is quite clear that superlative constructions logically require a partitive complement, which in latin can be formed in two ways:

  • with the genitive case;

  • with e, ex, de + the ablative case

Here some examples:

  • Aristoteles dictus est sapientissimus omnium hominum. / ex omnibus hominibus.

    Aristotle was declared the wisest man / of all men;

  • E romanis eloquentissimus fuit Cicero, Cicero was the best orator among the Romans

The partitive complement can also be expressed by means of the prepositions inter or apud, + the accusative case:

  • Honestissimus inter suos (Cic.), «The most honored among his people»;

  • Apud Romanos longe nobilissimus, «The noblest among romans».

Irregular and Defective Comparison of Adjectives

Some adjectives, which do not have the complete set of comparative and superlative derivatives from the same theme, they make up for this deficiency by taking the forms from other adjectives of similar meaning:

Positive Degree Comparative Superlative
iuvenis («young») iunior admŏdum iuvenis or minimus natu
senex («old») senior admŏdum senex or maximus natu
vetus («ancient») vetustior (or vetĕrior) (from vetustus) vetustissimus (or veterrĭmus)
novus («new») recentior (from recens) recentissimus
sacer («holy») sanctior (from sanctus) sanctissimus (or sacerrimus)
ferus («fierce») ferocior (from ferox) ferocissimus

Osservations

Also the following undeclinable adjectives have the compratives and superlatives:

Positive Degree Comparative Superlative
frugi, «frugale» frugalior (from the stem frugalis) frugalissimus
nequam, «da nulla», «malvagio» nequior nequissimus
