Indeclinable substantives

Latin has few Indeclinable substantives; The principals are:

  • fas, «right, lawful» opposite of nefas, «wrong, unlawful»; they are generally used in connection with the verb sum:

    • fas est, «it is lawful»;

    • nefas est, «it is unlawful, it is abominable»;

    They are also used in expression like:

    • contra fas, «Against the divine law»;

    • hoc contra ius fasque est, «this is against human and divine law»

    • Sit fas atque nefas, .. «Be it right, or wrong ..»

  • sponte: propriamente è l’ablativo dell’inusitato spons, spontis, f., used adverbialy: «by choice», «spontaneously»; frequently used after the possessive adjective: meā / tuā / suā sponte «on my/your/his own will or motion».

  • pessum, «in ruin» and venum, «for sale»; They are striclty speaking accusatives, generally used in connection with verbs like pessum dare (also as:pessumdăre), «to destroy, to undo» and venum dare (also as: venumdăre), «to set to sale»; venum dare, «to be set to sale»

  • instar, «(in) the likeness of»; it is an adverbial accusative that is followed by a genitive. As in:

    • Equum instar montis Paladis Arte aedificant, (Aeneis,Liber_II) - «By the divine assistance of Pallas they build a a wooden horse like a (as big as a) montain»;

  • pondo, «in/by weight»; it is the ablative of pondus, -i, disused. It was frequently used with the accusative libram;

    • corona ducentum libram pondo - «A crown of 200 pounds»

    • Auri quinque pondo abstulit (Cic.) - «He carried off five pounds' weight of gold».

    • patĕra aurea centum libram pondo, «A golden-cup of 100 pounds»
