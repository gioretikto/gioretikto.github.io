Gerund

In English the gerund is a verbal noun: it acts as a verb and as a noun. A gerund being a verbal noun can be used either as the subject of a verb or as the object:

  • Smoking is a bad habit

  • Poaching is illegal

As the object of a verb, as:

  • He likes running

The gerund and the infinitive are kindred forms, expressing the meaning of the verb in the form of a noun:

  • Gerund: Excercising is good for health

  • Infinitive: to excercise is good for health

Latin Gerunds and Infinitives are also verbal nouns. As nouns they are both neuter in gender. The infinitive however, is used in the nominative or accusative case, and the gerund supplies its place for the other cases. As as example consider the following phrases:

Nominative:

  • Humanum est errare - to err (error) is human

  • Bibere iucundum est- To drink is pleasant.

The infinitive errare, "to err", is equivalent to a neuter noun in the nominative case "the error". Bibere is als the subject and is present in the nominative case.

Accusative:

  • Sapiens discere amat - The wise loves to learn

  • Ministro bibere - I serve drinks

discere is the direct object of the verb amat. Bibere is direct object of ministro.

When the verbal noun is not functioning as the subject of a verb, the gerund is used. The gerund is declcined as a neuter singular second declension noun; It is formed by adding the ending -nd to the present stem of the verb. As a verb has an active voice.

Genitive laudandi monendi legendi audiendi
Dative laudando monendo legendo audiendo
Accusative (ad) laudandum monendum legendum audiendum

Parse the following examples:

Nominative Legere est difficilis To read (=Reading) is difficult
Genitive cupiditas legendi The desire to read (= of reading)
Dative utilis legendo worth reading
Accusative cupio legere I desire to read (=reading)
Accusative + PREP. te hortor ad legendum I exhort you to read(=reading)
Ablative legendo discitur We learn by reading

The accusative of the gerund is preferred to the infinitive mood when the verbal noun is in immediate connection with a noun and not with a verb ; thus we say, amo legere, I like to read; but te hortor ad legendum, I exhort you to read; te hortor legere, would be something like "I exhort you yo read" a nonsense.

Though a noun, the gereund isn't modified by adjectives but by adverbs reflecting its verbal nature:

Constructions with gerund and gerundive

A gereund can be followed up by a direct object:

cupiditas bibendi vinum: The desire of drinking wine

When the gerund would have an object in the accusative, the gerundive is used instead. The gerundive agrees in number, gender and case with the noun:

  • Ars scribendi epistulas utilis est - The art of writing letters is useful

It also possible the alternative gerundive construction:

  • Ars epistularum scribendarum utilis est- The art of writing letters is useful

This gerundive construction must be used to avoid a Direct Object with the Dative of the gerund, or with a gerund in the dative or in the accusative and ablative cases, dependent on a preposition:

CasesGerund + Direct Object Grundive Translation
Genitive Cupiditas legendi multos libros Cupiditas legendorum multorum librorum The desire of reading many books
Scribendi epistolam Scribendae epistolae of writing a letter
Dative Not possible Utilis scribendis epistolis useful for writing letters
Accusative + Prep Not possible Ad epistolas scribendas to write letters
Ablative multum temporis consumo scrībendō epistulās multum temporis consumo scrībendīs epistulīs I spend much time in writing letters

Other examples are:

  • Animis natum inventumque poema iuvandis. (Hor.) - Poetry was devised to enjoy the spirit.

  • Utilis temperandis animis - useful for governing passions.

  • Praefectus bello gerendo - Set out to conduct war

Uses of the gerund to express purpose

The accusative of the Gerund with the prepostion ad, is often used to express a purpose:

  • Caesar ad cohortandos milites decucurrit. (Caes.) - Caesar rushed to encourage the soldiers

  • Res facilis ad intelligendum - An easy matter to understand

  • ad bellandum venimus - we come to make war

Another construction to express the purposo of the action is the genitive of the gerund with causa or gratia:

  • Haedui legatos ad Caesarem sui purgandi gratiā mittunt. (Caes.) - The Haedui sent ambassadors to Caesar to exculpate themselves.

  • Haec faciunt recuperandorum militum causa (Caes.) - They do this to recover the soldiers

  • Legati Delphos missi sunt oraculi consulendi causa - Envoys were sent to Delphi for the purpose of consulting the oracle

  • vītandae suspīcionis causā (Cat.) - in order to avoid suspicion

  • simulandī grātiā (Iug.) - in order to deceive
