Prepositions with the Ablative and Accusative

In, «In, into; towards, until; against»

With The Accusative always implying motion or direction towards, as

  • Attulit in cavea pullos (Cic.) - He brought the chickens in a cage

  • In coelum ascendere (Cic.) - To ascend into heaven

Also figuratively:

  • Comis in uxorem - Civil to his wife

temporal as

  • Vindicari in posterum diem (Liv.) - To have the provisional possession granted till the next day

to express a distributive/division sense

  • Gallia est omnis divisa in partes tres (Caes.) - Gaul as a whole is divided into three parts

In + Ablative

In + ablative expresses the “place where” or location as well as the "tepomparal location"

  • Sedere in equo (Cic.)- To sit on horseback

  • Partim virorum ceciderunt in bello - Part of the men fell in the war

  • Vix ter in anno nuntium audire possunt (Cic.) - They can receive news scarcely three times in the year

  • in bello/pace - in time of war/peace

and also figuratively with regards to feelings:

  • Difficile est dictu, quanto in odio simus apud exteras nationes (Cic.) - It is difficult to say in what detestation we are held among foreign nations

  • In amicis fideles. (Sall.) - Faithful in regard to their friends

Sub, «Under, up to»

Sub with the accusative denotes motion towards, whith the ablative, rest in a place:

under, with motion

  • Exercitum sub iugum mittere (Caes.) - To pass an army under the yoke

  • Octavum iam annum Saguntum sub hostium potestate erat. (Liv.) - Saguntum was already in the eighth year under enemy control

Temporal location

  • Sub vesperum - On the approach of evening.

  • Sub lucem - Towards dawn

  • Sub eas (literas) statim recitatae sunt tuae (Cic.) - Directly after that letter was read yours

  • Sub noctem (Cic.) - At nightfall

Sub + Ablative

Signifies:

  1. under, usually without motion:

    • Sub hoc iugo Dictator Aequos misit (Liv.) - Under this (kind of) joke the Dictator made the Aequians pass

    • Sub terra habitare - to live under ground;

    • Sub divo (Hor.) - Under the open sky/in the open air;

  2. temporal location

    • Sub divo Augusto (Sen.) - Under the divine Augustus

    • Sub adventu Romanorum (Liv.) - Just before the arrival of the Romans

    1. Figuratively

      • Sub ea conditione - Under that condition

      • Adhuc sub iudice list est (Hor.) - The matter is still before the judge

    Super, «Above, beyond, concerning»

    This preposition also, like sub, is said by grammarians to govern an accusative or an ablative, according as it expresses a state of motion or rest; but this cannot be taken as a general rule;

    Super + Accusative

    Super with the accusative expresses

    1. Place relations (beyond, above, besides, upon at, over, besides)

      • Super Sunium navigare - To sail beyond Sunium

      • Super aspidem assidere (Cic.) - To sit on a snake;

      • Super terram - Above the earth

    2. time Relations (during):

      • Super cenam collocuti sumus - During supper we conversed

    3. Figuratively:

      • Super omnia - Above all

      • Vulnus super vulnus - Wound upon wounds

    Super + Ablative

    When Super signinfies about, concerning, for or because of, it takes the ablative and can express

    1. Place (over, upon):

      • Ensis (illi) super cervice pendet (Hor.) - A sword hangs suspended over his neck

      • Hic tamen hanc mecum poteris requiescere noctem fronde super viridi (Verg.) - Yet here this night you may take up your rest with me on a bed of green leaves

    2. Subject matter (about):

      • Hac super re scribam, (Cic.) - I will write on this subject (concerning this thing)

    3. Time (during, at)

      • Nocte super media - At midnight /Upon the middle of the night

      4. Subter, «Under»

      Used in Prose almost always with the Accusative

      • Manum subter togam exserere (Livy)- To thrust the hand out from under the toga

      • Rhoeteo subter litore (Cat.) - Neath the Rhoetean shore

      Clam, «Without knowledge of»

      USed indifferently with the accusative or ablative

      • Clam patrem (Ter.) - Without a father's knowledge

      • Clam vobis (Caes.) - Without your knowledge
