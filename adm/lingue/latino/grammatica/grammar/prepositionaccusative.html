Prepositions with the Accusative

Most of the prepositions in Latin are used to govern the use of the accusative case. There are twenty-eight prepositions in Latin, which goverm the accusative:

Ad, «To, Towards, at»

There are different functions of this preposition:

  1. Ad (as apud) has the primary meaning of expreessing the motion towards a place (or to a person in a place): to come "near" or "in the neighbourhood of"; The preposition to before names of countries is translated by in, not by ad. Ad expresses a motion near a place rather than a to the place itself:

    • Ad Siciliam navigare - to sail towards Sicily

    • Ad Capuam ivit - He went to the neighbourhood of Capua

    The preposition is omitted before names of cities or small islands, with domus "house" and rus "country", exception made when the author wants to emphasize the idea of approaching near a place (Capuam eo, «I come to Capua»; Ad Capuam eo, «I come towards Capuam»).

  2. In sentences expressing to return, in the meaning to bring back or lead back, with a verb of motion (as reducere, reddere).

    • Ad castra redire - to lead back to the camp

    • Ad consulem obsides reduxit - He returned the hostages to the Consul

    Ad is frequently strengthened by the adverb usque, which in this connection can generally be translated by up to, or as far as:

    • Ad Tiberim Usque venire - to come up to (as far) the Tiber

      • Figuratevely, ad expresses a purpose for or toward some end and is translated by to or for:

        • Natus ad laudem - Born to be glorious

        • Ad bellum paratum esse - to be prepared (ready) for war

        • Ad defensionem urbis - to defend the city

        The preposition Ad is frequenlty used to express the idea 1) according to and 2) up to a number, as many as (as synonym of circiter):

        • Ad voluntatem domini - At the will of the lord

        • Ad voluntatem nostram loquimur - We speak according to out will (hint)

        • Ad trēs milia et trecenti occisi - 3300 men were killed

        The verb mittere and scribere usually are constructed with ad or with the dative in sentence expressi to whom something is sent.

        • Legatos ad consulem mittere - To send ambassadors to the consul.

        • Statui ad te scribere. (Cic.) - I've decided to write you

      • In relation of time it answers to the question: "Till what time?" and is translated bt to or till:

        • Ad vesperum dimicare - to fight till evening

        • Ad duodecĭmum anno vixit - He lived to his twelth year

Aadversus, adversum, «against, toward»

This preposition serves to:

  1. answer the question "where", locating something as opposite, over, against something else and in this sense is synonym of the preposition contra.

    • Mons adversus Romam (or contra Romam) - A mountain opposite to Rome

  2. it expresses direction toward and in this sense is synonym of versus, also with the idea of hostility:

    • Copias adversus hostem educere (Caes.) - to lead the troops against the enemy

    • Fecerit aliquid Philippus cur adversus eum armatum et bellum gerentem hoc decerneremus; (Livy) - Assume that Philip has done something for which we should pass this decree against him when he was armed and waging war

Ante, «Before»

Ante is used:

  1. In local relation, to express that something is place "before" something else:

    • Castra ante urbem posita - The camp is placed before the town

  2. In relation of Time answering the question "When":

    • ante Christum natum- Before (the time) Christ

    • quem ante me deligo - whom I love before myself (more than myself)

    • Ante lucem ibat ad vespasianum imperatorem (Plin. Iun.) - Before daybreak, he used to go to the emperor vespasian

Apud, «At, by, among»

The functions of this preposition are:

  1. to denote where an action takes place; apud before the name of a place is translated by near, by, at, and is then a synonim of ad:

    • Perdiccas apud flumen Nilus interficio a Seleucus et Antigonus - Perdiccas is slain at the river Nile by Seleucus and Antigonus

    • Apud Hypănim fluvium, qui ab Europae parte in Pontum influit, Aristoteles ait bestiŏlas nasci, quae unum diem vivant. (Cic.) - Aristotle states that at the river Ipani, which flows into the Black Sea on the side of Europe, animals are born that live only one day.

    • Liber meus apud Laelium est - My book is at Laelius' house

  2. With reference to persons or communities, and it is translated by the English preposition among, with, by, before, according to the relation of the person to the mentioned place:

    • Apud Helvetios longe nobilissimus fuit et ditissimus Orgetŏrix. (Caes.) - «Among the Helvetians the noblest by far and the most wealthy was Orgetorix»

    • Preterea rectissime sunt apud te omnia - Everything with you is in a very good state

  3. Figuratevely, apud is often found before a person to indicate the author of a work:

    • Apud Platonem - in the writings of Plato

    • Apud Xenophontem autem moriëns Cyrus maior haec dicit (Cat. M. 79) - In Xenophon too, on his death-bed Cyrus the elder utters these words

    • Videtisne ut apud Homerum saepissime Nestor de virtutibus suis praedicet? (Cic.) - Don't you see in Homer how frequently Nestor talks of his own good qualities?

    4. Circum, circa «About, around»

    Circa is used both in expression of time and place, while circum only of place; they both mean around or about:

    • Urbes circa Neapolim - The cities around Naples

    • Plenum est forum, plena templa circa forum (Cic.) - full are the temples around (surrounding) the forum

    Circa (not circum) is used in temporal expressions:

    • circa lucem or circa lucis ortum - About daybreak

    • circa eandem horam - About the same hour

    Circa has the meaning of about (of numbers):

    • Circa quadrigentos milites - About 400 soldiers

    Both preposition are used to precise the number of persons surrondind somebody:

    • Ex eis trecentos iuvenes inermis circa se habebat (Livy) - Of these the had 300 unarmed youngmen about him

    Cis, citra «on this side of, within»

    They express limitation within some boundary, cis also temporal; They are the opposite of trans, ultra.

    • Cis Rhenum (Caes.) - This side the Rhine

    • Cis Padum (Liv.) - This side the Rhine

    • citra rustici operam - Within the labor of a farmer

    • natus mare citra - Born on this side of the sea"; (i. e. in Italy, and not in Greece.)

    Cis (rarely) has also a temporal meaning within:

    • Cis paucos dies hostis aderit - the ememy will be here within a few days

    • Citra Kalendas Octobris - before the 1st of October

    Figuratevely they mean wihtout i.e. short of, without reaching, not to fulness, as:

    • Peccavi citra scelus - I have committed an offence short of guilt

    • Citra satietatem - not to fulness

    • Cintra hanc necessitatem - without this necessity

    • Phidias in ebore citra aemulum fuit - Phidins was without a rival in ivory

    Contra, «Opposite, Against»

    In its general signification, implies opposition, and hence translated as "against", or "in opposition to" and also figuratevely with the idea of hostility.

    • Italia est contra Africam - Italy is opposite to Africa

    • Contra ea Tissaphernes nihil aliud quam bellum comparavit. (Nep.) Tissaphernes, on the contrary, devoted all his time to preparing for war.

    • Contra Epicurum satis superque dictum est. (Cic.) - Enough and more than enough has already been said about Epicurus

    • Suebi nihil omnino contra voluntatem faciunt. (Caes.) - The Suevi do nothing at all contrary to their inclination/will

    Erga, «Towards»

    It was used figuratevely in the Classical period to express feeling towards persons and things personified:

    • Iustitia erga deos religio dicitur (Cic.)- Justice towards the gods is called religion

    • Divina bonitas erga homines (N. D. ii. 13), the divine goodness towards men.

    Extrā, «Beyond, out of, outside of, free from, except (rare)»

    It is used to indicate something taking place or located outside a:

    • Extra portam Collīnam (Cic.) - Outside the Colline gate

    • Extra noxiam (Ter.) - Free from guilt

    • Extra teli iactum - Out of reach of darts

    In adverbial expression (without, beyond, clear of):

    • extra modum - beyond measure

    • extra iocum - joking apart

    To indicate exception:

    • Extra dŭcem paucosque pratĕrea (Cic.) - Excepting their leader and a few besides

    • Nemo extra eum adest - Nobody is present except him

    Infrā, «Beneath, below»

    of place or relation, as:

    • Infra lunam nihil est non mortale

    • Infra coelum et sidera (Tac.) - Beneath the sky and stars

    In figurative sense:

    • Infra se (Cic.) - Beneath oneself

    • Esse infra infimos homines (Ter.) - To be the lowest of the low

    Inter, «Between, among, during»

    • Inter Padum atque Alpes (Livy) - Between the Po and the Alps

    • Quot prandia inter continuum perditi triennium (Plaut.) - How many lunchepons I have lost during three years together!

    • Quod inter nos liceat (Cic.)- Between ourselves

    Intra, «Within»

    is used to express the boundary withing which any thing is contained referring either to space or time. It is also used in figurative sense

    • Intra Hos sex mense (Phaedr.)- Within the last six months

    • Intra moenia — Within the walls

    • Intra centum (Livy) - Within one hundred

    • Intra legem (Cic.) - Within the law (the limit prescribed by law)

    Iuxta, «next to, according to»

    Iuxta signifies approximation or contiguity, being derived from iungo, "to join". It denotes closer proximity than ad or propre.

    • Iuxta praeceptum Themistoclis (Iustin.) - According to the instructions of Themistocles

    It is also used adverbially for alike, equally, as:

    • Eorum ego vitam, mortemque iuxta estimo (Salut.) - I esteem their life and death alike

    Ob, «Before (of place), on account of, for the sake of»

    used to express the reason or cause of any thing and may be rendered by "for" or "on account of" as

    • Mortem ob rempublĭcam obire (Tac.) - To encounter deth on account of the state

    • Ob hanc rem - On account of this thing

    • Ob quaestum - for gain

    • Ob rem iūdĭcandam pĕcuniam accĭpĕre (Cic.)- To take a bride for one's judical vote

    • qui ob aliquod emolumentum suum (Cic.) - On account of some advantage of their own)

    It means "before", "in front of" only with oculos

    • Mors ob ŏcŭlos saepe versata est (Cic.)- Death was often before his eyes

    • Ob ŏcŭlos exitium versatur - Destruction is before my eyes.

    • Quotiens tua sumo conloquia, quaedam mihi ante oculos praesentiae tuae imago versatur - Whenever I converse with you, some image of your presence is turned before my eyes

    Per, «through, by mean of»

    Signifying continuation of space or time "Through"

    • Mihi quidem videtur Brutus noster iam vel coronam auream per forum ferre posse (Cic. Att XIV. 16) - It looks to me as though our Brutus can now walk through the Forum with a crown of gold upon his head

    • Per agros vagari (Liv.) - To roam over the country

    • Per mare, per saxa, per ignes Hor. - Through the sea, through rocks, through fire

    • Per hiemem (Cato)- Throughout the winter

    • Per viginti annos - Through a period of twenty years

    Also Through denoting the instrument or subordinate agency as

    • Per exploratores cognoscere (Caes.) - To learn by means of scouts

    It can specify the way an action takes place:

    • T. Roscio dare an insutus in culleum per summum dedecus vitam amittere - Roscius [would] lose his life in the most degrading way.

    It rarely signifies on account of

    • Per aetatem (Cic.) - Through (on account of) age

    In oaths as the meaning of by:

    • Per ego te deos oro (Ter.) - By the gods I entreat you!

    • Per ego te, inquit, fili, quaecunque iura liberos iungrunt parentibus, precor (Livy) - By whatever claims bind children to thei parents, I entrat you, my son!

    Post, «Behind, after»

    It is used in litteral and figurative sense to express relations of time as well as place

    • Post me ĕrat Aegīna, ante Mĕgăra - Behind me was Aegina, before me Megara

    • Post mortem - After death

    • Post paucos dies - A few days after

    • Post hominum memoriam - Since the memory of man

    Propter, «Near, on account of»

    • Propter Plătonis stătuam consedimus (Cic.) - We sat down close to the stature of Plato

    • Propter te sedet - He sits next you;

    In stating a cause, signifies "On account of, by reason of, from, for, because of":

    • Te in tantas aerummas propter me incidisse! (Cic.) - You fell in so many calamities because of me!

    • Propter frigora (Caes.) - By reason of cold.

    • Propter quos vivit (Mil. 22) - through whose means he lives;

    • Parēre lēgibus propter mĕtum (Cic.) - To obey the laws on account of fear
