Indeclinable adjectives

A few adjectives in latin are indeclinable and mantain their form unchanged. They are:

Nequam

nequam, «wicked», «briccone», «worthless»; it is the opposite of frugi:

  • homines nequam, «wicked men»;

Frugi

frugi, «frugal», «virtuos», «temperate»; striclty speaking is the dative of the disused noun frux, used generally in the plural: fruges, frugum, f., «frumentum halm-fruit, grain». It is used in expression like:

  • Homines plane frugi ac sobrii, Cic. Verr.- «Gentlemen»; «virtuous men»

  • victus frugi, «A frugal meal»;

Macte

macte, «bravo!», «evviva!»; striclty speaking is the vocative of the disused mactus that is magis actus («glorified», «honored»). It is used to congratulae or praise someone and it is frequently used in conjunction with the genitive or ablative of the thing which is praised:

  • macte animi!/animo!, «good!», «excellect!»;

  • macte virtute!, «bravo!»

It is also used with the imperative of sum, es, esto («be thou»), este, estote, «be thou (plur.)», as in:

  • Macte virtute esto!, «I congratulate you on account of your valour!»; «A blessing on your courage!»

  • Iuberem te macte virtute esse, «I would congratulate you for your valour!»;

The form macte sometimes is also used in the plural:

  • Macti virtute milites Romani este! «O Roman soldiers, to your valour!»;

Necesse

necesse, «it is a necessary thing»; found generally in expression like:

  • mihi hoc necesse est facere, «I must needs do it»

  • necesse habeo scribere, «I must needs write»

Potis

potis (m.) (rarely pote, n.), «able», «powerful», used generally along with the verb sum:

  • potis est, «it is possibile»

  • Festīna, quantum potis es (= potes). «Hurry as much as you can»

from pote + sum, derived from the compound verb possum (pote-sum -> pot-sum -> possum) «I am able», «I can».
