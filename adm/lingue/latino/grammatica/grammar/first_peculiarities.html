Peculiarities of the First Declension

The first declension has some peculiarities concerning the cases and the number.

Peculiarities of the cases

Regarding the cases, it should be noted that:

  • Originally the genitive singular ended in -s. This ending is still found in archaic expression, such as "pater familias", 'head of the household' and in other compounds such as:

    • pater familias («head of the household");

    • mater familias ("The female head of a household");

    • filius familias ("The son of the household");

    • filia familias («The daughter of the household»);

  • the genitive plural, of nouns of Greek origin and those which have the compounds "-gena" ("native of", "born in") and -cola ("inhabitant"), presents two possibile terminations in -um, and in -arum:

Genitive plural
amphora, -ae, f., an anphora amphor-arum / amphŏr-um
drachma, -ae, f., the drachma drachm-arum / drachm-um
Graiugĕna, -ae, m., born in Greece Graiugen-arum / Graiugĕn-um
Troiugĕna, -ae, m., born in Troy Troiugen-arum / Troiugĕn-um
caelicŏla, -ae, m., ihabitant of the heaven caelicol-arum / caelicŏl-um

  • in the dative and ablative plural the termination -abus is used instead of -is, to avoid confusion with the correspondent masculine noun of the second declension, as in:

    • filiis et filiabus ("to the sons and daughters");

    • deis et deabus ("to the gods and goddesses");

    • equis et equabus («to horses and mares»);

    There are also nouns of the first declension that have the locative case, ending in -ae as the genitive. The locative is a Latin grammatical case which indicates a location used exclusively for cities and small islands. It corresponds to the English preposition "in":

    • Romae «In Rome»

    • Corcȳrae «In Corfu»

    Peculiarities of the number

    Some names of the 1st delcension exist only in the plural; they are called pluralia tantum, "inherently plural":

    Noun Meaning
    Athenae, Athenarum, f., Athens
    deliciae, deliciarum, f. delight
    divitiae, divitiarum, f. Richness, wealth
    epŭlae, epŭlarum, f. banquet
    indutiae, indutiarum, f. the truce
    insidiae, insidiarum, f. snare
    minae, minarum, f. menace
    nuptiae, nuptiarum, f. wedding
    Pīsae, Pīsārum, f. Pisa (city in Italy)
    Syracusae, Syracusarum, f., Syracuse
    Thebae, Thebarum, f. Thebes

    These nouns must always be treated as plurals and be followed by a plural verb, if the subject of a clause:

    • Athēnae in Atticā sunt - Athens is in Attica.

    • Athenae Graeciae gloria sunt- Athens is the glory of Greece.

    • Syracusae in Sicilia sunt - Syracuse is in Sicily

    Some nouns have a different meaning in the singular and the plural:

    Singular Plural
    littĕra, -ae , f. «a letter of the alphabet» litterae, -arum, f. «the letter, the literature»
    copia, -ae, f. «the Abundance» copiae, -arum, f. «the troops, the militias»
    fortúna, -ae, f. «luck, fate» fortúnae, -arum, f. «the goods, the substances, the heritage»
    opëra, -ae, f. «the work, the handiwork» operae, -arum, f. «the workers, the workmens»
    vigilïa, -ae, f. «the guard duty» vigiliae, -arum, f. «the sentinels»
