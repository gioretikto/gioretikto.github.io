Ablative of Agent

The Ablative of Agent introduced by the prepositions a or ab + the ablative case, designates the person by whom the action, expressed by a passive verb, is accomplished.

  • Quia non semper viator a latrone, non numquam etiam latro a viatore occidĭtur (Cic.) - Becaus a wayfarer is not always struck down by a bandit; sometime the bandit in turn is killed by the wayfarer;

  • Respondit se malle a cive spoliari quam ab hoste venire, (Quint.) - He replied that he would rather be robbed by a fellow-citizen than be sold by an enemy;

When the action is performed by an inanimate agent, the simple ablative is employed:

  • Tullus Hostilius, fulmine ictus, cum tota domo (Val. Max.) - Tullus Hostilius, struck by lighting, burned with all his family

  • Trahĭmur omnes studio laudis (Cic.) - We all like to be praised

When the substantive is an abstract noun as fortuna, voluptas, virtus, fatum, natura etc, which is perceived as animte, the prepositions a and ab can be employed:

  • Boni viri . . . non trahuntur a fortuna, sequuntur illam (Sen.) - Good men are not dragged by fortune, they follow her

Notas

The dative of agent can be employed istead of the ablative of agent, in constructions with the passive periphrastic conjugation.

  • Castris est vobis utendum, non palaestra. (Nep.) - You must cultivate the exercises of the camp, not those of the gymnasium.
