The apophony

The apophony or Vocalic Alternation, is a set of internal vowel changes expressing different morphological functions (often inflectional). In English this feature is preserved in strong verbs, to express the present and past tenses as in:

begin, began, begun

ring, rang, rung

There are three kinds of Apophony in Latin:

  • Qualitative apophony occurs when the vowel changes - e.g. ĕ becomes ŏ, as in:

    tĕgo, tŏga

    lĕgo, lŏgus

  • Quantitative apophony is as vowel lengthening, as in

    căv-eo(I take care), cāv-i, (I took care)

    fŏv-eo (I warm), fōv-i, (I warmed)

    vĕnio (I come), vēni (I came, perfect)

    tĕgo, tēgula

    rĕgo, rēgula

    mŏveo, mōvi (perfect)

  • Quantitative and qualitative apophony as in:

    căpio, cēpi (perfect)

    făcio, fēci (perfect)

    dōnum, dătus.

    • Apophony is particulary observed in compunds:

    • short vowels in a open syllable become ĭ as in:

      căpio -> concĭpio

      făcio -> confĭcio

      cădo -> concĭdo, decĭdo

      rĕgo -> dirĭgo

    • In a closed syllable a may become e, as in:

      damno -> condemno;

      carpo -> decerpo;

    • a before l becomes u; before nc, ng becomes i, as in:

      salsus -> insulsus;

      tango -> contingo;

    • Diphthongs tend to close - ae may become i, as in:

      caedo -> incīdo

      caedo -> accīdo;

      aestimo -> existimo

    • au become u, as in

      claudo -> inclūdo

      causa -> incūso

    • oe becomes either e, or u, as in :

      coena -> cena

      foecundus -> fecundus

      loedus -> ludus
