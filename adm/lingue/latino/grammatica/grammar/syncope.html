Syncope

The syncope (from Ancient Greek: συγκοπή, 'cutting up') is the loss of a vowel, (frequently short vowels in open syllables,), or an entire syllable, from the interior of a word:

  • calĭdus > caldus

  • dòminam > domnam

  • Somnìtis > Somnis

  • dextĕra > dextra;

  • solĭdus > soldus

  • divitior > ditio (comparative of dives, "rich").

  • mons < monss < monts < montis < "hill"

The name Maecenás, Arpinás, Samníse, Quirís derive from Maecenätis, *Arpinätis, *Samnïtis and *Quirïtis respectively.

Syncopation occurs frequently in verbs with perfect-stems in -ivi, as in:

  • laudavisti > laudavi

  • laudavĕram > laudaram

  • laudavisse > laudasse

  • novisti > nosti

  • novissem > nossem

  • consueverunt > consuerunt

Apocope

The Apocope (from the Greek ἀποκοπή, "cutting off") is the loss of a final vowel (frequently short) in a word, as it has happened in:

  • illíc < illīce

  • istíc < istīce

  • vidén < vidēne

  • redúc < redūce

  • puer < puĕrĕ, "oh boy"

The following imperative throw away the terminating e:

  • dic, from dico

  • duc, from duco

  • fac, from facio

  • fer, from fero

Contraction

Contraction (Lat. contractio, a drawing together) is the phenomenon whereby two or more vowels coalesce. The contraction occurs frequently betwen the stem-vowel and the desinence- as in:

  • rosīs < rosa-is

  • negotī < negoti-
