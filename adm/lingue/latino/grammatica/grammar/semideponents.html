Semi-Deponent Verbs

Τhere are a few verbs which are deponents in their perfect system only (perfect, pluperfect, and future perfect), i.e. the are always passive in form but passive in meaning. In all the other tenses they are active in meaning and form. For this reasong they are called semi-deponents (half deponents). They are:

  • audĕo, audes, ausus sum, audēre, «to dare»

  • gaudĕo, gaudes, gavīsus sum, gaudēre, «to rejoice»

  • solĕo, soles, solĭtus sum, solēre, «to beaccustomed, used to, in the habit of»

  • fido, fidis, fisus sum, fidĕre, «to trust»

along with the two compounds of fido:

  • confīdo, confīdis, confīsus sum, «to confide»

  • diffīdo, diffis, diffīsus sum, confidĕre, «to distrust; to be diffident»

Conversely, revertor and devertor, compunds of verto are deponents in the present and imperfect tenses (i.e. have active meaning; revertor also sometimes in the perfect), but they are passive in ther perfect system.

  • revertor, -ĕris, reverti (part. fut. reversurus), reverti, «return, turn back»

  • devertor, -ĕris, devertii (part. fut. deversurus), deverti, «turn away, turn aside.»
