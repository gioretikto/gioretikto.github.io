The Nominative Case

The Nominative Case is used to denote the Subject of a Sentence, and all the other elements which refer to it as: attribute, apposition and predicative complement.

Verbs with two nominatives

In a sentence there can be two nominatives (construction also known as double nominative): the subject and the predicative noun. The predicate noun is in the nominative case with the following verbs: verbs of being, becoming, appearing:

  • sum, "I am";

  • fio; evado, existo, "I become, exist": Nemo immortalis ignaviā factus est. (Sall.) - No one has become immortal by sloth;

  • nascor, "I am born": Nemo nascitur dives (Sen.) - No one is born rich

  • maneo, "I remain": Munitiones integrae manebant (Caes.) - The fortifications remained entire

  • videor, "I seem";

  • appaero, "I appear";

  • morior, "I die";

It is common to see two nominatives (construction known also as double nominative) with the expression (ego) certior fio de aliqua re - "I am informed, or have information of a matter":

  • Caesar certior factus est per legatos - Caesar was informed by ambassadors

This construction is also used with certain transitive verbs of calling and naming, used in the passive voice: appellor, dicor, nominor, praedĭcor, inscribor, salutor, vocor.

  • Omnes rectae animi affectiones, virtutes appellantur. (Cic.) - All proper affections of the mind are termed Virtues

with passive verbs meaning, to be nominated, chosen, elected, regarded; as creor, eligor, "I am elected"; putor, habeor, indicor, existimor", "I am regarded"; fio, efficior, "I am made"; declaror, "I am declared"; renuntior, "I am proclaimed".

  • Postea Numa Pompilius rex creatus est (Eutr.) - Afterwards Numa Pompilius was made king.

This construction is used even after the infinitive of the above-mentioned verbs, when they follow possum, volo, malo, nolo, cupio, incipio, coepi, desino; videor and the like:

  • Non possunt omnes esse patricii; si verum quaeris, ne curant quidem. (Cic.) - Not everyone can be patricians and, to tell the truth, they do not even care about it

  • Cato bonus esse quam videri malebat (Sall.) - Cato preferred being good to seeming so
