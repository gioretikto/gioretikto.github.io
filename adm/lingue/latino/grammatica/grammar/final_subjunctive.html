Final Subjunctive

We have already studied that the future participle and supine can be used in sentences that express purpose, if in the main proposition there is a verb indicating movement. Now we introduce a more general type of construction of final sentences based on the subjunctive.

The Subjunctive mood is used, with the conjunction ut ("in order that"), in sentences which express purpose, as:

  • Pugnamus ut liberi simus - We fight to be free

  • Milites misit qui urbem caperent - He sent soldiers to capture the city

  • Non ut edam vivo, sed ut vivam edo. (Quint.) - I don't live to eat, but that I may live

Final senences are always secondary, i.e. are always attached to some other sentence, without which they would have no meaning, while it could perfeclty stand by itself. Thus in the example, Milites misit, is entirely independent and self-standing; it represents the primary. The conjunction ut introduces the dependent clause: urbem caperent, which wouldn't have meaning except in connection with the previous sentence, of which action it tells the purpose or motive.

Negative final sentences are introduced by ne:

  • Hoc omitto ne offendam tuas aures. (Cic.) - I omit this not to offend your ears.

  • Litteris stude, ne indoctus sis - Study so not to remain ignorant

  • Oppidum oppugnare insituit ne quem post se hostem reliqueret- He determined to besige the town, that he might non leave any enemy in his rear

It is possible to find the construction with ut non when the negation does not refere to all the sentence, but it is limited to one element:

  • Ne dolere quidem possum, ut non ingratus videar - I cannot even feel sorry, not to sound ungrateful to you

  • Confer te ad Manlium, ut a me non eiectus ad alienos, sed invitatus ad tuos esse videaris. (Cic.) - betake yourself to Manlius, so that you may appear to have gone to foreigners, not banished by me, but to your own, invited.

When in the secondary a comparative is present, the conjunction quo is used in place of ut.

  • Ab initio res vobis exponemus, quo facilius intellegatis. (Cic.) - I explain you the facts from the beginning, in order that you can better understand

  • Me adiuva, quo facilio opera mea sit - Help mein orderthat my job can be easier

There can be more secondaries clauses in a sentence; if the first final clause is positive the conjunction neque is used; if also the first final is negative the conjunction neu or neve are used.

Pronouns used in final sentences are always positive, because the negation is already given by the conjunction ne, to which the pronoun attaches:

  • ne ullus :in orderthat no one (adj.)

  • ne quis:in orderthat nobody (Pron.)

  • ne quid:in orderthat anything

  • ne umquam :in orderthat never

  • ne usquam :in orderthat nowhere
