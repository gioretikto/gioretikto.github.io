Future Participle

English has present active and perfect participles as does Latin, but it has not any future participles. Latin has a future participle that refers to a time subsequent to that of the main verb, and can be translated with the expressions: "intending to", "about to".

Future active participles are first/second declension adjectives, with active value, formed by adding -ūrus to the stem of the supine. All verbs, active and deponent, either transitive or transitive can form the future participle:

fero, ferre, tuli, lātum

video, videre, vidi, visum

The future participle can be used as noun:

Ave, imperator, morituri te salutant (Svet.) Hail, emperor! Those who are about to die salute you!

There fure participle is often used with the verb sum in the active periphrastic, to express an action that is going to happen or likely to happen:

Hamilcar in Africam exercitum traiecturus erat (Liv.) - Hamilcar was about to take the army in Africa.

Discessuri sumus. We are about to depart

Varro dux tuis militibus te est oppugnaturus. (Liv.) - Varro is about to attack you with your own soldiers.

If in the main proposition there is a verb indicating movement, the future participle can express the purpose of an action:

lēgātus vēnit pācem rogāturus -

The same sentence can be rendered with supine:

lēgātus vēnit pācem rogātum

Future Passive Participle

The Future passive participle is known as the gerundive, and it is used in the passive periphrastic.
