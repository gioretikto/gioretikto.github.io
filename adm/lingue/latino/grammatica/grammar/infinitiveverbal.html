Verbal use of the infinitive

The infinitive represents the predicate in infinitive propositions. These are subordinate clauses that can function as subject or direct object of the main clause. The subject is always rendered in the accusative case and never omitted.

Subjective infinitive propositions

Infinitive propositions act as subject generally after:

  • Impersonal verbs or constructions: convenit, decet, expedit, oportet, necesse est, opus est, licet, patet constat, sufficit, fugit, praeterit, piget, pudet, traditum est, dicendum est, nuntiatum est, tradĭtum est, intellegi/dici potest, etc.

    • Nuntiatum est adesse Scipionem cum legionibus. (Caes.) - «Word was brought that Scipio was at hand with legions»

    • Nunc opus est te animo valere (Cic.) - «Now you must be strong in mind»

    • Semel in anno licet insanire (Lat.) — «It is allowed once in the year to be foul»;

  • Impersonal expressions: formed by a voice of the verb sum or another copulative verb (fit, evadit, exsistit, videtur, habetur, etc) along with a:

    • neuter adjective (iustum est, verisimile est, stultum est, etc.):

      • Est stultum me praecipere quid agatis aut quo modo (Cic.) - «It is fatuous for me to lay down what you people are to do or how are you are to do it».

      • Utile est rei publicae nobiles homines esse dignos maioribus suis (Cic.) - «It is good for the State that there should be noblemen, worthy of their ancestors».

    • a noun (mos est «It is custom», fama/opinio/rumor est - «It is hearsay/opinion/ruomor»: tempus/locus est- «It is time/ It is the place»; or with the genitive with the verb sum construction:

      • Stultus est me praecipere quid agatis (Cic.) - «It is a foolish thing that I should prescribe you what to do»;

    Objective infinitive propositions

    Infinitive proposition acting as a direct object, are generally found after:

    • verba sentiendi, verbs that describe acts and states of feeling, thinking, perception: video, cerno, aspicio; audio, sentio; scio, cognosco; nescio, ignoro; animadverto; meditor; iudico, credo, arbitro; spero/despero; fido/diffido; recordor/obliviscor; etc.

      • Haeorum Caesar in omni Gallia summam esse auctoritatem sciebat (Caes.) - «Caesar knew that the authoritativeness of the Edui was highest throughout Gaul»

    • verba dicendi or declarandi: expressing a positive or negative statement: dico, aio, aspicio, adfirmo; nego; fateor; concedo; promitto, polliceor, scribo, respondeo; auctor sum; confiteor; minor; iuro; doceo, certiorem, facio, etc.

      • Hannibal promisit se victurum esse - «Hannibal pledged to win»;

      • Haruspices responderunt nihil illo puero clarius fore(Cic.) - «The Haruspices, responded that no one would be more renowned than that boy»

      When infinitive propositions express the desire for the action expressed by the verb in the main clause to take place they employ ut or ne followed by the subjuntive:

      • Deliberantibus Pytia respondit ut moenibus lignes se munirent.» (Nep.) - «To those who questioned her, Pythie replied that they defended themselves with wooden walls.»

    • verba voluntatis expressing a will, command or prohibition:volo, nolo, malo; cupio, opto, conor, studeo; iubeo, impero; veto, prohibeo; sino, patior; in animo habeo (I have intention to); in animum induco, consilium capio (I decide to) etc.

      • Dux copiata ad castra se recipere iussit - «The commander ordered the troops to come back to the camp».

    • verba affectuum, expressing an emotional state: gaudeo, laetor; doleo, queror; officior dolore (I am in pain); aegere/graviter fero; miror; gratulor (I congratulate).

      • These verbs use quod followed by and indicative or subjunctive to emphasize the cause of the emotional state.

      • Gratulor tibi, quod e provincia salvum te ad tuos recepisti. (Cic.) - «I congratulate you on your safe return from the province»

      • Molestissime fero quod te ubi visurus sim, nescio see (Cic.) - «What troubles me most, is not knowing whenre I shall see you»

      • Minime miramur te tuis praeclaris operibus laetari. (Cic.) - «No wonder that you are pleased with your own noble works».

      The verbal predicate of infinitive propositions

      The verbal predicate of infinitive propositions employs the infinitive mood, which is always relative to the main clause; its tense can be:

      • The present infinitive: when the verb in the infinitive proposition describes simultaneous events to those in the main clause.

        • Dico te bonum esse - «I say that you are good»

        • Socrates quidem cum rogaretur, cuiatem se esse diceret, mundanum, inquit. Totius enim mundi se incolam, et civem arbitratur (Cic.) - When Socrates was asked to what country he belonged, he said "that he was a citizen of the world".

        • Apparet eum esse commotum (Cic.) - «It appears that he is perturbed»

      • The perfect infinitive, represents the action or state expressed by the verb, as past at the time of the leading verb, and must be rendered accordingly, i.e., after a past tense, by the English Pluperfect; and after the present, by the English imperfect or perfect;

        • Dico te bonum fuisse - «I say that you were good»

        • Traditum est Homerum caecum fuisse - «That Homer was blind has been handed down by tradition».

        • Piso negavit se triumphi cupidum umquam fuisse (Cic.) - «Pisone said he was never anxious to celebrate the triumph».

      • The future infinitive represent the action or state expressed by the verb, as future at the time of the leading verb, and must be rendered accordingly.

        • Spero te bonum fore - «I hope you will be good»

        • Adulescens sperat se diu victurum esse - A young man hopes that he will live for a long time;

        • Sperant se maximum fructum esse capturos - «They hope they will derive the greatest profit»
