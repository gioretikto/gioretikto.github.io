Personal Pronouns

A Pronoun is a word used instead of a noun; pro in Latin means "in place of"; The Personal pronouns are not usually expressed when they are the subjects of personal verbs, since the endings of verbs already indicate it.

edo, "I eat"

habeo, "I have"

amāmus, "we love"

In other cases for the purpose of emphasis, contrast or clearnesse, they are expressed:

Hoc feci, "I did this"

Ego hoc fecit - I did it myself

The nominative singular ego, means "I"; the nominative singular of the first person, tu means, "you".

  • Tu dixisti, ego non adfui - You spoke, (but) I wasn't present

Here are the declensions of the first and second person personal pronouns:

In Latin there is no 3rd person personal pronoun "He/She/it", which is rendered with a demostrative pronoun:

  • is "He"

  • ea "She"

  • id "it";

The forms for the 3rd wich appear above must be regarded as reflexive pronouns.

The genitives nostri and vestri, are objectives:

  • memoria nostri - the memory of us

  • cupidus vestri - desirous of (pl.) you

  • Miserēre nostri - Have pity for us

The genitivs nostrum and vestrum are partitive:

  • unus nostrum - one of us

  • unus vestrum - one of (pl.) you

The expressions: "of all of us", and "of all of you" are rendered as: nostrum omnium, vestrum omnium.

The preposition cum is attached as a suffix to the personal pronouns in the ablative case, rather that preceding it, as in:

  • mecum - with me

  • tecum - with you

  • secum - with himself, themselves

  • nobiscum - with us

  • vobiscum - with (pl.)you

Personal pronouns are reinforced and stressed by the enclitic particles -met and -te:

  • egòmet, «I myself»; mihìmet, «to myself»; memet, «my own self»; tibìmet, «to you yourself»; nobismet, «to us ourself»;

  • the pronoun tu is reinforced with -te: tute, «you yourself»;

  • the reflexive pronoun se is doubled: sese, «me myself» (seldomly used are meme and tete)
