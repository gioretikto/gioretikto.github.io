Present Participle

The present active participle, indicates something current and in Latin corresponds to the English present participle, ending in -ing. In Latin there is no present passive participle. The present participle is declined as a second declension class adjective of one ending. It is formed by taking the present stem and adding the following desinences:

  • -ans, -antis for the first conjugation.

  • -ens, -entis for the second and third conjugation.

  • -iens, -ientis for the forth conugation.

For example the verb belonging to the first conjugation amo («to love»), forms the present participle amans, amantis, which is declined as follows:

  Singular Plural
Case Masculine/Feminine Neuter Maschile/Feminine Neuter
Nominative amans amans amant-ēs amant-iă
Genitive amant-ĭs amant-ĭs amant-ĭŭm amant-ĭŭm
Dative amant-ī amant-ī amant-ĭbŭs amant-ĭbŭs
Accusative amant-ĕm amans amant-ēs amant-iă
Vocative amans amans amant-ēs amant-iă
Ablative amant-ĕ/-ī amant-ĕ/-ī amant-ĭbŭs amant-ĭbŭs

Let's see some example:

  • Agricolae messem copiosam optant - The farmers are hoping for a good harvest

  • Dux triumphans plausus a civibus - The winning commander was praised by the citizens.

  • Nautae navigantes in Sardinia pervenerunt - Sailors arrived in Sardinia sailing

  • Philosophi peripatetici deambulantes disputabant - Peripatetic philosophers discussed walking.

The ablative singular has two possible endings in -i when used as an adjective, in -e when used either as a noun or verb. Some examples:

Deambulare cum viro sapienti (adj.)

Deambulare cum sapiente (noun)

splendente luna (noun)

splendenti gladio
