Russian Nouns

Russian nouns have:

  • two classes: animate, inanimate;

  • two numbers: singular, plural;

  • three genders: masculine, feminine, neuter.

  • three declension: first, secondo, third

  • six cases: Nominative, genitive, Accusative, Dative, Locative, Instrumental.

Animate nouns answer the question who? Кто?
Inanimate nouns answer the question what? что.
Animate nouns have two genders: masculine and feminine.

All Russian nouns belong to one of the three genders: masculine, feminine, neuter. Why is it important? When later we start using adjectives and verbs, it is the gender that affects the endings. Nella lingua russa nonesiste l'articolo; il genere si riconosce dalla desinenza delle parole.

In Russian, it’s fairly easy to understand what category the word belongs to. First, look at the «natural» gender (I mean the sex of the being). If you understand that it’s a male, the noun is masculine. (МУЖЧИНА – man, ПАПА – dad, ДРУГ – male friend, ДЯДЯ – uncle, ДЕДУШКА – Grandpa). If it’s clearly a female, the noun is feminine (ЖЕНЩИНА – woman, МАМА – mom, МАТЬ – mother). Second, look at the ending.

maschili: Tutti i sostantivi, che terminano in consonanti, come (ученйк - «scolaro»), alcuni che finiscono in -ь (словарь - «dizionario») e tutti in -И (ча Й).

Neutri: Tutte le parole che terminano in -o (слово - «parola») in -e (море) oppure in -мя (вре мя) sono neutre.

Also remember that words ВРЕ́МЯ (time) and И́МЯ (name) are neuter, too.

Il verbo ausiliario быть (essere) non si usa al presente indicativo, salvo nella terza persona singolare есть (jestj) che equivale a c'è. Esempi:

  • Кто ученик? Chi (è) lo scolaro?

  • Он ученик. Egli (è) uno scolaro.

  • Есть чай? Есть: - C'è del té? Ce n'è.
