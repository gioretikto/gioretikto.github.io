Negative sentences

We call a sentence negatives if it contains negative words like:

not, no, none, nothing, nowhere, neither, nobody, no one, hardly, scarcely, barely.

Two negatives should never be used together in the same sentence.

  • Right (one negative): «He told me nothing about it» or «He told me nothing about it»

  • Wrong (two negatives): He di not tell me nothing about it.

If you use negative words twice in a sentence, the sentence becomes positive instead of negative. Two negative in the same sentence cancel each other out and create a positive sentence, so When a sentence contains the word not, the indefinite word that is the opposite of the negative word must be used: I am not studying anything. e.g.

  • It is not an uncommon sight = It is a common sight

Negative and positive responses

so = usually used before the verbe do, be, and have, the modal can, and other auxiliary verbs to express the meaning “in the same way” or “similarly”.

too = used especially at the end of a sentence to show “in addition” or “also”.

either/neither = used with or as negative expressions to show that the listener agrees with the speaker's former negative comment.

  • The word “either” is used with a negative verb (e.g. have NOT).

  • The word “neither” is used with an affirmative (positive) verb (e.g. have).

Both have the same meaning! Let’s look at one of the previous examples.

A: My mother can’t drive a car.

B: My mother can’t (drive) either. / Neither can my mom.

Let’s look at the second example:

A: I haven’t seen Michael today.

B: I haven’t (seen him) either. / Neither have I.

Rule: when the verb is negative, you cannot use “too.” Remember that we are talking about the verb and NOT the meaning of the message. For example, “I hate carrots.” has a negative meaning, but the verb hate is not in a negative form. If you also hate carrots, you have to say “Me too!” since the previous sentence does not have “NOT” in it.

Initial positive statement: I like Japanese food a lot. Two responses showing agreement: So do I./I do, too.

  • One response showing disagreement: Really? I don't like it very much. Initial negative statement: I don’t like greasy food.

  • Two responses showing agreement: Neither do I./I don’t either.

  • One response showing disagreement: Oh, I like it a lot.

Point out how the adverbs so and too are used to show agreement in responses to initial positive statements, like this:

  1. When a verb other than be is in the initial statement (e.g., “I lovel like/enjoy ...."), use do or don’t in the response (e.g., “So do I./I do, I like it too / Me too.” for agreement, or “I don't.” for disagreement).

  2. When the verb be is in the initial statement (e.g., “I'm crazy about... .”), use am or 'm not in the response (o.g., “So am I/I am, too.” for agreement, or “I’m not.” for disagreement).

  3. When the modal can is in the initial statement (e.g., “I can eat... .”), use can or can’t in the response (e.g., “So can I/I can, too.” for agreement, or “I can't.” for disagreement).
