Deponent Verbs

A number of verbs are passive in form but active in meaning; as sequor, I follow, hortor, I encourage; vereor, I fear. Such verbs are called deponent because they have laid aside («depōno», I lay aside) the active forms.

They can be either transitive or intrasnitive and have four conjugations:

INDICATIVE PRESENT INDICATIVE PERFECT INFINITIVE PRESENT CONJUGATION
hortor, -āris hortātus sum hortāri 1st dep. tr., to encourage
verĕor, -ēris verĭtus sum verēri 2nd dep. tr., to fear
sequor, -ĕris secutus sum sequi 3rd dep. tr., to follow
partior, -īris partītus sum partīri 4th dep. tr., to divide, share, distribute

A rule of the thumb to know how to conjugate deponent verbs is the following:

  • if the infinitive ends in -āri (like hort-āri) they are conjugated like laudor;

  • if the infinitive ends in -ēri (like ver-ēri) they are conjugated like moneor;

  • if the infinitive ends in -i (like sequ-i) they are conjugated like legor;

  • if the infinitive ends in -īri (like larg-īri) they are conjugated like audior.

Deponents have the same endings as those of the passive voice of ordinary verbs, and also the participles, gerunds, supines, and participial formations of the active voice, excpetion made for the following forms which are active:

  • the future infinitive: hortaturum, -am, -um esse ecc., and not hortatum iri;

  • the present participle: hortans, -antis;

  • the future participle: hortaturus, -a, -um;

  • the gerund: hortandi, hortando etc.;

  • the supine in -um: hortatum.

The present and future participle, gerund and supine in -um are missining in the pasive, therefore the deponent verb, take these form from the active conjugation.

The gerundive and passive supine of trasitive deponent verbs, are also passive in meaning, in addition to possess passive forms:

  • hortatus - «one who ha admonished»

  • hortandus - «one who is to be admonished»

The great majority of the deponents belong to the first conjugation.
