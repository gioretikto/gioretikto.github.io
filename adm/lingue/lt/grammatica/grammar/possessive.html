Possessive Pronouns and Adjectives

Possessive pronouns can be used also as adjectives:

Frater meus (adjective) altior est quam tuus (pronoun). «My brother is taller than yours».

They are:

PERSON SINGULAR PLURAL
1st meus, mea, meum, «my» noster, nostra, nostrum, «our»
2nd tuus, tua, tuum, «your» vester, vestra, vestrum, («your»)
3rd suus, sua, suum, «his(reflexive)» suus, sua, suum, «their (reflexive)»

Possessives are declined as first and second class adjective; they do not have the vocative except for noster which is regularly noster and meus which has the form mi, execpt in the expression:

  • mi Bone Deus- «Oh my good God!»

which can also be expressed as:

  • Deus meus!, «Oh my God!»

Possessive pronouns can be intensified with the particle -pte, like meapte,tuapte, suapte, nostrapte, vestrapte:

  • meapte causa, «for my own sake»

  • nostrapte culpa, «by our fault»

  • meapte manu, «with my own very hand»

  • suopte pondere, «by its own weight»

Like personal pronouns, possessive pronouns sare used very much less in Latin thanin English. When possible, they are omitted, like in:

  • Mater filias amat, «The mother loves her daughters»

  • Totam vitam in studium consumpsi, «I spent my life studying»

The adjective mea in the above sentence is omitted because it is clear from the context. It cannot be omitted when there can be ambiguity as in:

  • Donuum tuum accēpī, «I received your gift»

The possessive adjective is generally postponed to the noun, as in the example above, but it can also be placed before the noun to put emphasis, as in:

  • Me si fata meis paterentur ducere vitam auspicis et sponte mea componere curas (Verg.), «Had the fates left me free to conduct my life by my own direction, and ease my Cares by Means of my own choosing»

Reflexive and non-refleve function of posssessive pronouns

The possessive pronouns for the 1st and 2nd person, meus, tuus, noster, vester are used in a reflexive sense when they refer to the subject of the clause. They have a non-reflexive sense when they do not refer to the subject:

Reflexive sense

  • (Ego) vidi matrem meam, «I saw my mater»

  • (Tu) vidisti matrem tuam, «You saw your mater»

Non-Reflexive sense

  • (Ego) vidi matrem tuam, «I saw your mater»

  • (Tu) vidisti matrem meam, «You saw my mater»

To show reflexive possession in the 3rd person, (i.e. If the possessive refers back to the subject of the sentence or clause), Latin uses the reflexive possessive adjective: suus, sua, suum. Non-reflexive possession (i.e. possessions belonging to people not the subject of the sentence/clause) is shown using the genitive of the determinative pronouns is,ea, id:

3rd Person Reflexive possession

  • Discipulus probat magistrum suum, «The pupil esteems his teacher»

  • Mater amat filias suas, «The mother loves her daughtel»

3rd Person Non-Reflexive possession

  • Libros eorum habeo, «I have their books»

  • Patrem eorum vidimus , «We saw their father»

  • Denique Q. Caecilius Metellus consul Iugurtham variis proeliis vicit, elephantos eius occidit vel cepit (Eutropio IV 27), «Finally the consul Quintus Caecilius Metellus conquered Jugurtha by various battles, he killed or seized its elephants, he accepted many communities in surrender.»
