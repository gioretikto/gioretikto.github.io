The genitive with the verb sum

The genitive with the verb sum (construction also know as genitive of pertinence) denotes to whom or what a thing belongs, as being suitable and appropriate. In English it is introduced by the phrases, "it is suitable, proper" or "it is the sign of", "it is up to" and the like; in Latin the voice of the verb sum is taken in the sense of "it is a person's office, business, lot", wiht the correspondend Latin words munus, "it is the office"; officium, "it is the duty; or the adjective proprium, "it is the part of", generally understood but rarely expressed.

  • Est adulescentis maiores natu verēri. (Cic.) - It is proper of young people to have respect of older people.

  • Sapientis est sibi temperare - Self-restraint is the part of a wise man.

  • Sapientis est proprium nihil, quod paenitere possit, facere. (Cic.) - It is the characteristic of a wise man to do nothing that he can regret

To express one's affair, task, duty, etc., the possessive neutral is used: meum, tuum, nostrum, vestrum est.

"It is up to him, to them" is rendered with eius / illius, eorum / illorum est;.

suum is used with the infinitive and the subjunctive, when "his" and "their" refer to the subject of the main clause and are used in a reflexive sense.

  • Tuum est id procurare - It is your duty to manage that.

  • Est tuum, Cato, videre quid agatur. (Cic.) - It is your duty, Cato, to see what is happening.

  • Dux ait suum esse militium saluti consulĕre (Liv.) - The commander said it was his duty to provide for the safety of the soldiers.

  • Sapientis est consilium explicare suum. (Cic.) - It pertains to a wise man to express his mind.
