The Accusative Case

The Accusative is the case of the Direct Object of a transitive verb and of all the other elements that refer to it: attribute, apposition and predicative complement of the direct object. It denotes that which is directly affected, or that which is caused or produced by the action of the verb:

  • Magister discipulum laudat - The teacher praises the pupil

  • Legatos mittunt - They send embassadors

  • Brutus Caesarem interfecit - Brutus killed Caesar

The accusative case is also used to express the semantic role of destination with the preposition ad and to show motion toward.

Transitive and Intransitive Verbs

A Transitive Verb has or requires a direct object to complete its sense as - discipulum laudat, "he praises the pupil".

An Intransitive Verb admits no direct object to complete its meaning; as curro, "I run".

Many verbs transitive in English are Intransitive in Latin. Conversely, some verbs intransitive in English are transitive in Latin or at least are often so used. The following are classes of Latin transitive verbs (thus constructed with the accusative) expressing:

  1. verba affectuum: expressing Feeling, or Mental State: despero, "to despair of"; dŏleo, "to grieve for"; gĕmo, "to sigh over" ; horreo, "to shudder at", lăcrĭmo, "to weep over"; moereo, "to mourn over", mīror, "to wonder at"; rīdeo, "to laugh at", sĭtio, "to thirst for", etc

    • Pacem desperavi - I lost hopes of peace

    • Quis non doluit rei publicae casum? (Cic.) - Who did not deplore the misfortunes of the State?

    • Caesaris mortem ulcisci volebant (Cic.) - They were out to avenge Caesar's death

    • Sitio honores - I thirst for honors

    • Gladiator sanguinem nostrum sitiebat (Cic.) - The Gladiator was thirsting for our blood

    • Itaque rideo istos, qui turpe existimant cum servo suo cenare. (Sen.) - Therefore I laugh about those who think it is disgraceful to eat with one's slave

    • Honores desperat (Cic.) - He despairs of honors

  2. Verbs expressing Taste or smell: ŏleo, redŏleo, sapio, resipio, rodoleo, sitio, and their compounds used both literally and figuratevely:

    • Hoc Vinum resipit picem - This wine tastes of pitch

    • Olet hircum (Cic.) - He smells like a goat

    • hominem pagina nostra sapit (Mar.) - My page tastes of humankind

Notas

Some verbs have sometimes an Accusative other times a Dative or ablative:

Effugio signifying «I avoid» has the accusative: effugere scopulos, «evitare gli scogli»; when it signifies «I escape», «I flee» from a place, or sitution; it is found with the preposition e, ex and the ablative case: effugere pericula, «To avoid danger»; effugere e manibus, «to slip out of or from one's hands»; Effugere ex urbe, «to flee out of town».

Deficio can be used as:

  • active transitive verb: Prudentia numquam defĭcit oratorem. (Cic.) «All’oratore non manca mai laprudenza»;

  • passive transitive, in expressions menaing "leave behind": deficior viribus, «I grow weak»; deficior aqua ciboque, «I am wanting water and food»;

    • Nostros vires lassitudine deficiebant. (Cic.) - Our men's strength was beginning to fail from exhaustion

  • intransitive, in expressions as: deficere animo, «to lose one's courage, to be disheartened»; deficere ab aliquo ad aliquem, «To revolt to one, to desert to any body»; sol, luna defĭcit, «the sun, moon is eclipsed»;

Despēro, "I despair", when Transitive, governs an Accusative; when Intransitive, either a Dative or an Ablative with the construction de + ablative:

  • Desperat de se, «He despairs of himself»;

  • Galli, nisi perfregerint munitiones, de omni salute desperant (Caes.) - «The Gaul, despair of all safety, unless they should force the line»

Abdĭco, "I resign", "I abdicate", takes sometimes an ablative, and sometimes an accusative of the thing renounced;

  • Abdicare se consulatu or Abdicare consulatum - to resign the consulship

  • Caesar dictaturā se abdicavit (Caes.) - Caesar resigned the dictatorship
