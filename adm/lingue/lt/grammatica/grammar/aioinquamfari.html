Aio, inquam and fari

Aio

The verbs Aio «I say», «I assert» is used:

  1. as opposite of nego, «I say no», «I deny»; e.g.:

    • Diogenes ait, Antipater negat, cui potius assentior, (Cic. De officis Lib. III) - Diogens says yes, Antipater denies it, to whom I rather assent.

In the past in colloquial Latin, the contracted form ain? (from aisne?) "meanest thou?" was used, the affermative answer could be aio "Yes, I do";

The imperative ai, is obsolete e.g. «vel ai vel nega (Navius)» - "say yes or no", and the perfect ait is like the present. In the passive voice there is only the form aiuntur.

Aio is used in the following cases:

  • Beetween the words of a quotation, particularly inside parenthetical clauses:

    • Bis dat, ut ait Pūblilius, qui dat celeriter - «As Pūblilius says, "He gives twice who gives in a trice"»;

    • Etenim, ut ait Homerus, “ex eius lingua melle dulcior fluebat oratio”. (Cic.) - For as Homer says, “Speech sweeter than honey flowed from his tongue”.

  • instead of dico, to introduce indirect speech:

    • Legati iam Hannibalem fines invasisse aiebant - «The messengers announced that Hannibal had already invaded the territories»;

    whilst it is not used in direct speech, as inquam is preferred.

Inquam

Inquam, «I say», is always an interposed word and never placed before the words quoted; it is used in indirect discourse:

  • “Satis”, inquit Epaminondas, “vixi: invictus enim morior”. «I have lived, says he, long enough; for I die unconquered».

Like fari are conjugated it compounds:

  • af-fāri, «I speak to, address»;

  • ef-fāri, «I speak, say out, utter»;

  • prae-fāri, «I say, utter», «I mention beforehand or in advance»;

  • pro-fāri, «I speak out», «I foretell, predict, prophesy»;
