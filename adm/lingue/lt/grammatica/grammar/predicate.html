Predicate

The Predicate (predicatum from predico, "I predicate") is that which is said or "predicated" of the subject, and includes the rest of the sentence. Depending on whether the predicate is a noun or a verb, a clause is said to be nominal or verbal.

  • The verbal predicate is composed by one or more predicative verb, a verb (active, passive or deponent) that can stand alone in the predicate having a complete meaning:

    • Scripta manent - written words remain

    • Puer in Ecclesia cantat - The boy sings in the church

  • The nominal predicate consists of a nominal part (noun or adjective) and the copula (i.e. connective) esse. The verb esse links the subject to the nominal part of the predicate.

    • Iustitia est regina virtutum (Cic.) - Justice is the queen of virtues. - est is the copula; regina virtutum the nomial part

    • Fortuna caeca est- fortune is blind; fate is blind.

    • Amicus est tamquam alter idem (Cic.)- A friend is, as it were, a second self

    There are some sentences in which the verb esse is not taken as a nominal predicate but as a verbal predicate. This occurs when the verb sum signify «to exist», «to belong to».

    • Pythagoras in italia erat - Pythagoras was in Italy.

    • Sunt viri fortes - There are (exist) brave men.

    In these sentences erat and sunt are verbal predicates and not copulas. While in :

    • Pythagoras graecus erat

    The verb erat is linked to a true nominal predicate.

Notas

The verb sum, is not the only copulative verb; other copulative verbs:

  • videor («to be regarded, seem»)

  • fio («to become»)

  • appareo («to appear»)

and the like, e.g.:

  • Illorum mors beata videtur - «Those who have died are accounted happy»

  • Melior fio - I am recovering my health
