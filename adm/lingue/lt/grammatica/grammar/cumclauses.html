Cum Clauses

Besides the function of cum as a preposition meaning "with", accompained by the ablative case, cum can also be used as a conjunction.

The most common way to introduce a temporal proposition is by using the conjunction cum, followed up by a conjunctive or indicative.

Cum + Indicative

Cum, with the indicative mood has the general meaning of "When", dating the action of the main clause:

  • Cum a te litteras accipio, laetus sum - When I receive a letter from you, I am happy

  • Cum legimus, discimus - When we read, we learn

  • Facile omnes cum valemus recta consilia aegrotis damus - We all find it easy to give the right advice to the sick when we are well

  • Senes cum rem publicam consilio defendebant, nihili agebant? (Cic.) - Old people, when they defended with their advice the state, did nothing?

  • Cum Caesar in Galliam venit, alterius factionis principes erant Aedui, alterius Sequani - When Caesar came to Gaul the leaders of one of the two factions were the Haedui, the Sequani of the other.

  • Ego, cum Pompeium ipsum audio, prorsus eum libero omni suspicione cupiditatis, cum autem eius familiares ominium ordinum video, perspicio eius corruptionem. (Cic.) - Every time I listen to Pompeus himself, I release him completely from all suspicion of greed; when I , instead see his intimates of all kinds, I discover his corruption

  • Marcus, filius meus, cum primum sapere coepit, acerbos dolores miseriasque percepit. (Cic.) - My son Marco, as soon as he began to understand, has known bitter pains and misfortunes

  • Animus nec cum adest, nec cum discedit, apparet (Cic.) - The soul is not seen, neither when it is present, nor when it leaves.

Cum meaning whenever takes also the indicative:

cum ego rosam videram, laetus eram - Whenever I saw a rose, I was happy

Cum inversum

Another construction, is the so-called cum inversum translated with «when suddenly», sometimes reinforced by adverbs as subito or repente. It enunciates an unexpected fact and expresses a relationship of contemporaneity with the main clause; usually has the perfect indicative or historical perfect (in the main clause it is possible to find find adverbs like aegre, vix, nondum, iam):

  • Hannibal iam subibat, Romani erumpunt in eum cum repente- Hannibal was already approaching, when the Romans made a sortie against him.

  • Vixdum epistulam tuam legeram, cum ad me amicus quidam venit - I had just read your letter, when Curtius came to me

Sometimes cum is coupled with tum in the main clause, which two different functions. It can mean "when.... then(adverbial)....", as in:

  • Fortuna vitrea est: tum cum splendet, frangĭtur. (P. Syr.) - Luck is crystal: when it shines, (then) it breaks.

  • Cum rosam viderat, tum incipere ver arbitrabatur Verres. - Whenever he saw a rose he used then to think spring to be coming.

This construction can also be used after a general clause with cum, introducing a particular or emphatic assertion, that can be translated as: "as . . . so", "while . . . also", "not only . . . but also", "as . . . so especially" :

  • cum multa non probo, tum illud in primis (Cic.) - while there are many things I do not approve, there is this in chief.

  • cum haec sunt videnda, tum vero illud est hominis magni,.. (Cic.) -while these things must be taken into account, but what characterizes especially a great and wise man ..

cum + Subjunctive

cum is used with the Subjunctive to denote the circumstances or situation under which an action occurs; for this reason it is also know as cum narrativum/historicum. It can be used to form: causal, concessive and circumstantial clauses.

Cusal Clauses: translates as "since" or "because"

  • Haec cum Crassus dixisset, silentium est consecutum. (Cic.) - After that Crassus pronounced these words, seguì il silenzio.

  • Cum pater domum venisset filios obiurgravit - Since the father arrived home, he reproaced his sons

  • Cum esset propter bellum fames, tumultus dissensionesque inter pauperes exardescebant - Since there was a lack of food due to the war, riots and conflicts between the poor broke out.

  • Cum ver incipiat Paulus Romam remeat - Since spring is beginning, Paulus comes back to Rome

Circumstantial Clauses:

  • Cum in curiam ingressus esset, Caesar a coniuratis occisus est - After he entered the curia, Caesar was killed by the conspirators

  • Pythagoras, cum in geometria quiddam novi invenisset, Musis bovem inmolavisse dicitur - It is said that Pythagoras, when he found something new in the study of geometry, sacrificed an ox to the Muses

  • Alexander, cum interemisset Clitum, familiarem suum, vix a se manus abstinuit. (Cic.) - Alexander, after killing Clito, his friend, barely held his hand away from him (He struggled not to commit suicide)

Concessive Clauses: can be translated as "Although":

  • Cum Alpes superavisset, Hannibal Romam non peti - Although Hannibal crossed the Alps, he could not arrive to Rome

  • Cum milites fessi sint, fortier pugnant - Although the soldiers are tired, they fight bravely

  • Cum velim consolatum capere, nequeo quod non sum nobilis. - Although I want to get the consulate, I can not because I am not a noble.

Summary

It can be subtle to distinguish between the Cum + indicative temporal clause and the Cum + subjunctive circumstantial clause. The Cum + indicative construction marks coincidence without a causal link whilst the cum + subjunctive marks temporal coincidence along with a causal link. To distinguish between the two, you should ask yourself if the statement of the main clause would be true regardless the cum clause. If so, it is a temporal clause. Otherwise it can be a circumstantial clause.
