Greek nouns of the first declension

Most proper and common names of greek origin follow the latin declesion as: bibliothēca, -ae (from Ancient Greek βιβλιοθήκη); philosŏphĭa,- ae (from φιλοσοφία); poëta, -ae (from ποιητής). They all follow the latin first declension.

Some nouns instead retain the terminations corresponding to the Greek declension, while others may present both Greek and latin terminations. The Greek names of the first declension can be masculine and feminine. Those in -ā and -ē are feminine; those in -ās or in -ēs are masculine. Æneas and Anchises (Æneas's father) are the Greek names of two Trojan heroes; They are thus declined:

Nominative Aenēas
Genitive Aenēae
Dative Aenēae
Accusative Aenēan, Aenēăm
Vocative Aenēa
Ablative Aenēa

Nominative Anchīses
Genitive Anchīsae
Dative Anchīsae
Accusative Anchīsēn (-am)
Vocative Anchisē (or -ā)
Ablative Anchisē (or -ā)

Other Greek proper nouns belonging to the first declension are Alcmene, -es, f., "Alcmena"; sophistes, -ae, m., "the sophist"; Circe, -ae, f (a goddess). They are declined thus:

Case Feminine in -ē Masculine in -ās Masculine in -ēs
Nominative Alcmen-ē (-ā) Aenē-ās sophist-ēs (-ā)
Genitive Alcmen-ēs (-āe) Aenē-ae sophist-ae
Dative Alcmen-ae Aenē-ae sophist-ae
Accusative Alcmen-ēn Aenē-ān (-ām) sophist-ēn (-ām)
Vocative Alcmen-ē (-ā) Aenē-ā sophist-ē
Ablative Alcmen-ē Aenē-ā sophist-ē (-ā)

Terminations indicated inside brackets are more frequently used in prose. Many names of Greek origin in -ēs follow the third Latin declension. Noun entries in a vocabulary, give the terminations of foreing term.
