Subject

The Subject (from sub, “under,” and iacio, “throw, place") is the basic element of a sentence performing the action described in the Predicate.

The subject is generally a substantive but other part of the speech can also perfom the function of the subject such as pronouns, adjectives, substativated participles, or entire propositions.

The subject of a propositions is expressed in the nominative case exception made for infinitive propositions in which the subject is rendered in the accusative case.

  • Galbam, Africanum, Laelium doctos esse traditum est. (Cic.) - «It is related that Galbam, Africanum, Laelium were well cultivated men»

If it is a personal pronoun, is generally omitted unless emphatic:

  • Spero - «I hope»

  • Ego spero - «I hope - though no one else does».

Part of the speech functioning as subject Example
Noun

Romulus Romam condĭdit - Romulus founded Rome
Manus manum lavat (Sen.)

Pronoun

Accurrit quidam - A man ran up

Substantival adjective

Semper avarus eget (Horace)- The greedy are always in need

An infinitive with nominal function

Semel in anno licet insanire (Lat.) —“It is allowed once in the year to be foul.””

A genetic part of the speech
(adverb, conjunction, preposition, etc.)

Nunc adverbium est - Nunc is an adverb

An infinitive proposition

Legem brevem esse oportet (Sen.) - A law should be brief

A completive proposition

Romam Nuntiatum est fugisse Antonium (Cic.) - Words was brought to Rome that Antony had fled

Grammatical and Logical subject

It is possible to distinguish between a grammatical and logical subject.

The logical subject is the person or thing of which you desire to state some action or condition, though the word denoting it may grammatically be an object or an attribute; While the logical Subject is the “doer of the action”.

The subject of the predication, does not always correspond to the grammatical subject:

Pars navium haustae sunt. (Tac.) - A part of the ships were drawn

Pars (f sing.) is the grammatical subject, but the predicate haustae sunt agrees with naves, the logical subject.
