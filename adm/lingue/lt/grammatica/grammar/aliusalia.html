Indeterminate pronouns

The indeterminate pronouns are so called because, they do not identify the person or thing to which they are applied.

alius, alia, aliud, «another», «other», is used when there is a distinction among two persons/things; its opposite is idem, «the same»;

Alius is called a pronominal adjective because it has features of the declension of pronouns: It declines as a 1st/2nd declension adjective with the slight irregularity of taking -ius in the genitive singular and -i in the dative singular. It declines as follows:

alter, altĕra, altĕrum

alter, altĕra, altĕrum, «the one...the other (alter...alter) », «the other, the second», «the former, the latter»; the use of alter .. alter implies that just two persons/things are involved:

  • Ex duobus frātrēs, unus sēdulus, alter piger erat - «Of the two brothers the one was diligent, the other was lazy»

or it can also be rendered as:

  • Ex duobus frātrēs, alter sēdulus, alter piger erat - «Of the two brothers the one was diligent, the other was lazy»

alter is also used in expression where two things are present like: altĕro oculo capi; «To become blind of one eye»

alterŭter, alterŭtra, alterŭtrum

alterŭter, alterŭtra, alterŭtrum (pronoun and adjective) is composed by alter and by uter; it means «one of two», «one or the other», «either». It is possible either to decline uter and leave unchanged alter-: gen. alterutrīus, dat. alterŭtri, etc., or to decline both parts, which in this case are written apart: altĕra utra, altĕrum utrum, gen. alterīus utrīus, etc.; It is followed up by the genitive of a pronouns:

  • alterŭter nostrum, «one or the other of us»

when used with a substantive it agrees in case and number:

  • alterŭtra actione, «one or the other» action».
