Predicative Substantives

Substantives can function attributively when used as appositions and predicatively when they complete the meaning of a predicate. A word which is used to complete the meaning of a simple predicate is known as a complement. This occurs particularly with substantives which relate to time and age such as iuvenis, puer, adulescens, senex or an office as impĕrātŏr, consul, praetor, tribunus, quaestor, magister, dux, etc.

When the predicative complement refers back to the subject, it is put in the nominative case; when it refers back to the direct object, it is put in the accusative case.

  • litterās Graecās senex didicī; (Cat.) - I learned Greek when an old man.

  • Consules creantur Caesat et Servilius - Caesar and Servilius are made consuls

  • Vel imperatore, vel milite me utimini (Sall.) - Employ me either as your commander or as your fellow - soldier. Terent.

  • Attalus heredem populum Romanum reliquit. (Eutr.) - Attalus named the Roman people as his heirs

  • Ancum Marcium regem populus creavit (Liv.) - The people made Ancus Marcius king

  • Aristaeus qui olivae dicitur inventor (Cic.) - Aristaeus, who is called the discoverer of the olive
