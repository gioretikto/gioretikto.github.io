Participles

As its name implies, the participle shares (“takes part” or “participates”) in the nature of both a verb and an adjective.

While English has only two participles, the present and the past, Latin has three:

  • present participle (active voice)

  • perfect participle (passive voice)

  • future (active and passive voices supine)

    • English does not have the equivalent of the future participles. The participles can be used either as adjective or as a noun.

    As adjective it qualifies a noun:

    iucunda est memoria praeteritorum malorum (Cic.) - (The memory of past evils is pleasant)

    here praeteritorum as the value of an attributive adjective.

    cum sapienti amico (with a wise friend)

    As a noun:

    Virtus pugnantium magna fuit - (The value of the fighters was great)

    Erranti via monstra - (Show the way to the one who has gone astray)
