Syllables

A syllable consists of one or more elementary sounds uttered in one emission of voice. There are some basic rules for dividing a word into syllables.

  1. A latin word has many syllables as it has vowels and dipthongs.

    a-gi-ta-tus, a-ma-re, ae-tas, poe-na, Cae-sar

  2. In dividing a word into syllables, a consonant after a vowel goes with the following syllable:

    re-cta, ho-mi-nes

  3. When a vowel or dipthongs is followed by two consonants, the first consonant goes with the first syllable, the second with the next syllable:

    cor-tex

  4. A special case of double consonants concerns the combination of a mute (b, p, c, g, d, t) or a f followed by a liquid (l, r). In this case both consonants belong to the same syllable, as in:

    ma-tres

    di-plo-ma

    in-frĕ-quens

  5. If there are three consecutive consonants, the first two belong to the syllable before, and the third to the syllable that follows.

    6. ma-gist-ra

    temp-lum

  6. The groups qu and gu, represent single consonant sounds, /ku/, /gw/, though written as two letters (u is not a stand-alone vowel, but represent a single grapheme with q- and -g);

    • antiquum, "old" is a word of three syllables an-ti-quum.

    • sán-guis, «blood»

  7. In compound words formed by prefixing prepositions, this latter makes a distinct syllable, as in:

    adiuvare, is divided in ad-iu-va-re

    abavus, is divided in ab-a-vus

    ades, ad-est

    ineo, in-e-o

    subigo, sub-igo

    Also suffixes liek -tus, -si and -ti make distinct syllables as in:

    scripsi, scrip-si

    doctus, doc-tus

    hostis, hos-ti-s

    testis, tes-ti-s

  8. The semivowel i is found either as an initial of a word before a vowel e.g. Iuno, Iupiter; or within the word and inter-vowe position, as in eiuro, peiuro. Sometimes it is written with the distinctive sign 'J'. It has an intermediate sound between a vowel and a consonant and for this must not be regarded as a syllable by itself, but it makes a syllable with the following vowel: Iu-no

A syllable ending with a vowel or diphthong is called open; all others are called closed. Thus in ma-ter the first syllable is open, the second closed.

Quantity of Syllables

The quantitaty of a syllable is the time occupied in pronouncing it. The latin syllable can be

  • short, when it contains a short vowel, marked by a ˘; thus ǎmor (love) has the a short.

  • long, double the length of a short one when it contains a long vowel or diphthong; a long vowel has the mark ⁻ over it as in māter;

  • ancipital (being two-headed or two-edged), when it can be either long or short; marked by a - as in mihī̆, tibī̆, sibī̆, ibī̆, ubī̆

The Quantity of the Syllable is important for the position of the accent and in versification.

A syllable is long, by nature (i.e., by the natural length of its vowel) or by position (i.e. when its vowel is followed by two or more consonants). Thus fūr and mā-ter have u and a long by nature. While fāx and amabūnt have a and u long by position (x is a double consonant).

The are some basic rules governing the quantity of a syllable:

  1. A closed syllable is always long- in lēc-tus, lec is long.

  2. A syllable is always long by nature if it has a long vowel or a diphthong. Dipthongs are contracted syllables and are always long.

    aedēs, coelum

  3. One vowel immediately followed by another is short- as rĕus, dĕus, urcĕus, .

  4. A syllable containing a short vowel followed by two consonants (except a mute before l or r) or by a double consonant (x, z) is said to be long by position, but the vowel is pronounced short.

    cǎptus, has a short by nature but the syllable is long by position because the vowel is folowed by two consonants.

    5. fǎctus, has a short by nature but the syllable fac is long by position.

    li-ber-tas, has e short by nature but the syllable ber is long by position

  5. A vowel is shortened before a vowel', vocalis ante vocalem corripitur, as in: audio, "I hear", that becomes audīre, "to listen".

  6. Monosyllabic nouns ending in a consonant are long as: sōl, fūr, iūs, but all the other monosyllabic conjunction and pronouns are short as: ĕt, ut, in, ad, sĕd, quis, quot as well as the enclitic particles: quĕ, vĕ, sĕ, nĕ, tĕ, psĕ, ptĕ.

  7. The vowels of radical syllables retain their natural quantity in derivative and compound words:

    ămor, ămābĭlis, ĭnămābĭlis, ămīcĭtĭa.

    māter, māternus

When a consonant is doubled the pronunciation should show this distinctly. Thus in transmitto both Ts should be pronounced as in out-talk and not merely a single t as in the english letter.
