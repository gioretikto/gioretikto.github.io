Demostrative pronouns and adjective

Demostrative pronouns (dēmōnstro, "I point out) are used to locate or designate a particular thing in space or time. Latin demostrative pronouns are:

hic, haec, hoc, this (near me), points out a thing close to the speaking person

iste, ista, istud, that (near you) points out a thing close to the listener

ille, illa, illud, that (near him) points out a thing far both to the speaking and the listener

Demostrative pronouns can be used both as pronouns and as adjectives; in the nominative case they can also be used as personal pronouns. They are thus declined:

Osservations

  1. The -c of hic derives from the ancient particle -ce which acted as an intensifier; It can be found in ec-ce («here!»), and it can be also found in the forms of hic ending in s as in: huiusce («exaclty this»), hisce, hosce, hascem as well as in the genitive with adverbial value huiuscemodi («Of such a kind or sort»).

  2. Ille was originally olle, of which still exist the alternatives forms of the dative singular olli and ollis (for illi, illis). It is often used to praise a person, that is to say «that famous man», as in

    • Xenophon, Socratius ille, «The Socrates' famous student»

    • Ego ille quem nosti, «That famous me, whom you know»

    The neuter illud can mean «that famous saying», as in:

    • Illud Solonis, «That (famous saying) of Solon's».

  3. Iste is often used in a derogatory or sarcastic sense. As in:

    • ut isti homines volunt?, «How can these individuals demand such things?».

    • isti di mala multa dent clienti,.. , «may the gods damn that clients of yours».

  4. The neuter demostrative pronoun in direct cases can mean «thing», res in latin which is left unexpressed, while in oblique cases res is expressed; As in:

    • Hoc, «this thing».

    • illa, «those things».

    • hac re, «with this thing».

    • harum rerum, «of this thing».
