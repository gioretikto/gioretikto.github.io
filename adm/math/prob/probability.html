Definitions of Probability

There are several ways to define probability. In this section we consider three definitions; namely the axiomatic definition, the relative-frequency definition, and the classical definition.

The Classical Definition of Probability

The classical definition of probability (Laplace, 1812) assumes the equiprobability (equal likelihood) of events, which is regarded as a primitive concept and hence not subject to formal definition. For example, in the throwing of a single perfectly cubical die made of completely homogeneous material, the equally likely events are the appearance of any of the specific number of points (from 1 through 6) marked on its faces, since by virtue of the symmetry no one face has objective preferences over the other.
In the general case, denote by E1, E2, ..., En the n pairwise mutually exclusive, equally likely, elementary events outcomes of the sample space Ω.

  • Mutually exclusive events: Ei ∩ Ej = ∅ for i ≠ j;

  • Equally likely events: none of them is expected to occur in preference to the other, P(Ei) = p for i = 1, ..., n

The classical definition of probability is given in terms of the events of the family Ω and may be formulated as follows:

Definition 2.2.1. Classical probability. If an event A is decomposable into the sum of m events belonging to a complete group of n pairwise mutually exclusive, equally likely and mutually exhaustive events, then the probability P(A) of the event A is equal to

P(A) = m/n = No. of elementary events favourable to A/ Tot. No. of elementary events in Ω □

In other words, it is the ratio of the cardinallity of the event to the cardinality of the sample space. Four observations may be made from the definition

  1. 0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1. This is clear as m and n being positive integers 0 ≤ m ≤ n, we get

    0/n ≤ m/n ≤ n/n or  0 ≤ P(A) ≤ 1.

  2. P(A) + P(Ac) = 1, where Ac = Ω − A denotes the complementary event. This follows from the fact that |A| = m implies |Ac| = n − m. Hence,

    P(Ac) = |Ac|/|Ω| = (n − m)/n = 1 − m/n = 1 − P(A)

  3. P(Ω) = 1 and P(∅) = 0.

  4. If A ∩ B = ∅, then P(A ∪ B) = P(A) + P(B). To see this note that

    |A ∪ B| = |A| + |B| if A ∩ B = ∅

    Hence,

    P(A ∪ B) = |A ∪ B|/|Ω| = (|A| + B)/|Ω| = |A|/|Ω| + |B|/|Ω| = P(A) + P(B).

Example 2.2.2. A die is cast. Find the probability of getting (i) an even number, (ii) and odd number, (iii) a prime numbre, (iv) a number less than 5, (v) a number more than 6, (vi) a positive number.

Solution. Let Ω denote the sample space. Then Ω = {1,2,3,4,5,6}. Let A denote the event of getting an even number, B denote the event of getting an odd number, C denote the event of getting a prime number, D denote the event of getting a number less than 4, E denote a number greater than 6 and F denote the event of getting a positive number.

Then, A = {2,4,6}, B = {1,3,5}, C = {2,3,5}, D = {1,2,3}, E = ∅, F = {1,2,3,4,5,6}. Hence

P(A) = |A|/|Ω| = 3/6 = 1/2
P(B) = |B|/|Ω| = 3/6 = 1/2
P(C) = |C|/|Ω| = 3/6 = 1/2
P(D) = |D|/|Ω| = 3/6 = 1/2
P(E) = |∅|/|Ω| = 0/6 = 0
P(F) = |F|/|Ω| = 6/6 = 1

Note that, the classical definition of probability has a drawback that Ω must be finite. But in real-world problems, Ω may not be finite. Hence, to overcome this, Kolmogorov introduced the axiomatic definition of probability which is stated as follows

Definition 2.2.3. Axiomatic Definition of Probability Let Ω a sample space and ℱ its associated σ-algebra, a real value function P(⋅) with domain ℱ is a probability function if the following axioms are satisfied:

  1. The probability of an event is non-negative, that is P(A) ≥ 0;

  2. The probability of the sure event equals 1, that is, P(Ω) = 1;

  3. If A1, A2, ..., is a countable sequence of mutually exclusive events in ℱ

    P ( ⋃ i = 1 ∞ A i ) = ∑ i = 1 ∞ P ( A i )

    That is, for any set of mutually exclusive events defined on the same space, the probability of at least one of these events occurring is the sum of their respective probabilities.
    When the above property is satisfied for finite sequences, it is called finite addivitty.

Then P is called probability function.

The axiomatic definition of probability reduces to the classical definition of probability when Ω is finite, and each possible outcome is equally likely. From the above definition, one can observe that P is a set function whcih assigns a real number to subsets of Ω. In particular, P is a normalized set function in the sense that P(Ω) = 1. For each subset A of Ω, the number P(A) is called the probability that the outcome of the random experiment is an elemnt of the set A, or the probability of the even A, or the probability measure of the set A.

Classical probability fails if sample space is uncountable and/or events are not equally likely and mutually exclusive.

Relative-Frequency Definition

Consider a random experiment that is performed n times. If an event j occurs nj times, then the probability of event j, pj, is defined as follows:

Definition 2.2.4. Frequentist probability. The probability of an event, pj, is the limit to which the ratio of the number nj of occurences of j to the number n of trials tends, when n goes to +∞:

p j = lim n → ∞ n j n

The ratio nE/n is called the relative frequency of event E. While the relative-frequency definition of probability is intuitively satisfactory for many practical problems, it has a few limitations. One such limitation is the fact that the experiment may not be repeatable (e.g., economic events), especially when we are dealing with destructive testing of expensive and/or scarce resources; the limit of the number of tirals (+∞) is unattainable.
In other words, accoring to the frequentist definition the relative frequency of a certain event is a proxy of its probability (approximation for finite n).

Because 0 ≤ nj ≤ n, pj must satisfy the condition

0 ≤ pj ≤ 1

When pj = 1, we say the event j is a certainty and when pj = 0, we say it is impossible. In addition, because

Σn nj = n

we have the normalization condition,

Σn pj = 1

which means that the probability that some event occurs is a certainty.

Definition 2.2.5. Subjective probability. The probability of an event P(E), is the individual's level of belief about its occurence. This personal judgement relies on opinions and past experience of the individual. □

Sometimes A ∪ B and A ∩ B are denoted by A + B and AB

Andrey Nikolaevich Kolmogorov (Russian: Андре́й Никола́евич Колмого́ров, 1903 – 1987) was a Russian mathematician who made significant contributions to the mathematics of probability theory. It was Kolmogorov that axiomatized probability in his fundamental work, Foundation of the Theory of Probability (Berlin), 1933.
