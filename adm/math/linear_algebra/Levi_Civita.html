The Levi-Civita symbol

While Eq. (10.6) is convenient for working out the cross product of explicit numerical vectors, as in the previous problem, it becomes quite cumbersome when the vectors contain symbolic entries and when multiple products are involved. A more efficient way of writing the cross product is facilitated by the Levi-Cicita symbol εijk in three dimensions

ε i j k = { + 1 if  ( i , j , k , ) = ( 1 , 2 , 3 ) , ( 2 , 3 , 1 ) , ( 3 , 1 , 2 ) − 1 if  ( i , j , k ) = ( 2 , 1 , 3 ) , ( 3 , 2 , 1 ) , ( 1 , 3 , 2 ) 0 otherwise  (1.12.1)

Here εijk = +1 for even permutations of 123, and εijk = −1 for odd permutations of 123; so an even exchanges of the indices such as ε231 = ε312 = 1, while ε213 = ε132 = −1. A few familiar examples of its use are listed below

  • The determinanta of a 3 x 3 matrix may be defined using the Levi-Civita symbol εijk as the product,

    εijk det A = εabc AaiAbjAck

  • Using this symbol, the cross product can be written, using Einstein summation convention as

    (v × w)i = εijk vj wk  (1.12.2)

    Thus we have the familiar component expression of the cross-product

    (v × w)1 = ε1jk vjwk = v2w3 − v3w2
    (v × w)2 = ε2jk vjwk = v3w1 − v1w3
    (v × w)3 = ε3jk vjwk = v1w2 − v2w1

    Example: The cross product of a = (2,3,4) and b = (5,6,7)

    • c1 = a2b3 − a3b2 = 3×7 − 4×6 = −3.

    • c2 = a3b1 − a1b3 = 4×5 − 2×7 = 6.

    • c3 = a1b2 − a2b1 = 2×6 − 3×5 = −3.

  • The Levi-Civita symbol εijk is related to the Kronecker delta δij by the identities,

    ε i j k ε l m n = | δ i l δ i m δ i n δ j l δ j m δ j n δ k l δ k m δ k n | = δ i l ( δ j m δ k n − δ j n δ k m ) − δ i m ( δ j l δ k n − δ j n δ k l ) + δ i n ( δ j l δ k m − δ j m δ k l ) .

    A special case of this result is

    εijkεimn = δjm δkn − δjn δkm  (1.12.3)

    Note the use of Einstein's summation convention,

    ∑ i = 1 3 ε i j k ε i m n = δ j m δ k n − δ j n δ k m

    The Kronecker and Levi-Cività symbols simplify the proof of vector identities. An example is the following known as bac-cab rule.

    a × (b × c) = b(a · c) − c(a · b) (1.12.4)

    Using eq (1.12.2), the ith component of a × (b × c) is

    [a × (b × c)]i = εijk aj × (b × c)k = εijk aj εklm blcm

    The definition (1.12.1) tells us that εijk = εklm. Therefore, the identity (1.12.3) gives

    [a × (b × c)]i = εijk aj × (b × c)k = (δil − εjm − δim − εjl) ajblcm = ajbicj − ajbjci

    The final result, bi(a ⋅ c) − ci(a ⋅ b), is indeed the ith component of the right side of (1.12.4).

    Example 1. Prove that ∇ · (∇ × g) = 0.

    Solution: Begin with ∇ · (∇ × g) = ∂i εijk ∂j gk = ½ ∂i∂jgkεijk + ½ ∂i∂jgkεijk.

    Which is equal to

    ∇ · (∇ × g) = ½Σᵢⱼₖ∂ⱼ∂ᵢgₖϵᵢⱼₖ + ½Σᵢⱼₖ∂ⱼ∂ᵢgₖϵᵢⱼₖ

    Exchanging the dummy indices i and j in the last term, does not change the sum; An it gives

    ∇ · (∇ × g) = ½Σᵢⱼₖ∂ⱼ∂ᵢgₖϵᵢⱼₖ + ½Σⱼᵢₖ∂ᵢ∂ⱼgₖϵⱼᵢₖ

    ∇ · ∇ × g = ½ ∂i∂jgkεijk + ½ ∂j∂igkεjik = ½ {εijk + εjik} ∂i∂jgk = 0

    The final zero comes from εijk = −εjik, which is a consequence of (1.12.1).  □
