Projection of a vector onto a subspace

In real three-dimensional Euclidean space, one projects a vector on a plane or a line by "dropping a perpendicular" from the tip of the vector; e.g. a computer graphics software that renders 3D objects must project the polygons that make up a 3D object onto a 2D rendering plane.
To generalize the conept of projection onto a 2D plane to abstract vector spaces, suppose that V1 is an m-dimensional subspace of an n-dimensional vector space V. We want to determine the vector u ∈ V1 that has the minimum distance to v ∈ V.

Theorem (Unique minimum-distance projection) Let V an inner product vector space and V1 an m-dimensional subspace of V. Let v ∈ V such that v ∉ V1. Then there exist and are uniquely determined the vectors u, u⊥, such that

v = u + u⊥,   u ∈ V1, u⊥ ∈ V1⊥ (orthogonal complement)

The vector u⊥ is said orthogonal projection of v onto V1 and among all vectors of V1 is the minimum-distance vector from v.

Proof. By the corollary 2.6.2 of the Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization theorem, there exists an orthonormal basis e1, ... , en ∈ V, such that the first m vectors are an orthonormal basis of V1 and the last n − m vectors are an orthonormal basis of V1⊥. We write u as a linear combination of this basis

v = ∑ i = 1 n c i e i = ∑ i = 1 m c i e i + ∑ i = m + 1 n c i e i ≡ u + u ⊥

By definition of u ∈ V1 and u⊥ ∈ V1⊥ we show that u is the unique minimum distance vector of V1 from the given vector v. Let u1 another vector of V1. We have that

||v −u|| = dist(u, v) = ||u⊥||
||v −u1|| = ||u⊥|| + ||u −u1||

Since u −u1 ∈ V1 and u⊥ ∈ V1⊥ by Pythagorean theorem

dist2(v,u1) = ||v −u1|| 2 = ||u⊥||2 + ||u −u1||2 ≥ ||u⊥||2 = dist2(v,u)

Thus

dist2(v,u1) ≥ dist2(v,u) □

Notice how to find the element u ∈ V1, which best approximates v, it suffices to know an orthonormal basis of V1, as it can be read out directly from the proof

u = ∑ i = 1 m c i e i = ∑ i = 1 m ( v ⋅ e i ) e i
