Components of a Vector

When we represent a vector by an arrow in space, a coordinate system is not needed. The graphical addition of two vectors, for instance, is independent of any coordinate system. Adding vectors geometrically can be tedious, so we will now develop a new vector representation or descritpion that requires the use of a coordinate system, that will permit us to add, subtract, and later multiply vectors analytically, i.e. algebrically. We make use of the projections of a vector along the axes of a rectangular coordinate system. These projections are called components. Any vector can be completely described by its components.
Consider a vector A in a rectangular coordinate system, as shown in Figure 1. A can be expressed algebrically as the sum of two vectors: Ax, parallel to the x-axis, and Ay, parallel to the y-axis. Mathematically

A = Ax + Ay

where Ax and Ay, are the component vectors of A.

The projection of A along the x-axis, Ax, is called the x-component of A, and the projection of A along y-axis, Ay, is called the y-component of A. These components can be either positive or negative numbers with units. From the definition of sine and cosine, we see that

Ax = A cos θ
Ay = A sin θ

Direction cosine

Vectors in 3D space (as 2D) are resolved using directions cosines. The direction of the vector is specified by the direction cosines of a (directed) line which are the cosines of the angles which the line makes with the positive directions of the coordinate axes. For example another way to resolve a 2D vector v, into its components (v1, v2), using direction cosines is as follows. We use trigonometry as usual to write the components

v1 = v cos θ1
v2 = v cos θ2

θ1 and θ2 are known as direction angles, their cosines, cos θ1 and cos θ2 the direction cosines. Note that

|v|2 = (cos θ1)2 + (cos θ2)2

This equations shows that the angles are not independent because knowking one of them determines the second.
