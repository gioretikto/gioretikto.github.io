Eucliedean Spaces

Most of topology particularly manifold theory, is modeled on the behaviour of Euclidean spaces and their subsets, so we begin with a quic review of their properties.
The Cartesian product ℝn = ℝ x ... x ℝ of n copies of ℝ is known as n-dimensional Euclidean space. It is the set of all ordered n-tuples of real numbers. An element of ℝn is denoted by (x1, ..., xn) or simply x. The numners xi are called its components or coordinates. Zero-dimensional Euclidean space ℝ0 is, bt convention the singleton {0}.
We use without further comment the fact that ℝn is an n-dimensional real vector space with the usual operations of scalar multiplication and vector addition. We refet to an element of ℝn either as a point or as a vector, depending on whether we wish to emphasize its location or its direction and magnitude. The geometric properties of ℝn are derived from the Euclidean dot product, defined by

x ⋅ y = x1y1 + ... + xnyn

In particular, the norm or length of a vector x ∈ ℝn is given by

|x| = (x ⋅ x)1/2 = (x12 + ... + xn2) 1/2

if x and y are nonzero vectors in ℝn, the angle between x and y is defined to be cos−1 (x ⋅ y) / (|x| |y|). Given two points x,y ∈ ℝn, the line segment from x to y is the set {x + t(y − x) : 0 ≤ t ≤ 1} and the distance between x and y is |x − y|.
