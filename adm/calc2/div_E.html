The differential form of Gauss’s laws: The divergence of the electrostatic field

By the divergence theorem:

∭ V ( ∇ → ⋅ E → ) d V = ∫ ∂ V E → ⋅ d S →

where

  • V is the volume;

  • ∂V est la frontière de V;

  • dS est le vecteur normal à la surface, dirigé vers l'extérieur et de norme égale à l'élément de surface qu'il représente

  • E est continûment dérivable en tout point de V;

  • ∇ est l'opérateur nabl; ∇ ⋅ F → = div F (valable uniquement en coordonnées cartésiennes).

    Gauss’s law, based on this theorem can be written as

    Φ ( E → ) = ∭ V ( ∇ → ⋅ E → ) d V = 1 ε 0 ∫ V ρ d V

    where ρ is the density of charge within the volume V, hence dq = ρ dV. Since this equality must hold for all volumes, the integrands must be equal. Thus,

    ∇ → ⋅ E → = ρ ε 0

    which is the differential form of Gauss’s law for electric fields. The divergence of E, is defined as scalar product of the vector nabla, whihc in cartesian coordinates {x,y, z} par rapport à une base {i, j, k} of the Euclidean space, can be written as

    ∇ → = ∂ ∂ x i + ∂ ∂ y j + ∂ ∂ z k

    be the electrostatic field E, thus a scalar quantity:.

    ∇ ⋅ E → = div E → = ∂ E x ∂ x + ∂ E y ∂ y + ∂ E z ∂ z

    Thus we have

    ∇ ⋅ E = ρ(x,y,z)/ε

    This is a local formulation of Gauss's Law for the electrostatic field. Recall that electric field lines originate on positive charge and terminate on negative charge, and it is only at the location of those charges that the divergence of the electrostatic field is non-zero. At a point where the divergence is non-zero the vector field has a (scalar) source or sink.

    By stubstituting the electri potential V, we obtain

    ∇ ⋅ ∇ V = ∇ 2 V = ∂ 2 V ∂ x 2 + ∂ 2 V ∂ y 2 + ∂ V ∂ z 2 = − ρ ε 0

    this is known as Poisson Equation and the scalar operato ∇2 = ∇ ⋅ ∇ is known as Laplacian operator. In empty space ρ = 0, thus

    ∇ 2 V = ∂ 2 V ∂ x 2 + ∂ 2 V ∂ y 2 + ∂ V ∂ z 2 = − ρ ε 0 = 0

    which is known as Laplace equation.
