Ionic Bonding

When atoms with nearlyu the same electronegativity form bonds, the molecular orbitals are spread more or less evenly over the two atoms, and covalent bonds are formed. When atoms have somewhat different electronegativities, the bonding electrons are not evenly shared, but are drawn towards the more electronegativite atom. When the electronegativities are quite different, an electron moves completely to the more electronegative atom and an ionic bond is formed. An ionic compound results when a metal reacts with a nonmetal.
The property of ionic compounds that they conduct electricity when dissolved in water or on melting. When solid sodium chloride is melted, it conducts electricity, a fact that convinces us that sodium chloride contains Na+ and Cl− ions. On the other hand Methane and dissolves in water to a slight extent, but it does not ionize. Thus Methane molecules are formed by covalent, not ionic, bonding. Pure water does not conduct electricity because it contains virtually no ions.

The electronegativity of Na is 0.9 and that of chlorine is 3.0, hence the difference is 2.1 which facilitates the transfer of an electron from sodium to chlorine so that when they react they to form Na+ and Cl− ions, which then aggregate to form a close packed ionic crystal structure. Let's consider the energetics of the formation of NaCl. The Na atom has a small ionization energy of about 5 eV and a small electron affinity of about of 0.5 eV. Therefore it easily lose an electron to form Na. The chlorine atom has a large ionization energy of more thatn 10 eV and a large electron affinity of almost 4 eV. Thus it does not easily lose an electron, rather it easily can gain an electron

Na(g) ⟶ Na+ (g) + e−   EI = +495 kJ/mol

Cl(g) + e− ⟶ Cl− (g)   AE = −349 kJ/mol

Na(g) + Cl(g) ⟶ Na+(g) + Cl− (g)   ΔE1 = 146 kJ/mol

Notice that forming gaseuos sodium and cloride ion from gaseous sodium and chlorine atoms increases the potential energy of the system: in order to transfer one electron from a dosium atom to a chlorine atom an energy expenditure of 145 kj/mol (1.45 eV) is required. This tells us that if the IE and EA were the only energy changes involved, ionic sodium chloride would not form from atoms of sodium and chlorine. So where does the stability of the compound come from?
In the calculation above, we looked at the formation of gasesous ions, but salt is not a gas; it's a solid. Therefore, we need to see how the energy would change if the gaseous ions are condensed to give the solid, and to do this we must examine a quantity called the lattice energy.
The lattice energy is the energy released when ions are brought together from an infinite separation (i.e. a cloud of gaseous) to form one mole of the solid compound. For sodium chloride, the change associated with the lattice energy is pictured in Fig.1, and in equation form can be represented as

Na+ (g) + Cl− (g) ⟶ NaCl (s)

Because Na+ and Cl− ions attract each other, their potential energy decreases as they come together, so the process releases energy and is exothermic. In fact, the lattice energy for sodium chloride is very exothermic and is equal to −786 kJ mol−1.
If we include the lattice energy along with the ionization energy of Na and electron affinity of Cl, we have

Na(g) ⟶ Na+ (g) + e−   +495 kJ/mol (EI)

Cl(g) + e− ⟶ Cl− (g)   −349 kJ/mo (AE)
Na(g) + Cl(g) ⟶ NaCl (s)   −786 kJ/mol (lattice energy)

Net −640 kJ

Thus, the release of energy equal to the lattice energy provides a large net lowering of the potential energy as solid NaCl is formed. We can also say that is the lattice energy that provides the stabilization necessary for the formation of NaCl. Without it, the compound could not exist.

Determining Lattice Energies from the Born–Haber Cycle

In realtà quello che ci interessa non è il processo ipotetico di formazione di specie gassose di NaCl a partire da atomi isolati, ma la formazione di cristalli di cloruro di sodio. Ossia

Na(s) + 1/2 Cl2 (g) ⟶ NaCl(s)

The energy released when a crystalline solid forms from ions is known as the lattice energy of the solid.

Na+ (g) + Cl– (g) ⟶ NaCl(s)

The same change in energy occurs when an ionic solid is separated into isolated ions in the gas phase: The distances between ions in the crystal are continuously enlarged until the ions are very far apart.

Direct experimental determination of the lattice energy of an ionic solid is difficult. However, this quantity can be indirectly determined from experiment by means of a thermochemical “cycle” originated by Max Born and Fritz Haber in 1919 and now called the Born–Haber cycle. The reasoning is based on Hess’s law.

Possiamo ipotizzare la reazione globale come il risultato di una serie di processi parziali

  1. Sublimation of sodium. Metallic sodium is vaporized to a gas of sodium atoms. (Sublimation is the transformation of a solid to a gas.) The enthalpy change for this process, measured experimentally, is 108 kJ per mole of sodium.

    Na(s) ⟶ Na(g)   ΔE1 = +109 kJ ⋅ mol–1

  2. Dissociation of chlorine. Chlorine molecules are dissociated to atoms. The enthalpy change for this equals the Cl––Cl bond dissociation energy, which is 240 kJ per mole of bonds, or 122 kJ per mole of Cl atoms.

    1/2 Cl2 (g) ⟶ Cl (g)   ΔE2 = +122 kJ ⋅ mol–1

  3. Ionization of sodium. Sodium atoms are ionized to Na+ ions. The enthalpy change is essentially the ionization energy of atomic sodium, which equals 494 kJ per mole of Na.

    Na(g) ⟶ Na+(g) + e−   ΔE3 = +494 kJ ⋅ mol–1

  4. Formation of chloride ion. The electrons from the ionization of sodium atoms are transferred to chlorine atoms. The enthalpy change for this is the electron affinity of atomic chlorine, which equals −349 kJ per mole of Cl atoms.

    Cl (g) + e– ⟶ Cl– (g)   ΔE4 = –349 kJ ⋅ mol–1

  5. Formation of NaCl(s) from ions. The ions Na+ and Cl– formed in Steps 3 and 4 combine to give solid sodium chloride. Because this process is just the reverse of the one corresponding to the lattice energy (breaking the solid into ions), the enthalpy change is the negative of the lattice energy. If we let U be the lattice energy, the enthalpy change for Step 5 is –U.

    Na (g) + Cl (g) ⟶ NaCl (s)   ΔE5 = –787 kJ ⋅ mol–1

Let us write these five steps and add them. We also add the corresponding enthalpy changes, following Hess’s law.

ΔHf° = ΔH1 + ΔH2 + ΔH3 + ΔH4 –U = 375 –U

Adding the enthalpy changes, we find that the enthalpy change for this formation reaction is 375 kJ – U. But the enthalpy of formation has been determined calorimetrically and equals –411 kJ. Equating these two values, we get 375 kJ – U = –411 kJ. Solving for U yields the lattice energy of NaCl: U = (375 + 411) kJ = 786 kJ.

In definitiva nella reazione tra sodio solido e cloro gassoso vengono rilasciati 411 kJ/mol di energia per cui il processo comporta una notevole diminuzione di energia. La formazione di un legame ionico avviene soltanto quando il valore dell'energia reticolare è maggiore dell'energia richiersta per formare gli ioni che costituiranno il reticolo cristallino. Questa condizione si realizza quando si uniscono elementi che possiedono bassi valori di energia di ionizzazione ed elementi caratterizzati da elevata affinità elettronica. La maggior parte dei composti ionici deriva quindi dall'unione degli elementi dei gruppi IA, II A, III B o delle serie di transizione, con elementi dei gruppi VI B e VII B.
