Autoionization of water

Prima di considerare gli equilibri ionici in soluzione acquosa, tramite la teoria di Bronsted-Lowry, è necessario introdurre l'equilibrio di autoionizzazione dell'acqua (detto anche autoprotolitico). Questo equilibrio fu scoperto da Friedrich Kohlraush, notando che anche quando l'acqua viene purificata sino a raggiungere un elevato grado di purezza, questa risulta ancora conduttrice di elettricità, anche se scarsamente. Experiments have shown that water undergoes autoionization, in which one molecule of water functions as a Brønsted-Lowry acid and a second molecule functions as a Brønsted-Lowry base, giving the following equilibrium

H2O(ℓ) + H2O(ℓ)  ⇌  H3O+(aq) + OH−(aq)

For which the equilibrium constant is

K = [ OH − ] [ H 3 O + ] [ H 2 O ] 2

from the equilibrium the amphoteric nature of water emerges.

Water concentration can be considered constant at contant temperature and pressure knowing its density of 1 g/ml, its molar value is

[ H 2 O ] = 10 3 g ⋅ l − 1 18 g ⋅ mol − 1 = 55 M

tramite misure di conducibilità elettrolitica risulta that such constant is 3.35 x 10-18 at 25°C; Its low value indicates that the equilibrium is shifted towards water. Being water concentration constant we can inglobate it inside K:

Kw = K ⋅ [H2O] = [H3O+] [OH−]

the product Kw = K ⋅ [H2O] is known as the ion product constant for water, its value at 25° C is equal to 10−14; Indeed we have:

Kw = 3.25 ⋅ 10−18 ⋅ 55.52 = [H3O+] [OH−] = 10−14

It is important to recognize the meaning of Kw: just as a Kc for any reaction is independent on the concentrations of chemical species in the solution, but can vary with temperature, so too does Kw which doesn't vary with the concentrations of [H+] and [OH−] in the solution. In all aqueous solution at 25°C (not just pure water), no matter what they contain (which other ions may be present), the product of [H3O+] and [OH−] must always equal 1.0 x 10−14. As [H3O+ goes up, [OH−] goes down, in the same proportion. There are three possible situations:

  1. A neutral solution, where [H+] = [OH−]

  2. An acidic solution, where [H+] > [OH−]

  3. A basic solution, where [H+] < [OH−]

Pure water is neutral because the concentrations of [H3O+] and [OH−] are equal. Knowing this, we can easily compute the value of each ion from the value and definition of Kw

[H3O+] = [OH−]
Kw = [H3O+] [OH−] = [H3O+]2 = 10−14
[H3O+] = 10−7 M

Thus pure water (at 25 °C) has a concentration of [H3O+] = [OH−] = 10−7 M.

The autoionization of water is an endotermic process, and according to Le Chatelier principle, Kw increases with increasing temperature. The condition of neutrality is always satisfied but the concentrations of [H3O+] anf [OH−] vary.

The pH scale

Because [H+] in an aqueous solution is typically quite small, the pH scale provides a convenient way to represent solution acidity as a number between 0 and 14. The pH of a solution is the negative of the base-10 logarithm of the hydronium ion concentration.

pH = −log [H+]

Thus for a neutral solution with a concentration of [H+] = 10−7 M, has a pH = 7.

A similar scale pOH is defined as the negative logarithm of the hydroxide ions [OH−]:

pOH = −log [OH−]

From

Kw = [H+][OH−] = 1.0 x 10−14

Taking the negative logarithms of both sides of the expression of Kw

pKw = pH + pOH = 14   at 25 °C.

pH indicators

Some natural and commercially prepared indicators indicators are made up os several colorful organic molecules, and they change color throughout the pH range. Notable among the natural indicators are the anthocyanins, which are responsible for most of the different colors found in vegetables and flowers. There are over 150 naturally occuring anthocyanins in foods such as the red cabbage (cavolo rosso), that we cook and serve with dinner. The juice from the red cabbage can therefore be used as an indicator. Figure 1 shows the wide range of colors that can be obtained by adjusting the pH of red cabbage juice.

Commercially prepared solutions of mixtures of indicators can mimic these color changes, but far more intensely, so far less is required. For instance, the commercially prepared "universal indicator" is a mixture of blue, methyl red, bromthymol blue and phenolpthalein.
