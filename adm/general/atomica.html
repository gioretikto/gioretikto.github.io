Teoria atomica

Nella seconda metà del XVII secolo G.E. Stahl (1659-1734) propose l’esistenza di un principio materiale combustibile chiamato flogisto (dal greco, φλoξ, fiamma) in grado di spiegare in maniera coerente ed unitaria i processi di combustione e di calcinazione. La calcinazione è un processo pirometallurgico che prevede il riscaldamento dei materiali ad alte temperature al di sotto del loro punto di fusione, in genere in assenza o con un apporto limitato di aria o ossigeno. Gli scopi principali includono:

  • Rimuovere le impurità volatili (ad esempio, anidride carbonica, acqua o composti organici);

  • Induzione della decomposizione termica;

  • Realizzare transizioni di fase o produrre materiali facilmente polverizzabili.

Ad es. nella cottura del calcare (prescindendo per ora dalle impurità che esso può contenere e che possono superare anche il 10 e il 15%) avviene la reazione:

CaCO3 ⇄ CaO + CO2

Si ha cioè eliminazione di anidride carbonica con perdita del 44% circa in peso, il calcare si contrae da 1/10 fino ad 1/5 del proprio volume, e resta l'ossido di calcio chiamato calce viva.

L'idea che la complessità della Natura sia riducibile a poche sostanze elementari è stata sviluppata fin dall'antichità. Secondo Empedocle (ca. 483-423 a.C.) gli elementi fondamentali erano quattro: aria, fuoco, acqua e terra. Il concetto che la materia sia discontinua, cioè che non sia suddivisibile all'infinito in parti sempre più piccole, si può far risalire al filosofo ionico Leucippo (seconda metà del V seconolo a.C.). L'idea che tutte le cose siano costituite dall'aggregazione di particelle indivisibili, dette atomi, fu successivamente sviluppata da Democrito (ca. 460 - 370 a.C.), alliveo di Leucippo e poi rielaborata da Epicuro (341-270 a.C.). Secondo quest'ultimo la materia, anche quella che costituisce l'uomo, è formata da atomi che hanno forme di ogni genere e sono in eterno movimento; urtandosi, gli atomi possono unirsi in modi diversi, dando così origine a tutte le forme possibili della realtà.
Il poeta latino Lucrezio (ca. 98-95 a.C.) nel poema filosofico De rerum natura ha esposto in dettaglio la dottrina atomistica di Epicuro sulal natura del mondo e degli uomini. Questo poema, riscoperto nel Rinascimento, ebbe grande influenza sul pensiero scientifico e nel Seicento si affermò la convinzione che la materia fosse realmente di natura corpuscolare, al contrario di quanto si era comunemente creduto fino ad allora, seguendo le idee di Aristotele.

Legge di conservazione della massa

The first practical atomic theory was put forward by the English chemist and physicist John Dalton during the years 1803 to 1808. The background for this theory was the law of conservation of mass and the law of constant proportions. The first of these laws had been formulates by the French chemist Antoine Laurent Laivoisier in 1789. It was based on the exact process of weighing and states that there is no measurable change in mass during a chemical reaction.
Fu uno dei primi ad applicare il metodo scientifico sperimentale in maniera rigorosa alla chimica fu Antoine Lavoisier, che per questo è riconosciuto come il "padre" della chimica moderna. Si prefisso come regola di investigazione scientifica quella di misurare il peso di ogni sostanza prima e dopo averla sottoposta ad una reazione chimica. Così nel 1774 scoprì che la massa di (contentitore vetro + stagno + aria) prima del suo riscaldamento (calcinazione) e dopo quando si ottiene (contentitore vetro + calce di stagno + aria) erano le stesse. Riconobbe inoltre che l'aumento di peso dello stagno era dovuto alla combinazione dello stagno con porzioni di aria, per dare il composto oggi noto come ossido di stagno.
È con esperimenti, condotti in recipienti chiusi che Lavoisier fu in grado di confermare due dei principi basilari della chimica:

  1. il principio di conservazione del peso (della massa);

  2. la combustione e la calcinazione dei metalli sono due fenomeni che avvengono per combinazione con l’aria.

Scoperta dell'ossigeno

Nel 1774, lo scienziato inglese Joseph Priestley (1733-1804) isolò l'ossigeno, riscaldando dell'ossido di mercurio, ma non si rese subito conto di aver isolato un elemento. Menzionò la scopeta a Lavoisier nell'ottobre 1774, il quale applicando il suo metodo sperimentale heated mercury in a sealed flask that contained air. After several days, a red substance, an oxide of mercury, was produced. The gas remaining in the flask was reduced in mass and could no longer support life or combustion; a candle was extinguished by it, and animals suffocated when forced to breathe it. We now know that this residual gas was nitrogen, and that the oxygen in the air had reacted with the mercury. The second law was enunciated by the Frenchman Joseph Louis Proust (1754 - 1826) an active professor of chemistry in Madrid. By the end of the 18th century there was considerable debate as to whether the ratio of elements in a compound was dependent on the method by which the compountd was prepared. A controversy arose between two schools of thought, led by a pair of French chemists, Caluse Berthollet and Joseph Proust. Berthollet believed that the proportions (by mass) of the elements in a particular compound were not fixed, byt could actually vary over a certain range. Water, for example, might contain more or less than 11.1% hydrogen by mass, the generally accepted value. Proust disagreed, arguing that any apparent variation was due to impurities and experimental errors. Much of this debate was due to inaccurate determinations of atomic weight. Proust noticed that copper carbonate — be it native or prepared in a lab — always broke down into the same proportions of copper, carbon, and oxygen by mass. There were about five parts of copper to four parts of oxygen to one part carbon − a constant ration of 5.3:4:1. Proust's law of definite proportions was published in 1794:

In a given chemical compound, the proportions by mass of the elemens that compose it are fixed, independent of the origin of the compound or its mode of preparation.

All samples of a compound have the same composition. Its elements are all present in distinct proportions by weight, regardless of how the compound is prepared.

Pure sodium chloride contains 60.66% chlorine by mass, whether we obtain it from salt mines, crystallize it from waters of the oceans or inland salt seas, or synthesize it from its elements, sodium and chlorine. Another example, pure water obtained from different source such as, sea, river or an industrial process always contains hydrogen and oxygen combined together in the ratio 1:8 by mass. Similarly carbon dioxide contains a carbon and oxygen in the ration of 3:8 by mass.

Although all gaseous compounds obey Proust's law, certain solids have compositions that vary over small ranges; such a such a solid is called a nonstochiometric compound. An example is wüstite, which has the nominal chemical formula FeO (with 77.73% iron by mass), but the composition of which, in fact, ranges continuously from Fe0.95O (with 76.8% iron) down to Fe0.85O (74.8% iron), depending on the method of preparation. Such compounds are called berthollides, in honor of Bethollet. We now know, on the atomic level, why they are nonstoichiometric.

1803: Dalton's law of multiple proportions

In 1803, English chemist John Dalton (1766 - 1844) proposed his atomic theory of matter. It both dependend on and explained Proust's law of constant proportions. Dalton't theory held that each chemical element is composed of its own unique atoms, and that each atom has the same specific, relative weight. His theory therefore explains why a fixed weight of a substance always combines with a fixes weight of another substance when forming a compound.
Dalton's theory explained many of the leftover anomalies established earlier by Berthollet and unexplained by Proust's law alone. Dalton found that when two (or more) elements combined in different proportiona under different conditions, the results could be multiple compounds; e.g. when he combined 3 parts of carbon and 8 parts oxygen (strictly by weight), he got carbon dioxide. But when he mixed 3 parts of carbon with 4 parts oxygen, he got carbon monoxide. Dalton also noticed that all the combinations were in the form of small whole numbers.
Dalton had therefore discovered what was to become one of the basic laws of chemistry, the law of multiple proportions.

law of multiple proportions, statement that when two elements combine with each other to form more than one compound, the weights of one element that combine with a fixed weight of the other are in a ratio of small whole numbers, e.g., there are five distinct oxides of nitrogen, and the weights of oxygen in combination with 14 grams of nitrogen are, in increasing order, 8, 16, 24, 32, and 40 grams, or in a ratio of 1, 2, 3, 4, 5. The law was announced (1803) by the English chemist John Dalton, and its confirmation for a wide range of compounds served as the most powerful argument in support of Dalton’s theory that matter consists of indivisible atoms.

Dalton knew that under different conditions, some elements combine with others in more than one way. For example, carbon and oxygen can combine to form two different gases, containing different relative amounts of carbon and oxygen, as shown by the following data:

Mass of Oxygen That Combines
with 1 g of Carbon
Compound I 1.33 g
Compound II 2.66 g

Note that the ratio of the amounts of oxygen in the two compounds is 2 to 1 (2.6642 / 1.3321), which indicates that there must be twice as much oxygen in the second compound, hence we can deduce that Compound I might be CO and Compound II might be CO2.

Dalton realized that when element combine with each other in serval different ways, the law of definite proportions still holds true. It is just that several different proportions are sometimes possible. Dalton called this the law of multiple proportions:

When two elements form more than one compound, the masses of one element in these compounds for a fixed mass of the other element are in ratios of small whole numbers.

As another example we consider nitrogen, which forms different compounds with oxygen, as illustrated by the following table

Mass of Nitrogen That Combines
with 1 g of Oxygen
Compound I 1.750 g
Compound II 0.8750 g
Compound III 0.4375 g

which yield the following ratios:

Compound I/ Compound II = 1.750/0.8750 = 2/1
Compound II/ Compound III = 0.8750 / 0.4375 = 2/1
Compound I/ CompoundIII = 1.750 / 0.4375 = 4/1

From which we can deduce that compound I contains twice as much nitrogen (N) per gram of oxygen (O) as does compound II and that compound II contains twice as much nitrogen per gram of oxygen as does compound III. II contains twice as much nitrogen per gram of oxygen as does compound III. In terms of the numbers of atoms combining, these data can be explained by any of the following sets of formulas:

Compound I N2O NO N4O2
Compound II NO or NO2 or N2O2
Compound III NO2 NO4 N2O4

Since an infinite number of other possibilities exists, Dalton could not deduce absolute formulas from the available data on relative masses. However, the data on the composition of compounds in terms of the relative masses of the elements supported his hypothesis that each element consisted of a certain type of atom and that compounds were formed from specific combinations of atoms.

Dalton’s Atomic Theory

In 1808, Dalton publishes A new System of Chemical Philosophy, in which he presented the first modern atomic theory. Although it was Proust who first stated the law of definite proportions, it was the English chemist John Dalton who discovered what the law really meant. Elements always combine in definite proportions because each element is made up of indivisible particles, with a definite size and mass, which Dalton called atoms. Atoms are the smallest possible particles of each element.

Dalton's atomic theory was bades on the following postulates

  1. Each element is made up of tiny indivisible particles called atoms.

  2. The atoms of a given element are identical; the atoms of different elements are different in some fundamental way or ways.

  3. Chemical compounds are formed when atoms combine with one another. A given compound always has the same relative numbers and types of atoms.

  4. Chemical reactions involve reorganization of the atoms—changes in the way they are bound together. The atoms themselves are not changed in a chemical reaction.

In order to represent chemical reactions as combinations of atoms, Dalton affirmed a “principle of greatest simplicity. This assumption led him to assign the formula AB to the single compound formed by A and B, the formulas CD and either C2D or CD2 to the two compounds formed by C and D, and so on. Application of this principle led to assigning the formula HO to water (believed at the time to be the only compound of hydrogen and oxygen).
In Dalton’s time, water was known to be composed of the elements hydrogen and oxygen, with 8 grams of oxygen present for every 1 gram of hydrogen. If the formula for water were OH, an oxygen atom would have to have eight times the mass of a hydrogen atom. However, the same ratio by mass can be obtained considering the possibility for water to be H2O, (two atoms of hydrogen for every oxygen atom), this would mean that each atom of oxygen is 16 times as heavy as each atom of hydrogen.
Following his simplicity principle, given the known 1: 8 weight ratio in water, the formula for water should be OH, with the consequant assignment of an atomic weight of 8 to oxygen, relative to hydrogen at 1. This assignment was puzzling. Clearly water (HO) must be heavier than oxygen (O), but it was known that the density of water vapor is less than the density of oxygen.

As another example a fixed massa of carbon, e.g. 100 g., possono reagire con 133 g di ossigeno per produrre un ossido e con 266 grammi di ossigeno per produrne un altro. Il rapporto tra la massa di ossigeno che reagisce con 100 grammi di carbonio è 266:133 = 2:1, un rapporto tra piccoli numeri interi. Dalton interpretò questo risultato con la sua teoria atomica proponendo correttamente (questa volta) che i due ossidi hanno uno e due atomi di ossigeno per ogni atomo di carbonio. Esprimendo in formule chimiche si ha: CO (monossido di carbonio) e CO2 (anidride carbonica).

Sebbene Dalton avesse chiarito molti aspetti della reattività chimica, rimanevano ancora delle ambiguità. Dalton credeva che le molecole da lui chiamate "atomi composti", potessero formarsi esclusivamente tra atomi di elementi diversi escludendo la possibilità come oggi sappiamo che elementi uguali possano unirsi a formare molecole.

L'esempio precedente in cui Dalton riesce a ricavare le formule chimiche degli ossidi del carbonio è un caso fortuito. Dalton considerava infatti la formula dell'acqua come HO dal fatto che il rapporto tra le masse di idrogeno e ossigeno è 1/8. Ponendo il peso atomico dell'idrogeno uguale ad 1, concludeva erroneamente che quello dell'ossigeno fosse 8.

Tramite la legge delle proporzioni multiple non si possono dedurre i pesi atomici. Si possono ottenere solo rapporti ponderali e in base ad essi si possono ricavare pesi che possono essere multipli o sottomultipli dei pesi atomici.

A further difficulty for Dalton was the volume relation in the reaction that produces carbon monoxide

C + oxygen (1 volume) = CO (2 volumes).

It seemed to Dalton that equal volumes of gases may contain unequal numbers of particles. In the above reaction, there must be twice as many oxygen particles as carbon monoxide particles in a given volume of gas.

Legge sui rapporti di combinazione tra sostanze gassose di Gay-Lussac

Le contraddizioni contenute nell'impianto teorico di Dalton sono cominciate ad emergere quando le quantità di gas elementari che formano i composti gassosi sono state misurate con maggiore precisione. Nel 1805 il chimico e fisico francese Joseph Louis Gay-Lussac (1778 - 1850), insieme al naturalista e geografo tedesco Alexander der von Humboldt (1769 - 1859), ha pubblicato una memoria in cui si dimostrava che, quando l'idrogeno e l'ossigeno si combinano per formare l'acqua, due volumi di idrogeno ed un volume di ossigeno danno luogo a due volumi di vapore acqueo. Nella sua teora atomica Dalton aveva invece assunto che un volume di idrogeno si sarebbe dovuto combinare con un volume di ossigeno per dar luogo ad un volume di vapore d'acqua. Negli assi successivi Gay-Lussac ha continuato i suoi esperimenti utilizzando altri gas e determinandone i relativi rapporti di combinazione, trovando, per es., che

3 Volumes Hydrogen + 1 Volume of nitrogen ⟶ 2 Volumes of ammonia
2 Volume of Hydrogen + 1 Volume of oxygen ⟶ 2 Volumes of water
1 Volume of Hydrogen + 1 Volume of chlorine ⟶ 2 Volumes of hydrochloric acid

I risultati dei suoi studi sono stati da lui pubblicati nel 1808 nell'articolo intitolato Sur le combinaisons des substances gazeuses les unes avec les autres, in cui egli ha formulato la sua legge sui rapporti di combinazione tra sostanze gassose, che afferma:

Nelle reazioni tra gas nelle stesse condizioni di pressione e temperatura, i volumi dei reagenti e i volumi dei prodotti stanno tra loro in rapporti costituiti da numeri interi piccoli.

This law is strikingly similar to Proust's and Dalton's lawns by weight.
The fact that gases always combine in volumes having simple ratios, made the discovery very favorable to Dalton's work and his atomic theory, but Dalton never accepted it. According to Gay-Lussac's observations, we have that 2H + O ⟶ 2HO. This is incorrect since it doesn't obey the law of conservation of mass and it's not consistent with Dalton's formulation of water as HO. But even Dalton's formula was problematic. It was known that the density of water was less than the density of oxygen, whilst writing a water molecule as HO (H + O ⟶ HO), the density of water should necessarily be greater than that of oxygen. Hence we need a new formula for water. The difficulty in reconciling Dalton's and Gay-Lussac findings required the asertion of the existence of diatomic elemental gases (H2, O2, etc) and were overcome by Avogadro in 1811, gave the correct interpretation of Gay-Lussac's law of combining volumes.

Changes in temperature or pressure change the volume of gas in a container. So temperature and pressure must remain contant for Gay-Lussac's law to be true. In 1802, Gay-Lussac had already proposed his gas-law which states that equal volumes of all gases at constant pressure expand equally with the same increase in temperature. Further a gas expands linearly with a fixed pressure and rising temperature; He determined that the expansion of gases was 1/266.66 of the volume, at 0°C, for each degree rise in tempreature. (Today, we know that at a constant pressure a gas expands 1/273.15 of its volume at 0°C, for each degree Celsius of rise in temperature. Today this law is known as Charle's Gas Law after the French Physicist J. A. Charles who made the same discovery in 1787, though he did not measure the coefficient of expansion.
