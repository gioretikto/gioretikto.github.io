Law of contant composition of definite proportions

By the end of the 18th century there was considerable debate as to whether the ratio of elements in a compound was dependent on the method by which the compountd was prepared. Much of this debate was due to inaccurate determinations of atomic weight. Proust noticed that copper carbonate—be it native or prepared in a lab—always broke down into the same proportions of copper, carbon, and oxygen by mass. There were about five parts of copper to four parts of oxygen to one part carbon − a constant ration of 5.3:4:1. In 1794 the French chemist Joseph-Louis Proust (1754–1826) developed the law of definite proportions:

A chemical compount always contains same elements combined together in same proportions by mass.

It implies from this law that in a chemical compound the elements are present in fixed and not arbitrary ration by mass. For example, pure water obtained from different source such as, sea, river or an industrial process always contains hydrogen and oxygen combined together in the ratio 1:8 by mass. Similarly carbon dioxide contains a carbon and oxygen in the ration of 3:8 by mass.
