The Bohr Model

Il modello di Rutherford dell'atomo non precisa come gli elettroni siano disposti attorno al nucleo carico positivamente. Se fossero staticamente posizionati rispetto a questo secondo l'elettrostatica classica sarebbero attrattii dal nucelo sino a collassare su di esso. Si potrebbe pensare invece che siano in moto attorno al nucleo, come pianeti orbitanti una stella. Secondo la fisica classica sarebbero in accelerazione costante per contrastare la forza centripeta. Classicamente comunque è previsto che particelle in accelerazione debbano emetere radiazione elettromagnetica. Questa emissione provocherebbe una perdita di energia da parte dell'elettrone con conseguente collasso sul nucleo.

Niels Bohr (1913) used quantum theory to interpret the spectral line of hydrogen. He argued that if according to quantum theory (E = hν) only certain frequencies of light are emitted by H atoms, then in contrast to the classical ideas, an H atom can exist only in certain discrete energy states. He then postulated as follows:

  1. L'elettrone si muove su orbite circolari secondo le leggi della fisica classica.

  2. Esistono delle orbite stazionarie e sinchè l'elettrone rimane in tali orbite questo è stabile e non perde energia.

  3. L'elettrone può passare da un'orbita ad una altra emettendo o assorbendo fotoni (energia).

    ΔE = Ef − Ei = hν

    Dove Ei è l'energia dello stato iniziale e Ef quella dello stato finale; h la costante di Plank parì a:

    h = 6.626 ⋅ 10−24 j ⋅ s

    Viene così incorporataa l'ipotesi fatta da Einstein nella modelizzazzione dell'effetto fotoelettrico: lo scambio di energia avviene per quantità discrete di energia pari a hν

  4. L'esistenza di determinate orbite stazionarie si giustifica con la quantizzazione del momento angolare, seconodo:

    me vr = nh   n = 1,2,3,...

    Dove ℏ = h/(2π), nota come acca tagliato è una costante ubiquitaria in meccanica quantistica.

Applying Newtown's sencod law, a stable orbit is achieved by satisfying

e2/(4πε0r2) = (mev2)/r

We thus obtain for the kinetic energy of the electron:

Ke = 1/2 ⋅ (mev2) = 1/2 ⋅ e2/(4πε0r)   1.5.1

The electrical potential energy between the negative charge of the electron moving in a circle with radius r around the positive charge of the nucleus is defined as

Ue = −e2/(4πε0r)

This energy corresponds to the work done in bringing the electorn from a radius of infinite distance to its present orbital position r. We know that for a conservative system the mechanical energy E = K + U is a constant, we have thus the total mechanical energy

E = K + U = mev2/2 −e2/(4πε0r) = −e2/(4πε0r) −e2/(8πε0r) =−e2/(8πε0r)

Therefore, we find the classical energy of the electron for atomic hydrogen is inversely proportional to its radius. However classical electrodynamics indicates that an accelerating charge must emit radation, similar to the electrons within a radio antenna. As an orbiting electron is continually accelerating, its total energy must drop and eventually spiral into the nucleus. Instead, observation showed stable atoms without emitting radiation from electrons in orbits. Bohr introduced two postulates in its semi-classical model. The first involves the quantization of the angular momentum of the electron: an electron does not radiate energy when in a stable orbit. A stable orbits is possible when the angular momentum of the orbit is an integral multiple of h/2π:

L = mvr = nh/2π = nℏ,   n = 1, 2, 3, ..

where ℏ = h/2π. Second, the electorn may emit or absorb energy only by undergoing a transition to an orbit of lower or higher energy, respectevely. The radiation corresponding to the electron's transition from an orbit of energy En to another Em is carried out by a photon of energy

hν = En − Em

The allowed orbits can now be determined by combining Eqs.

Ke = 1/2 ⋅ (mev2) = 1/2 ⋅ me(nℏ/mer)2 = e2/8πε0r

Hence, if we solve for r, the allowed orbital radii become

r = (4πε0ℏ2n2)/(mee2)   n = 1,2,..   1.5.2

The orbit with the smallest radius, called the Bohr radius, a0, corresponds to n = 1 and has the value

a0 = (4πε0ℏ2)/(mee2) = 0.053 nm.

According to the Bohr model, the elctron cannot get any closer to the nucleus than orbital radius a0, and that is why the atttraction beween electron and nucleus does not simply collapse them together.

Substituting Eq. 1.5.2 into Eq. 1.5.1, we find the allowed orbital energies to be

En = −(mee4) / (8ε02h2n2),   n = 1, 2, ..   1.5.3

Both the orbital radius and energy increase with the square of the principal quantum number n. Let's reason on the negative sign of equation 1.5.3.

Orbits closer to the nucleus correspond to values of E that are most negative. The value of E for n = ∞ (E∞) is set to zero. Electron in orbits close to the nucleus travel much more rapidly than electron in the outside orbits. The increase in kinetic energy of the negatively charged electron closest to the nucleus is offset by a greater decrease in the potential energy (it becomes more negative) due to its position relative to the positive nucleus. Kinetic energy is always positive and potential energy negative; the electrostatic energy between the opposite charges of the proton and electron is negative. So the magnitude of the electrostatic energy must be greater than that of the kinetic, in order that the total can be negative. The more negative this energy is, the more stable will be the nucleus that is formed. At the ground state E0 = −13.6 eV, which agree very well with the experimentally observed binding energy fro hydrogen.

The uppermost level shown, corresponding to E = 0 and n ⟶ ∞, represents the state for which the electron is completely removed from the atom. In this state the electron’s K and U are both zero, which means that the electron is at rest infinitely far away from the proton. The minimum energy required to ionize the atom—that is, to completely remove the electron — is called the ionization energy. The ionization energy for hydrogen is 13.6 eV.

Evaluating the orbital velocity of an electron in the ground state of hydrogen, we find v = 2.2 x 106 m/s. It is apparent from the equation that this is the largest velocity possible for a hydrogen atom electron. The fact that this velocity is less than 1% of the velocity of light justifies the use of non-relativisti mechanics in the Bohr model. On the other, hand for large values of Z the electron velocity becmes relativistic, and the such approximation cannot be applied.
