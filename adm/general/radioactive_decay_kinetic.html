The Kinetics of Radioactive Decay

The half-life for radioactive decay is that characteristic time during which half of the nuclei in any given sample will undergo decay. The half-life is independent of the amount of sample. Half-life provides an easy way to estimate the time required before a radioactive element is no longer a health hazard.

Strontium-90, for example, is a β emitter with a half-life of 29.1 years. Sr-90 emits only β radiation, however the high-energy gamma emitted by its Y-90 progeny is easily detected. The health problems associated with strontium-90 arise because calcium and strontium have similar chemical properties. Strontium-90 in grass and hay is incorporated into cow’s milk along with calcium and is then passed on to humans, where it lodges in the bones. Strontium-90 is taken into the body and deposited in bone, taking the place of calcium. Leaves of broad-leaved plants have less Sr than grass, so grazing herbivores have a higher Sr/Ca ratio than browsing herbivores Enamel apatite is best for Sr/Ca analysis. Radiation damage by strontium-90 (a β emitter) in bone has been directly linked to bone-related cancers. In addition significant quantities of strontium-90 were dispersed into the environment in atmospheric nuclear bomb tests in the 1960s and 1970s, and, from the half-life, we know that almost one half is still around.

It is fairly easy to measure the half-life of a short-lived radioisotope; we can measure the rate of radioactive decays using a Geiger counter. Because radioactive decay is a first-order process, the rate of decay is proportional to the number of atoms left. The rate of nuclear decay is determined from measurements of the activity (A) of a sample. Activity refers to the number of disintegrations observed per unit of time, a quantity that can be measured readily with devices such as a Geiger–Müller counter. Activity is proportional to the number of radioactive atoms present (N).:

A ∝ N

If the number of radioactive nuclei N is reduced by half, the activity of the sample will be half as large. Doubling N will double the activity. This evidence indicates that the rate of decomposition is first order with respect to N. Consequently, the equations describing rates of radioactive decay are the same as those used to describe the kinetics of first-order chemical reactions; the change in the number of radioactive atoms N per unit of time is proportional to N. Since the process is continuos;

dN/dt = −kN

The integrated rate equation can be written in two ways depending on the data used:

ln(N/N0) = −kt

or

ln(A/A0) = −kt

Here, N0 and A0 are the number of atoms and the activity of the sample initially, respectively, and N and A are the number of atoms and the activity of the sample after time t, respectively. Thus, N/N0 is the fraction of atoms remaining after a given time (t), and A/A0 is the fraction of the activity remaining after the same period. By taking the exponential of both sides

N = N0 e−kt

In these equations, k is the rate constant (decay constant) for the isotope in question. By substituting N = 1/2 ⋅ N0 and t = t1/2 into the previous equation, we find that

1/2 = e−kt1/2
t1/2 = ln 2/k = 0.693/k

Exercise. The activity of a radioactive substance in a sample at a given instant is 1/λ of what it was an hour before. What is the half-life (in hours) of the substance in the sample?

Solution. We have to calculate, t1/2 = ln 2/ ln k. To obtain k we use the fact that

ln(A/A0) = −kt
ln(1/λ) = −k  (after 1 hour)
ln(λ) = k

Thus t1/2 = ln 2/ ln λ.  ■

The previous Equations are useful in several ways:

  • If the activity (A) or the number of radioactive nuclei (N) is measured in the laboratory over some period t, then k can be calculated. The decay constant k can then be used to determine the half-life of the sample.

  • If k is known, the fraction of a radioactive sample (N/N0) still present after some time t has elapsed can be calculated.

  • If k is known, the time required for that isotope to decay to a fraction of the original activity (A/A0) can be calculated.

Although you might be able to obtain the half-life of a radioactive nucleus by direct observation in some cases, this is impossible for many nuclei because they decay too quickly or too slowly. Uranium-238, for example, has a half-life of 4.51 billion years, much too long to be directly observed! The usual method of determining the half-life is by measuring decay rates and relating them to half-lives.
