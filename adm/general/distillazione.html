La distillazione frazionata

The underlying principles of the distillation of mixtures of miscible liquids are embodied in two laws of physical chemistry: Raoult's law and Dalton's law. Daltont's law of partial pressures states that the total pressure of a gas, or vapour pressure of a liquid (P), is the sum of the partial pressures of its individual components A and B:

p = pA + pB

Raoult's law states that, at a given temperature and pressure, the partial vapour pressure of a compound in a mixture (pA) is equal to the vapour pressure of the pure compound p°A multiplied by its mole fraction (XA) in the mixture

pA = p°A ⋅ XA
pB = p°B ⋅ XB

Hence, from both laws, the total vapour pressure of a liquid mixture is dependent on the vapour pressures of the pure components and their mole fraction in the mixtures.

Come si vede la fase vapore si arricchisce sempre di più del componente più volatile, nel nostro caso il componente A. Ripetendo varie volte questa operazione, si raggiunge una situazione in cui la fase vapore è composta solo da A puro e la fase residua da B puro.
Se uno dei due componenti della soluzione ha una volatilità trascurabile (es. il sale marino rispetto all'acqua) questo non comparirà affatto nel distillato ed il frazionamento può essere eseguito in una sola operazione (distillazione semplice). Simple distillation can only be used to separate components that differ in boiling point by at least 80°C. A simple distillation is said to have one theoretical plate. A theoretical plate is a hypothetical zone where evaporation and subsequent condensation occurs. If one performed several simple distillations, the liquid would get increasing pure with each distillation, however, it would take substantial time an effort to perform multiple simple distillations.
More commonly, however, it is used to purify volatile components that are already fairly pure - by separating them from high-boiling impurities, for example. If the liquid is relatively pure, th first few drops of liquid to condense is called the forerun. The forerun consists of low boiling impurities that you do not want to keep.

Fractional distillation

Se invece entrambi i costituenti sono volatili, come avviene nel caso del vino (essenzialmente una soluzione di alcool in acqua), è necessario eseguire una serie di operazioni successive (distillazione frazionata) che permettono di giungere alla separazione di almeno un dei componenti allo stato puro. Fractional distillation is used for the separation of liquids that boil just a few degrees or even a fraction of a degree apart. La distilalzione frazionata viene utilizzata nell'industria petrolchimica così come nella produzione di liquori.

In a fractional distillation, a fractionating column is placed between the distillation flask and the distilling head. The fractionating column is packed with some material (glass beads, ceramic plates, steel gauze, etc.), which provides multiple sites for tiny simple distillations to occur. As the vapors rise up the fractionating column they are constantly being condensed on the packing material and subsequently re-vaporized. The percent composition of the more volatile component becomes greater and greater as the vapors progress up the column. Each vaporization/condensation cycle corresponds to one theoretical plate, thus a fractional distillation has many theoretical plate. More theoretical plates result in a more efficient distillation. The difference is equipment between a simple distillation and a fractional distillation, therefore, is the introduction of a fractional column between the distilling flask and the distilling adapter - or distilling head. The fractionating column should be perfectly vertical, and it needs to be lagged to prevent undue heat loss. The simplest way to do this is to wrap the column with aluminum foil shiby side.
There are many types of fractionating colums, but all have the common characteristic of providing many surfaces for heat exchange between the ascending vapor and the descending condensate. Some of the condensing liquid obtains heat from the ascending vapor and thus revaporizes into vapor richer in the low-boiling component. Similarly, some of the rising vapor loses heat to the descending condensate and recondenses into liquid richer in the high-boiling component. In this way, the vapor, which, after a seires os successive condensation and vaporitazion along the way up the columns, ascends to the top of the column, becomes the pure low-boiling component, and passes through the condenser into the receiver. The condensate, also after a series of successive vaporizations and condensations along the way down the column, descends to the bottom of the column, returns to the distilling flask, and becomes the pure high-boiling component.

Never attempt to distill the liquid to dryness; always leave a residue in the distillation flask. Distillation to dryness is potentially very dangerous since it involves strong heating of the material. You should never heat a closed system! Additionally, ensure that there is a slow steady stream of water running through the condenser.
