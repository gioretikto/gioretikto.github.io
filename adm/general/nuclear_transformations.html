Nuclear Transformations

In 1896 the French scientist Antoine Henri Becquerel accidentally found that salts of uranium (e.g potassium uranyl sulphate, K2UO2(SO4)2) emitted a radiation which affected a photographic plate, wrapped in a black paper and possessed the property of ionising gases. He attributed this phenomenon to a spontaneous emission of radiation by the uranium salt, without supplying any external energt and are completely unaffected by phisical and chemical conditions. Finally, several months after the initial discovery, he found that non-fluorescent uranium ore could also expose the plates, and he deduced that unknown emanations from the rock samples themselves caused the exposure. The new radiation produced ioniziation, and the intensity of the radioactivity could be measured by this ioniziation. He was also able to demonstrate that a large portion of these rays could be deflected by a magnetic field and were charged particles of the property similar to cathode rays. These radiations were caleld Bacquerel rays. His student, Marie Sklodowska Curie while working on her doctoral project, realized that the activity of uranium compound depended solely on the amount of uranium present and concluded that the emission of the rays described by Becquerel was an atomic property not an ordinary chemical reaction, she called it radioactivity. Pitchblende, a uranium ore, was the source of the uranium she needed for ther reasearch. She determined that the pitchblende was four times as active as uranium and concluded that pitchblende must contain something much more radioactive than uranium. Marie Curie discovered that not only uranium gave off the mysterious rays discovered by Becquerel, but thorium did as well. Pierre and Marie Curie observed that the intensity of the spontaneuous rays emitted by uranium or thorium increased as the amount of the uranium or thorium increased.
Working through the bitter winter of 1896 and all through 1897 (in which they had their first daughter, Iréne, who also became a prominent scientist), in July 1898 the Curies finally isolated in pitchblende a previously unknown element they named polonium after Marie’s homeland. Although most people would be satisfied by discovering a new element 400 times more radioactive than uranium, the Curies kept working. By this time, the 1 ton of pitchblende had been concentrated to an amount that would fit into an ordinary flask. Marie continued to extract and crystallize increasingly smaller amounts of material. Finally, in November 1898 she obtained crystals of the salt of another radioactive element in the pitchblende ore with chemical properties close to that of barium; they radium, which turned out to be 900 times more radioactive than uranium. They named the new element "radium" from the Latin word radius meaning "ray".
For their work the Curies were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1903, sharing the award with Henri Becquerel.

What was initially referred to as "Bacquerel rays", demonstrated to be three types of radioactive emission: alpha particles, beta particles, and gamma rays.

α radiation

An alpha particle, or a particle, is a helium nucleus 4 2 He 2 + . Alpha-particle production is a very common mode of decay for heavy radioactive nuclides. For example,

84 210 Po → 82 206 Pb + 2 4 α

The product nucleus has an atomic number that is two less, and a mass number that is four less, than that of the original nucleus. Another example is the radioactive decay of radium-226.

88 226 Ra → 86 222 Rn + 2 4 He

The fact that α particles are emitted rather than single neutron or protons reveals that these particles are very stable. When heavy nuclei undergo alpha decay, mosto of the decay energy appears as kinetic energy of the alpha particle. α particles have an elevated kinetic energy but a relatevely moderate velocity, for this reason.

β Radiation

The most common decay process in which the mass number of the decaying nucleus remains constant is beta-particle production. A β− particle is a high-speed electron emitted from an unstable nucleus. Beta emission is equivalent to the conversion of a neutron to a proton.

neutron ⟶ proton + electron

that can be written symbolically as

0 1 n → 1 1 p + − 1 0 e

The β particle is assigned the mass number 0, since its mass is tiny compared with that of a proton or neutron. An example of beta decay is the following

53 131 I → 54 131 Xe + − 1 0 e

It should be pointed out that although the β particle is an electron, the emitting nucleus does not contain electrons. As we shall see later in this chapter, a given quantity of energy (which is best regarded as a form of matter) can become a particle (another form of matter) under certain circumstances. The unstable nuclide creates an electron as it releases energy in the decay process. The electron thus results from the decay process rather than being present before the decay occurs. Think of this as somewhat like talking: Words are not stored inside us but are formed as we speak. Later in this chapter we will discuss in more detail this very interesting phenomenon where matter in the form of particles and matter in the form of energy can interchange.

γ rays

A gamma ray, or γ ray, refers to a high-energy photon (wavelength about 1 pm, or 10−12 m). Frequently, γ-ray production accompanies nuclear decays and particle reactions, such as in the a-particle decay of 238

92 238 U → 2 4 He + 90 234 Th + 2 0 0 γ

where two γ rays of different energies are produced in addition to the a particle. The emission of γ rays is one way a nucleus with excess energy (in an excited nuclear state) can relax to its ground state. Often gamma emission occurs very quickly after radioactive decay. In some cases, however, an excited state has significant lifetime before it emits a gamma photon. A metastable nucleus is a nucleus in an excited state with a lifetime of at least one nanosecond (10−9 s). In time, the metastable nucleus decays by gamma emission. An example is metastable technetium-99, denoted 99m 43 Tc, which is used in medical diagnosis

43 99 m Tc → 43 99 Tc + 0 0 γ

Positron production

Positron production (abbreviated β+) occurs for nuclides that are below the zone of stability (those nuclides whose neutron/proton ratios are too small). The positron is a particle with the same mass as the electron but opposite charge. Positron emission is equivalent to the conversion of a proton to a neutron

proton ⟶ neutron + β+

1 1 p → 0 1 n + + 1 0 e

An example of a nuclide that decays by positron production is sodium-22:

11 22 Na → 10 22 Ne + + 1 0 e

Electorn capture

Electron capture (abbreviated EC): the decay of an unstable nucleus by capturing, or picking up, an electron from an inner orbital of an atom. In effect, a proton is changed to a neutron, as in positron emission.

1 1 p + − 1 0 e → 0 1 n

as illustrated by the process

80 201 Hg + − 1 0 e → 79 201 Au + γ

This reaction would have been of great interest to the alchemists, but unfortunately it does not occur at a rate that would make it a practical means for changing mercury to gold. Gamma rays are always produced along with electron capture to release excess energy.

Nuclear transformations

In 1919 Lord Rutherford, while working in the Cavendish Laboratory at Cambridge University in the UK, observed the first nuclear transformation, the change of one element into another. He found that by bombarding N-14 with α particles, the nuclide O-17 could be produced:

7 14 N + 2 4 He → 8 17 O + 1 1 H

For the first time, the alchemist's dream became − in principle − a reality: transmutation had occured by man's intervention. The reaction yield was very low and it was obvious that for further study much more intense sources of α particles were required. Many α emitters amont natural radioelements were available, but the only one that was feasible for the preparation of intense sources was polonium. Obviously, the best place to prepare large amounts of this element was the “Institut du Radium” in Paris, founded by the discoverer of the element. In 1925, Marie Curie assigned this task to her new assistant Frédéric Joliot (1900-1958). The following year, Joliot married Iréne Curie (1897-1956), Marie’s daughter and a close collaborator. The Joliot-Curie couple was soon to reach fame equal to that of Marie and Pierre Curie.
After Joliot had submitted his doctoral thesis on the electro-chemistry of polonium, he continued the work with his wife and together they made the strongest polonium source available at that time. They exposed certain light elements to the intense beam of α-particles and on 15 January 1934 reported that some of them, like aluminum and boron, could be made artificially radioactive. It was also reported that the respectively formed isotopes, radiophosphorus and radionitrogen, obey a law of exponential decay in the same way as do naturally occurring radioactive elements. The first man-made radioelements emitted a new type of radiation consisting of a positively charged electron, the positron. This particle had been predicted by Paul Dirac and until then its existence was confirmed only in cosmic rays. For the discovery of artificial, or induced radioactivity Joliot-Curie received the 1935 Nobel Prize for chemistry. In their experiment, they bombdarded aluminum-27 with α particles emitted by polonium, phosphorus-30 and neutrons are produced. Phosphorus-30 is radiocative, has a half-life of 2.5 minutes, and decays to silicon-30 with the emission of a positron. The equations are:

13 27 Al + 2 4 He → 15 30 P + 0 1 n 15 30 P → 14 30 P + + 1 0 e

The possibility of manufacturing transuranic elements in the laboratory only became a possibility after 1932, when Chadwick performed a series of experiments at Rutherford's laboratory and the neutron was discovered, reconceptualising the atomic nucleus as proton-neutron rather than a proton-electron composite.

In 1934, Enrico Fermi (who succeded with the theory Fermi-Dirac theory and the β-decay theory) and his group in Rome, including Edoardo Amaldi, Emilio Segré and Oscar D'Agostino, studied systematically neutron reactions with all the elements of the periodic system, paying particular attention to the heaviest elements. The most significant discovery was that the artificial radioactivity induced by slow neutrons was very much larger than that by fast neutrons; In October, Fermi discovered by accident that if the bombarding neutron were caused to be slowed ("moderated") before hitting the target element by first having them pass through paraffin, the acitivity of the induced radioactivity could in some cases be drastically increased. Famously, they bombarded uranium with slow neutron and since all of these products emitted beta rays and none of these had chemical properties similar to that of U or nearby elements, Fermi and collaborators attributed these activities to the production of new transuranic elements with Z = 93 and 94: These new elements he called Ausenium (Ao) and Hesperium (Hs), which they believed to have been formed as

92 238 U + 0 1 n → 92 239 Ao + β − → 94 239 Hs + 2 β −

They do not consider the isotope U-235 because it was discovered only about one year later. Indeed on 12 December 1938 Enrico Germi received the Nobel Prize in physics "for his demostration of the existence of new radioactive elements produced by netruon irradiation, and for his related discovery of nuclear reactions brought about by slow neutrons".

However, it quickly turned out the the Nobel Foundation's support of Ausenium and hesperium was an embarassing mistake. Ironically, in december 1938, Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann were finishing the work that would lead directly to the recognition of nuclear fission and the rejection of Fermi's "demonstration" of these "new" elements. They discovered that one of the induced activities in neutron-irradiated uranium came from an isotope of barium, a nucleus with roughly hald the mass and charge of the uranium nucleus. Georhe Gamow had suggested early on, and Bohr had given good arguments that a nucleus was much more like a liquid drop ready to divide itself at the slightest provocation, such as the impact of a single neutron. We know there were strong froces that would resist such a process, just as the surface tension of an ordinary liquid drop tends to resist its division into two smaller ones. But the nuclei differed from ordinary drops in one important way: they were electrically charged, and wthat was known to counteract the surface tension. After separation the two nuclei would be drive apart by thir mutual electrin repulsion and would acquire high speed and hence a very large energy about 200 Mev in all; Where Could the energy come from? Lise Meiner remembered the empirical formula for computing the masses of nuclei and worked out that the two nuclei formed by the division of a uranium nucleus together would be lighter thaqn the original uranium nucleus by about one-fifth the mass of a proton. Now whenever mass disappears energy is created, according to Einsteins's formula E = mc2, and one-fifth of a proton mass was just equivalent to 200 MeV.
Otto Hahn was awarded (solely) the 1944 Nobel Prize in Chemistry; Meitner and Strassmann did not share in the recognition.

The fission products are created in an excited state and would likely lose energy by emitting neutrons. These neutrons could be captured by other nuclei and induce more fission events, i.e., in uranium it is possible to obtain a chain reaction of fission accompained by release of enormous energy. However this was in disagreement with the observed facts: nobody had ever seen a piece of uranium explode when bomdarder with neutrons. Pondering this contradiction, Bohr remembdered that four years earlier, Arthur Dempster had found, using his advanced mass-spectrometer, the rare uranium isotope U-235, and that uraniums appeared to consist of 99.28% of U-238 and 0.72% of U-235. Three new questions presented themselves: How many neutron and of what energy are produced by each fission event of U.235? What happens to nuclei of U-238 after neutron capturing? What conditions are required for a sustained chain reaction in uranium to occur?

Over the years, many other nuclear transformations have been achieved, mostly using particle accelerators, which, as the name reveals, are devices used to give particles very high velocities. Because of the electrostatic repulsion between the target nucleus and a positive ion, accelerators are needed when positive ions are used as bombarding particles. The particle, accelerated to a very high velocity, can overcome the repulsion and penetrate the target nucleus, thus effecting the transformation. A schematic diagram of one type of particle accelerator, the cyclotron, is shown in Fig. 18‐5. The ion is introduced at the center of the cyclotron and is accelerated in an expanding spiral path by use of alternating electric fields in the presence of a magnetic field. The linear accelerator illustrated in Fig. 18‐6 uses changing electric fields to achieve high velocities on a linear pathway.
In addition to positive ions, neutrons are often used as bombarding particles to effect nuclear transformations. Because neutrons are uncharged and thus not repelled electrostatically by a target nucleus, they are readily absorbed by many nuclei, leading to new nuclides. The most common source of neutrons for this purpose is a fission reactor (see Section 18‐6).
By using neutron and positive-ion bombardment, scientists have been able to extend the periodic table. Technetium was first prepared by directing deuterons, or nuclei of hydrogen-2 atoms, from a cyclotron to a molybdenum target. The nuclear reaction is

42 96 Mo + 1 2 H → 43 97 Tc + 0 1 n

The energies of particles obtained from a cyclotron are limited (to about 20 MeV for deuterons, for example). For higher energies, more sophisticated accelerators are required. Figure 20.7 shows the accelerator at Fermilab in Batavia, Illinois. Larger and larger particle accelerators are being built to study the basic constituents of matter.

The transuranium elements

The transuranium elements are elements with atomic numbers greater than that of uranium (Z = 92), the naturally occurring element of greatest Z. In 1940, E. M. McMillan and P. H. Abelson, at the University of California at Berkeley, discovered the first transuranium element. He was trying to measure the energies of the two recoiling fragments from the neutron-induced fission of uranium. He placed a thin layer of uranium oxide on on piece of paper. Next to this he stacked very thin sheets of cigarette paper to stop and collect the fission fragments of uranium. During his studies he found that there was another radiocative product of the reaction − one that did not recoil enough to escape the uranium layer as did the fission products. He suspected that this product was formed by the capture of a neutron by the more abundant isotope of uranium, U-238. M. McMillan and Philip Abelson who joined him in this research, showe in 1940, by chemical means, that this product is an isotope of element 93, formed by the following sequence

92 238 U + 0 1 n → 92 239 U + γ 92 239 U ( t 1 / 2 = 23.5  min ) β − → 93 239 Np ( t 1 / 2 = 2.6  days )

Neptunium, the element beyond uranium, was named after the plane Neptune because this planet is beyond the planet Uranus, after which uranium had been named.
Plutonium was the second transuranium element to be discovered, by bombarding uranium with charged particles, in particular, deuterons 2H, using the 60-inch cyclotrone at the Univeristy of California Berkley, McMillan's colleagues Seaborg, Kennedy, Wahl, succeeded in preparing a new isotope of neptunu, which decayed by β emission to Pu-238:

92 238 U + 1 2 H → 93 239 Np + 2 n 93 239 Np ( t 1 / 2 = 2.12  days ) β − → 94 238 Pu ( t 1 / 2 = 87.7  years )

Early in 1941, Pu-239, the most important isotope of plutonium, was discovered by Kennedy, Segré, Wahl and Seaborg. Pu-239 wsa produced by the decay of Np-239, which had been produced by the irradiation of U-238 by neutrons, using the reaction.

92 239 U + 0 1 n → 92 239 U + γ 92 239 U ( t 1 / 2 = 23.5  min ) β − → 93 239 Np ( t 1 / 2 = 2.35  days ) β − → 94 239 Pu ( t 1 / 2 = 24110  years )

This isotope Pu-239, was shown to have a cross section for thermal neutron-induced fission that exceeded that og U-235, a property that made it important for nuclear weapons, considering that no isotope separation was necessary for its preparation, unlike for U-235 was named after the planet Pluto, following the pattern used in naming neptunium.
Plutonium-238 emits only alpha radiation, which is easily stopped by shielding. The isotope has been used as a power source for space satellites, navigation buoys, and heart pacemakers.

The next transuranium elements to be discovered, americium and curium (Am and Cm; Z=95 and 96, respectively) represent an important milestone in chemistry, namely the recognition of a new group of elements in the Periodic Table, the actinides. According to the Periodic Table of Figure 3, one expected americium and curium to be eka-iridium and eka-platinum, i.e. to have chemical properties similar to those of iridium and platinum. In 1944, Seaborg conceived the idea that all the known elements heavier than actinium (Z= 89) had been misplaced in the Periodic Table. He postulated that the elements heavier than actinium form a second series similar to the lanthanide elements, called the actinide series. This series would end in element 103 (Lr) and, analogously to the lanthanides, these elements would have a common oxidation state of +3.
Once this redox property had been understood, the use of a proper chemical procedure led quickly to the identification of an isotope of a new element.

Neptunium, plutonium, and americium were unknown prior to their preparation via these nuclear reactions. Later, these elements were found to be present in trace quantities in uranium ores.
