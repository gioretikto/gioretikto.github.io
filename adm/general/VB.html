Valence bond (VB) theory

The are two theories of covalent bonding that have evolved based on quantum theory: the valence bond theory (or VB theory) and the molecular orbital theory. In VB theory, the atoms are considered to retain their identity and except valence shell atomic orbitals which overlap, the other atomic orbitals remain undisturbed. In case of MO theory (Hund and Mulliken, 1932), the atomic orbitals of the bonded atoms lose their identity, they mix up and form what is known as molecular orbitals. A molecular orbital may be conceived as the region in the space around the nuclei of all the bonded atoms where the probability of finding an electron is maximum.

Valence bond theory was proposed by Heitler and London in 1927 and later developed by Linus Pauling. Sone salient features of the theory are:

  1. A covalent bond is formed by the partial overlap of atomic orbitals of combining atoms. Complete overlap is not possible due to repulsion between nuclei.

  2. The atomic orbitals which are to overlap must be half filled and the unpaired electrons should have opposite spins.

  3. The strength of bond formed depends upon the extent of overlapping. The greater the overlapping, the stronger is the bond formed.

  4. Due to overlapping, electron pairing results with the net decrease in energy which imparts it greater stability.

  5. Only the valence electrons of the combining atoms participate in bonding, whereas, the inner orbitals from each atom remain undisturbed.

  6. The stability of the covalent bond is due to the exchange of valence electrons between the atoms involved in bond making.

  7. Depending upon the type of overlapping, the covalent bonds are of two types, i.e., sigma bonds and pi bonds.

  8. Valency of an element is equal to the number of half filled orbitals in the valence shell of the atom.

Limitations of VBT

  1. Since the two electrons of the shared pair constitute the covalent bond this theory does not give any explanation of the formation of a coordinate bond.

  2. The two atoms in O, molecule should have close electronic shells resembling those of Ne which would give no unpaired electrons to the molecule and thus, will make it diamagnetic.

  3. Actual experiments show that O, molecule is paramagnetic, indicating the presence of unpaired electrons in oxygen molecule.

  4. This theory does not consider the formation of odd electron molecules or ions such as H2+ ion where no pairing of electrons occurs.

  5. If fails to explain the bonding in metals and intermetallic compounds as well as the bonding in electron deficient compounds.
