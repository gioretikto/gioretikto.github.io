Equivalent weight

The equivalent weight (mass) of a substance is the mass of that substance that will react with 1 gram of hydrogen. In the case of a substance that doesn't react directly with hydrogen, its equivalent weight corresponds to the quantity in grams that react with an equivalent weight of a another known substance. Thus, the equivalent weight of oxygen is 8 grams that of Chlorine is 35.46 g.

Example 1. 1 g of Calcium combines with 4 g of Bromine. The equivalent weight of bromine is 80 g. Calculate the equivalent weight of Calcium.

Solution. We have to set the equation 1g Ca/ 4 g Br = x g Ca : 80 g Br. Which yields to the equivalent weight of Calcium of 20 g.  ■

The name weight, is not correct since we should userather the term equivalent mass, even though the former is more used. Contrary to the atomic weight, which is a pure number, the equivalent weight is measured in grams.

The equivalent masses of certain substances differ from compound to compound and from reaction to reaction. From example, in hydrogen peroxyde H2O2, the equivalent weight of oxygen is 16 g.

Example 2. Since Cu = 63.5 a.m.u., has two oxidation states (Cu+) and Cu2+ it can form two oxides Cu2O and CuO. So, eq. wts are 63.5/1 g and 63.5/2 g erspectevely.

63.5 ⋅ 2 g Cu /16 g O = x g Cu / 8 g. O

Form last examples it becomes evident that elements having more than one valency values, have different equivalent weights and can be found by the relation

Equivalent weight of an element = Atomic weight of the elment/valency of the element

So since Cu has two valency corresponding to 1 for Cuprous (Cu+) and 2 for cupric Cu2+ we can calculate the eq. wts as 63.5/1 g and 63.5/2 g erspectevely.

The concept of equivalent weight arose from the studies conducted by the Prussian chemist Jeremias Benjamin Richter (1762-1807), lo stesso che coniò il termine stoichiometry, da στοιχεῖον, "stoicheion" "elemento" e μέτρον, "metron" "measure", published a book entitled "Anfangsgriinde der Stochiometric oder MeBkunst chemischer Elemente", that is Principles of Stoichiometry or Art of Measuring Chemical Elements. Nel 1792, formulò la legge delle proporzioni reciproche:

Weights of two elements A and B which combines separately with a fixed weight of third element C, are either the same or the simple multiples of weights of A and B which combine with each other.

As an example, 1 g of Hydrogen (A) combines with 35.45 g of Cl (B) or with 8 g of Oxygen (C). The ration between the two masses is m(B):m(C) è 35.45:8. The same ratio is observed when Cl and O react to form ClO2: 70.9 g of Cl react with 16 g of O, with the ratio m(C):m(B) = 70.9:16 = 35.45:8.

Compounds Reacting masses Ratio between masses
H2O 1 g 8 g 1:8
Cl2O 70.9 16 g 70.9:16 = 35.45:8
HCl 1 g 35.45 g 35.45:1
H2O2 2 g 32 g 1:16

An other example is the following:

Compounds Reacting masses Ratio between masses
CS2 12 g 64 g 64:12
CO2 12 g 32 g 32:12
SO2 32 g 32 g 32:32

The ratio between the masses of S and O, involved in the formation of CS2 and CO2 is 32:32 a submultiples dof the ratio 64:32 = 2.

Un elemento può avere più di un peso equivalente, poichè può formare più di un composto. Ad esempio l'idrogeno oltre all'acqua forma l'acqua ossigenata H2O2, in questo caso il peso equivalente dell'ossigeno è 16 e non 8 come nell'acqua; L'azoto ha peso equivalente 14 g in N2O, 7g in NO e 4.67g in N2O3

The equivalent weight of a compount is obtained from the sum dei pesi equivalenti degli elementi di cui esso è costituito:

pE(CO2) = pE(C) + pE(O) = 12g + 8g = 20g

Example 1. A sample of Al2O2 contains 42.95 grams of Al and 38.4 grams of oxygen. Knowing that the equivalent weight of oxygen is 8.0 g, calculate the equivalent weight of Aluminum.

Solution. The ratio between the combination weights of the two elements is proportional to the equivalent weights of the two elements. That is

42.95 g : 38.4 g = x : 8.0 g

with x the equivalent weight of aluminum. Solving the equation we find x = 9 g. ■
