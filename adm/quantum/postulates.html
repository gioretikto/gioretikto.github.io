Postulates of quantum mechanics

Certain hypotheses, or postulates, serve as the foundation on which a physical theory is built up. The theory considers these postulates as fundamental. In other words, an explanation of the postulates is beyond the scope of the theory; the theory is rather concerned wth the consequences of the postulates. In fact, the theory is nothing but the mathematical framework which connects the postulates to their observable consequences. Of couse, one often regards the postulates as part of the theory itself, in which case the consequences are referred to as predictions of the theory. Agreement between these predictions and the experimental observations providews a verification of the theory, and thus, an indirect justification of the postulates.

Superposition of Quantum States

Superposition of states is an important characteristic property of quantum mechanics. The Schrödinger equation is a linear differential equation. If two solutions ψ1 and ψ2 to the equation exist, the linear superposition ψ = c1 ψ1 + c2 ψ2 represents also a solution. The most general solution is the linear combination of all eigenstates:

|ψ⟩ = Σ ci |ψi⟩   1.5.1

Superposition states exist as long as no other measurements are performed on them. They continue to exist as long as they are not exposed to an interaction with a measurement probe, or, more generaly, with any kind of external environment. We encountered this propery already in the discussion of the double slit experiment with particles. The wave ψ is a probability amplitude, which is not itself a probability, but from which a probability may be calculated. With only slit #1 open, the probability amplitude that the electron lands a a particular point X on the scree is ψ1, so that the probability of finding the electron there is P1 = |ψ|2. Opening only slit #2 yields an amplitude ψ2, wich gives rise to a probability P2 of finding the photon at X. But with both slits open, we have a combined probability amplitude ψ = ψ1 + ψ2, yielding a probablity

P = |ψ|2 = |ψ1 + ψ2|2

for the electron to wind up at X. The two probability amplitudes may reinforce one another or cancel each other out, enhancing or suppressing the probability that the electron lands at X.

Since the wavefunction is normalized, the integral gives a weighted average of the possible observable values.

Experimentally, in principle, the average value of an observable (e.g. the momentum) is determined as follows. One prepares large number, say N, of identical systems (called ensemble), all in one and the same state |ψi⟩. One then measures the observable in question for each system. In general, a range of measured values is obtained (the distribution depending on the state). The observable is truly a random variable.
At the end of our experiment, the eigenvalue λi has been seen fi times, where 0 ≤ fi ≤ N. The experimental average value of the observable is the sum of the measured values divided by N.

A ¯ = ∑ i f i λ i N = ∑ i p i λ i

where pi = fi/N is the probability to find an individual system of the ensemble to be described by the state |ψi⟩. If N is sufficienlty large, this number (known is statistics as the estimated expected value of A) will be very close to ⟨ψ|Aψ⟩.

For this series of measured values, any function (e.g., a power) of the measured values can be computed. Again taking the average value, one obtains the experimental average value of a function of the observable. Axioms III and II state how these average values are computed quantum mechanically by means of the operator corresponding to the observable and the wave function.
