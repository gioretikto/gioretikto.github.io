Simple Harmonic Oscillator

The simple harmonic oscillator is one of the most important problem in quantum mechanics. It not only illustrates many of the basic concepts and methods of quantum mechanics but also has much practical value. Essentially any potential well can be approximates by a simple harmonic oscillator, so it describes phenomena from molecular vibrations to nuclear structure. Moreover, because the Hamiltonian is basically the sum of square of two canonically conjugate variables, it is also important starting point for much of quantum field theory.

The basic Hamiltonian is

H = p2/2m + mω2x2/2

where ω is the angular frequency of the classical oscillator related ot the spring constant k in Hooke's law via ω = (k/m)1/2. The operators x and p are, of couse, Hermitian.

It is convenient to define two non-Hermitian operators,

a = m ω 2 ℏ ( x + i p m ω ) , a † = m ω 2 ℏ ( x − i p m ω )

which are known as the annhilation operator and the creation operator, respectively. Here's what these two new operators do:

  • Creation operator: It raises the energy level of an eigenstate by one level, so if the harmonic oscillator is in the 5th energy leel, the creation operator raises it to the 6th level.

  • annhilation operator: The reverse, lowering eigenstates one level.

These operators make it easier to solve for the energy spectrum without a lot of work solving for the actual eigenstates. Now we can write the Hamiltonian in terms of a and a†.

H = ℏω(aa† + 1/2)

We make an additional definition introducing the number operator,

N = a†a

which is obviously Hermitian. So we write the Hamiltonian as

H = ℏω(N + 1/2) *

The N operator returns the number of the energy level of the harmonic oscillator. If you denote the eigenstates of N as |n⟩, you get this, where n represents the energy level or quantum number.

N |n⟩ = n |n⟩

Because of * we also have

H |n⟩ = (n + 1/2)ℏω |n⟩

which means that the energy eigenvalue are given by

En = (n + 1/2) ℏω

To appreciate the physical significance of a, a† and N, let us first note that

[a†, a] = (1/2ℏ) (-[x,p] + i[p,x]) = 1

[N, a] = [a†a, a] = a†[a, a] + [a†, a] a = -a

Likewise, we can derive

[N, a] = a†

As a result, we have

Na†|n⟩ = ([N,a†] + a†N)|n⟩ = (n + 1)|n⟩a†|n⟩

and

Na|n⟩ = ([N,a] + aN)|n⟩ = (n - 1)|a|n⟩

With slightly more effort, it can be shown that

a |n⟩ = n1/2|n - 1⟩
a† |n⟩ = (n + 1)1/2|n + 1⟩

Hence, a and a† convert |n⟩ into |n - 1⟩ and |n + 1⟩, respectively, which is why they are called annhilation and creation operators.
