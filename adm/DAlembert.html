D'Alembert's Principle

A virtual displacement {δri} of the system is an arbitraty, infinitesimal change of the coordinates that is compatible withe the constraints and the applied forces.
It is performed at a fixed time and therefore has nothing to do with the actual, infinitesimal motion {dri} of the system during the time dt (i.e. the real displacement).

Suppose the system is in equilibrium; i.e. the total force on each paricle vanishes, Fi. Then clearly the dot product Fi ⋅ δri, which is the virtual work of the force Fi in the displacement δri, also vanishes. The sum of these vanishing products over all particles must likewise be zero:

Σi Fi ⋅ δri = 0   1.2.1

Decompose Fi into the applied force Fi(a), and the force of constraint fi

Fi = Fi(a) + fi

so that Eq. 1.2.1 becomes

Σi Fi(a) ⋅ δri + Σi fi ⋅ δri = 0

We now restrict ourselves to system for which the net virtual work of the forces of constraint is zero. We have seen that this conditions holds true for rigid bodies and it is valid for a large number of other constraints. Thus if a particle is constrained to move on a surface, the force of constraint is perpendicular to the surface, while the virtual displacement must be tangent to it, and hence the virtual work vanishes. We therefore have the condition for equilibrium of a system that the virtual work of the applied forces vanishes:

Σi Fi(a) ⋅ δri = 0   1.2.2

The previous equation is often called the principle of virtual work. Note that the coefficients of δri (i.e. in general Fi(a) ≠ 0), since the δri can no longer be set equal to zero: the δri are not completely independent but are connected by constraints. In order to equate the coefficients to zero, we must transform the principle into a form involving the virtual displacement of the qi, which are independent.

Eq. 1.2.2 satisfies our ness in that it does not contain the fi, but it deals only with static. We shall focus now ona condition involving the general motion of the system. To obtain such a principle, we use a device first thought of by James Bernoulli and developed by D'Alembert. The equation of motion

Fi = ṗi

can be written as

Fi − ṗi = 0

which states that the particles in the system will be in equilibrium under a force equal to the actual force plus a "reversed effective force" − ṗi. Instead of Eq 1.2.2, we can immediately write

Σi (Fi − ṗi) ⋅ δri = 0

and, making the same resolution into applied forces and forces of contraint, there results

Σi (Fi(a) − ṗi) ⋅ δri + Σi fi ⋅ δri = 0

We again restrict ourselves to systems for which the virtual work of the forces of constraint vanishes and therefore obtain

Σi (Fi(a) − ṗi) ⋅ δri = 0  1.2.3

which is often called D'Alembert's principle. We have achieved out aim, in that the force of constraint no longer appear, and the superscript (a) can now be dropped without ambiguity. It is still not in a useful form to furnish equations of motion for the system. We must ow transform the principle into an expression involving virtual displacement of the generalized coordinates, which are independent of each other (for holonomic constraints), so that th ecoefficients of the δqi can be set separately equal to zero.
The translation from ri to qi language starts from the transformation equation

ri = ri(q1, q2, ..., qn, t)

assuming n independent coordinates, and is carried out by means of the usual "chain rule" of the calculus of partial differentiation. Thus, vi is expressed in terms of the q̇k by the formula

v i ≡ d r i d t = ∑ k ∂ r i ∂ q k q ˙ k + ∂ r i ∂ t  1.2.4

Similarly, the arbitrary virtual displacement δri can be connected with the virtual displacement δqi by

δ r i = ∑ j ∂ r i ∂ q j δ q j  1.2.5

Note that no variation of time δt is involved here, since a virtual displacement by definition considers only displacements of the coordinates. In terms of the generalized coordinates, the virtual work of the Fi becomes

∑ i ∂ F i δ r i = ∑ i , j F i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j δ q j = ∑ j Q j δ q j  1.2.6

where the Qj are called the components of the generalized force, defined as

Q j = ∑ i F i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j  1.2.7

Note that just as the q's need not have the dimensions of lenght, so the Q's do not necessarily have the dimensions of work. For example, Qj might be a torque Nj and dqj a differential angle dθ, which makes Nj dqj a differential of work.
The second term in d’Alembert’s Principle 1.2.3 can be transformed using Equation 1.2.5

∑ i n p ˙ i ⋅ δ r i = ∑ i n m i r ¨ i ⋅ δ r i = ( ∑ i n m i r ¨ i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j ) δ q j  1.2.8

The right-hand side of 1.2.8 can be rewritten as

( ∑ i n m i r ¨ i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j ) δ q j = ∑ i n { d d t ( m i r ˙ i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j ) − m i r ˙ i ⋅ d d t ( ∂ r i ∂ q j ) } δ q j  1.2.9

Note that Equation 1.2.4 gives that

∂ v i ∂ q ˙ j = ∂ r i ∂ q j  1.2.10

therefore the first right-hand term in 1.2.9 can be written as

d d t ( m i r ˙ i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j ) = d d t ( m i v i ⋅ ∂ v i ∂ q ˙ j )  1.2.11

The second right-hand term in 1.2.9 can be rewritten by interchanging the order of the differentiation with respect to t and qj

d d t ( ∂ r i ∂ q j ) = ∂ v i ∂ q j

Substituting 1.2.10 and 1.2.11 into 1.2.9 gives

∑ i n p ˙ i ⋅ δ r i = ( ∑ i n m i r ¨ i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j ) δ q j = ∑ i N { d d t ( m i v i ⋅ ∂ v i ∂ q ˙ j ) − m i v i ⋅ ∂ v i ∂ q j } δ q j  1.2.12

Inserting 1.2.6 and 1.2.12 into d’Alembert’s Principle 1.2.3 leads to the relation

∑ i n ( F i A − p ˙ i ) ⋅ δ r i = − ∑ j N { d d t ( ∂ ∂ q ˙ j ( ∑ i 1 2 m i v i 2 ) ) − ∂ ∂ q j ( ∑ i N 1 2 m i v i 2 ) − Q j } δ q j = 0

The ∑ miv2/2 term can be identified with the system kinetic energy T. Thus d’Alembert Principle reduces to the relation

∑ j N { [ d d t ( ∂ T ∂ q ˙ j ) − ∂ T ∂ q j ] − Q j } δ q j = 0  1.2.13

For cartesian coordinates T is a function only of velocities (ẋ, ẏ, ż) and thus the term ∂T/∂qj = 0. However, as discussed in appendix xx, for curvilinear coordinates ∂T/∂qj ≠ 0 due to the curvature of the coordinates as is illustrated for polar coordinates where v = ṙr̂ + rθ̇ θ̂.
Thus far no restriction has been made on the nature of the constraints other than that the be workless in a virtual displacement. The variables qj can be any set of coordinates used to describe the motion of the system. If, however, the constraints are holonomic, then it is possible to find sets of independent coordinates qj that contain the constraint conditions implicitly in the trasformation equations xx. Any virtual displacement δqj is the independent of qk, and therefore only way for (1.2.13) to hold is for the individual coefficients to vanish

d d t ( ∂ T ∂ q ˙ j ) − ∂ T ∂ q j = Q j  1.2.14

There are n such equations in all.
When the forces are derivable from a scalar potential function V,

Fi = −∇i V

Then the generalized forces can be written using definition 1.2.7 as

Q j = ∑ i F i ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j = ∑ i ∇ i V ⋅ ∂ r i ∂ q j

which is exactly the same expression for the partial derivative of a function V(r1, r2, ..., rn, t) with respect to qj:

Qj ≡ − ∂V/∂qj

Eq. 1.2.14, can be rewritten as

d d t ( ∂ T ∂ q j ˙ ) − ∂ T − V ∂ q j = 0  1.2.15

The equations of motion in the form are not necessarily restricted to conservative systems. only if V is not an explicit function of time is the system conservative. As here defined, the potential V does not depende on the generalized velocities. Hence, we can include a term in V is the partial derivative with respect to q̇j:

d d t ( ∂ T − V ∂ q j ˙ ) − ∂ T − V ∂ q j = 0

Or, defining a new function, the Lagrangian L, as

L = T − V

The Eqs. 1.2.14 become

d d t ( ∂ L ∂ q j ˙ ) − ∂ L ∂ q j = 0

known as Lagranges's equations.
