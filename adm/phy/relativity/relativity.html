Relativity as a coordinate symmetry

In broadest terms, relativity holds that the universe doesn’t care what systems of coordinates, or reference frames we use to describe physical phenomena. 3 Such a statement hardly sounds revolutionary, yet its implications are far-reaching because in the theory of relativity time is taken as a coordinate in a four-dimensional geometry of space and time, rather than as a parameter in pre-relativistic physics. 4 Coordinates are essential for making measurements and performing calculations, yet they’re not fundamental—they don’t exist in nature—they’re artifacts of our thinking, what we as humans impose on the world. Therein lies the rub. We need coordinates for practical purposes, yet the goal of physics is to formulate laws of nature as manifestations of an objective reality, that which occurs independently of human beings. The laws of physics should be expressed in a way that’s independent of coordinate system. Relativity is an outgrowth of a single idea, the principle of relativity, that physical laws be independent of the reference frame used to represent them. Relativity is therefore a law about laws. 6 Albert Einstein said: “. . . time and space are modes by which we think, and not conditions in which we live.”[2, p81] The program of relativity is to express equations of physics in such a way that, if true in one system of space-time coordinates, are true in any coordinate system, and thereby transcend coordinates. We will travel far in the theory of relativity in pursuit of this goal, which, as we’ll see, is achieved by expressing equations as relations between tensors, 7 tensors defined on a four-dimensional geometry where time is a dimension.

Postulates

Special Relativity is baded on two postulates that are remarkable for their simplicity yet whose consequences are far-reaching.

  1. The Laws of Physics are the same in any Inertial Frame of Reference.

  2. It follows that any measurement of the speed of any flash of light by an observer in an inertial frame will give the answer c.

The first postulate includes not only Newton's Laws of motion, but also the more recently discovered Maxwell's Equations describing electric and magnetic fields, which predict that light always travels at a speed c = 1/sqrt(ε0μ0), where ε0 and μ0 are the dielectric permittivit and magnetic permeability of vacuum, two constnats that could be measured in the laboratory by permorming experiments that had nothing obvious to do with light.

We have already noted one counter-intuitive consequence of this, that two different observers moving relative to each other, each measuring the speed of the same blob of light relative to himself, will both get c, even if their relative motion is in the same direction as the motion of the blob of light.

Relativity of simultaneity

Einstein pointed out that, when signal transmission cannot be carried out at infinite speed, the simultaneity of two events is a relative concept: two events, that are seen by one observer to be simultanueous are seen by another observed in relative motion to the first to occur at different times.

Time Dilation

An immediate and deep consequence of the second postulate is that time loses it absolute character. Let us consider two frames of references. One M (which may be a trian) that is moving with a velocity v, with respect to another frame of reference S. We imagine the following clock, moving in the reference frame M.

A light ray bounces back and forth between the mirrors, recording one "tick", the times it takes for light to travel the distance d.
In the frame M of the clock, because the clock has no displacement in this frame, the duration of a tick is

t ′ = d c

But for the ground-based observer in S, the time interval between emission and reflection on the mirror will take a longer time as the train is moving, a horizontal distance of v x t, during each of its ticks. From Pythagoras, then,

t2(1 − v2/c2) = d2/c2

and, taking the square root of each side, the time one click takes for the ground-observer is

t = d c 1 1 − v 2 / c 2

because t' = d/c, we can express this result as

t = t ′ 1 − v 2 / c 2

Where $$ \gamma =\frac{1{\sqrt{1-v^2/c^2}} $$ is called the relativistic factor or the Lorentz factor. Since γ is always greater than 1, t > t', that is, time is dilate in the stationary frame of reference S with respect to the moving frame of reference M (or this will be true for any two frames of reference that have a relative velocity with respecto to each other).

Length Contraction

From time dilation it follows length contraction. In the fixed frame of reference the clock is moving with velocity v and so during the time interval t that the light travels from O to the mirror, the point O has moved a distance s = vt. Imagine that you've a stick on your floor that has a length s wich the clock just travels in one click. From the point of view of M, in the time t' the stick passes it, thus the lenght of the stick is s' = vt' = vd/c. Thus we have s' = s/γ a shorter length. This is called length contraction. It is important that length contraction occurs only along the direction of motion, non perpendicular to that direction.

Symmetries in physics

Symmetry in physics means that physics equations remain the same after some definite changes, a symmetry transformation. The symmetry of relativity is a symmetry of physics equations under coordinate transformations. A change of the coordinate system is equivalent to a change of the observer’s reference frame. Special relativity asserts that physics is the same for all inertial observers, that there is no physics observation one can make to distinguish between inertial frames of reference in uniform rectilinear motion. General relativity extends the symmetry to include non-inertial observers in reference frames that may be accelerating with respect to one another or that may include gravity.

Coordinate transformations

For a given coordinate system with a set of orthonormal basis vectors {ei} in three dimensions (so i = 1, 2, 3), a vector—for example, the vector A — can be represented by its components x1, x2, and x3, with {x i} being the coefficients of expansion of A with respect to the basis vectors:

A = ∑ i = 1 3 x i e i = x e 1 + y e 2 + z e 3

With a change of the coordinate basis {ei} → {e'i}, the same vector would have another set of components

A = ∑ i = 1 3 x i ′ e i ′ = x ′ e 1 ′ + y ′ e 2 ′ + z ′ e 3 ′

Thus a coordinate change would bring about the transformation 3 of the vector components as

(x, y, z) ⟶ (x', y', z')

Inertial frames are related to each other by rotation and boost transformations.
We shall first discuss the rotation transformation.

Rotation transformation

Take for example the coordinate transformation that describes a rotation of the coordinate axes by an angle θ around the z axis. The new position components are related to the original ones by relations that can be worked out geometrically from Fig. 1.1; A point P has coordinates (x, y) with respect to the original system and coordinates (x′, y′) with respect to the new system. In the new coordinate system, the point P will appear to have been rotated in the opposite direction, that is, clockwise through the angle θ.
In the xy system, let the point P have polar coordinates (r, α). Then, in the x′y′ system, P will have polar coordinates (r, α − θ).
Using trigonometric functions, we have

x = r cos⁡ α  1.1.1
y = r sin α  1.1.2

and using the standard trigonometric formulae for differences, we have

x′ = r cos⁡ (α − θ) = r cos α cos θ + r sin α sin θ   1.1.3
y′ = r sin⁡ (α − θ) = r sin⁡ α cos⁡ θ − r cos α sin θ   1.1.4

Substituting equations (1.1.1) and (1.1.2) into equations (1.1.3) and (1.1.4), yields

x′ = x cos θ + y sin θ   1.1.5
y′ = −x sin⁡ α cos⁡ θ − y sin θ   1.1.6

Equations (1.1.5) and (1.1.6) can be represented in matrix form as

[ x ′ y ′ ] = [ cos ⁡ θ sin ⁡ θ − sin ⁡ θ cos ⁡ θ ] [ x y ]

Expressed in component notation, this matrix equation becomes

x'i = ∑ni=0 Rij xi = Ri1 x1 + Ri2 x2 + Ri3 x3   1.7

where i and j are the row and column indices, respectively. Namely, the components of any vector should transform under a rotation of coordinate axes in the manner of (1.6). The relation given in (1.4) is a specific example of a rotational transformation, i.e., around a specific axis (z) by a specific amount (θ) with R11 = cos θ and R12 = sin θ, etc.
Rotational invariance is a prototype of a physics symmetry. Having rotational symmetry means that the physics is unchanged under a rotation of coordinates by a fixed angle (N.B., not a rotating coordinate system, in which the coordinate axes have a rotational velocity). Take, for example, the equation of Newton’s second law Fi = mai (i = 1, 2, 3), which is the familiar F = ma equation written in vector-component form. These components are projections onto the coordinate axes. In order for the equation to exhibit rotational symmetry, the validity of Fi = mai in a system O must imply the validity of Fi' = m' a'i in any other system O' that is related to O by a rotation. Since mass m is a scalar (unchanged under a transformation) whereas the vector components of acceleration ai and force Fi transform according to (1.7) (as displayed in Fig. 1.1),

m' = m,   a'i = Σj Rij aj,   Fi' = RijFj

the validity of F'i − m'a'i = 0 follows from Fi – mai = 0, because the non-singular transformation matrix [R] is the same for each set of vector components, {Fi} and {ai}:

F'i − m'a'i = Σj Rij (Fj − maj) = 0

Under a transformation, the components of force and acceleration change values. However, since the corresponding components of force {Fi} and acceleration {ai}, both being vector components, transform in the same way, Fj – maj = 0 implies F'i – m'a'i = 0, and so the physics equation keeps the same form and their relation is not altered. The resultant equation is said to be covariant because all terms in the equation transform in the same way.
Scalars and vectors are the simplest examples of tensors, having no or one index, respectively. A tensor in general is a mathematical object having definite transformation properties under coordinate transformation. Each term in a tensor equation must transform in the same way. A covariant equation maintains its form under transformation. Therefore, if a physics equation can be written in tensor form, it is covariant and automatically respects coordinate (e.g., rotation) symmetry.

Boost transformation

In classical mechanics, the transformation from one inertial frame O with coordinates xi to another inertial frame O' with coordinates x'i is the Galilean transformation. If the relative velocity of the two frames is a constant v and their relative orientations are specified by three angles α, β, and γ, the new coordinates are related to the original ones by xi → x'i = [R]ij xj – vit, where [R] = [R(α, β, γ)] is the rotation matrix discussed above. Here we are mainly interested in coordinate transformations among inertial frames with the same orientation, [R(0, 0, 0)]ij = δij (see Fig. 1.2). Such a transformation is called a (Galilean) boost:

x'i = xi – vit,  1.1.8
t' = t.

In Newtonian physics, the time coordinate is assumed to be absolute; i.e., it is the same in every coordinate frame. As a direct corollary, we have the velocity addition rule, with the velocity components ui = dxi /dt and u'i = dx'i /dt' related by

u'i = ui – vi, 1.1.9

which is obtained, because t' = t, by differentiation on both sides of (1.1.8) by d/dt' = d/dt.
