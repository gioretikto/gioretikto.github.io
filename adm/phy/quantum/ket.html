In quantum mechanics a physical state - is represented by a state vector in a complex vector space, the Hilber space. Following Dirac, we call such a vector a ket and denote it by |α>. This state key is postulated to contain complete information about the physical state; everything we are allowed to ask about the state is contained in the ket. Two kets can be added:

|α> + |β> = |γ>

The sum |γ> is just another ket. If we multiply |α> by a complex number c, the resulting product c|α> is another ket. The number c can stand on the left or on the right of a ket; it makes no difference:

c|α> = |α>c

In the particular case where c is zero, the resulting ket is said to be a null ket.

One of the physics postulates is that |α> and |α> with c≠ 0 represent the same physical state. In other words, only the "direction" in vector space is of significance.

An observable, such as momentum and spin components, can be represented by an operator, such as A, in the vector space in question. Quite generally, an operator acts on a ket from the left.

We'll see in the following discussion that

  1. Physically observables quantities are represented by Hermitian operators

  2. The eigenvalues of such operators correspond to the possible values of measurements.

Observables must be represented by Hermitian operators because the eigenvalues of Hermitian operators are real, and physical measurements always yield real numbers - never imaginary or complex numbers.

Consider a measuremente of the position of a particle. Before the measurement is made the particle wavefunction is a superposition of several position eigenfunctions, each corresponding to a different possible position for the particle. When the measurement is made the wavefunction collapses into one of these eigenfunctions, with a probability determined by the compoistion of the original superposition. One particular position will be recorded by the measurement: the one corresponding to the eigenfunction chosen by the particle. If there are no further interaction between the quantum system and its surroundings, another position measurement would yield the same eigenvalue. However if a measurement of the momentum of the particle is now made, the particle wavefunction will change to one of the momentum eigenfunctions (which are not the same as the position eigenfunctions). With respect to position, the wavefunction is in a superposition of position states after the momentum measurement and only the probability of obtaining a particular position measurement would be known. Since the momentum has just been set by the momentum measurement, all values of position measurement are equally likely in correspondence with the Heisenberg uncertainty principle.
