Spin-orbit coupling

An electron is a chraged particle, so its orbital angular momentum gives rise to a magnetic field, just as an electric current in a loop gives rise to a mangetic field in an electromagnet. That is, an electron with orbital angular momentum acts like a tiny bar magnet. An electron also has a sping angular momentum, and this intrinsic 'spinning motion' menas that is also acts as a tiny bar magnet. The magnet arising from the spin interacts with the magnet arising from the orbital motion and gives rise to the interatction called spin-orbit coupling.
The two magnets have a higher energy when they are parallel than when they are antiparallel. The total angular momentum J is the sum of the orbital angular momentum L and the spin angular momentum S, J = L + S.

The total orbital angular momentum quantum number, L, tells us the magnitude of the angular momentum through {L(L + 1)}1/2 ℏ. It has 2L + 1 orientations distinguished by the quantum number ML, which can take the values L, L − 1, ..., −L. Similar remarks apply to the total spin quantum number, S, and the quantum number MS, and the total angular momentum quantum number, J, and the quantum number MJ.
The value of L (a non-negative integer) is obtained by coupling the individual orbital angular momenta by using the Clebsch–Gordan series:

Therefore, because the relative orientation of the magnets reflects the relative orientation of the orbital and spin angular momenta, the energy of the atom depends on the total angular momentum quantum number J (because its value also reflects the relative orientation of the two kinds of momentum). Spin-orbit coupling is a relativistic effect that may be visualized as the action of an effective magnetic field seen by the elctrons while orbiting around the charged nuclei. Seen from the electron, relative motion of the nucleus in an atom corresponds to a ring current that produces a magnetic field along which the electron spin tends to align. The effect increases linearly with teh orbital momentum of the electron, but much more strongly (to the fourth power) with the nuclear charge.
