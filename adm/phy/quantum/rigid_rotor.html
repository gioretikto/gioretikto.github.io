The Rigid Rotator

Consider a particle of mass m that is free to move anywhere on the surface of a sphere of radius r. The potential energy of the particle is the same at all angles and for convenience may be taken to be zero (in an atom, the potential due to the nucleus is constant at a givem radius). The Schrödinger equation is therefore

− ℏ 2 2 m ( ∂ 2 ∂ x 2 + ∂ 2 ∂ y 2 + ∂ 2 ∂ z 2 ) ψ = E ψ

The sum of the three second derivatives is the "laplacian" and denoted ∇2. The the equation becomes

−(ℏ2/2m)∇2ψ = Eψ

To take advantage of the symmetry of the problem it is appropriate to change to spherical coordinates when the laplacian becomes

∇ 2 = 1 r ∂ 2 ∂ r 2 r + 1 r 2 Λ 2

Where the derivative with respect to θ and φ have been collected in Λ2 which is called the "legendrian" and is given by

Λ 2 = 1 sin 2 ⁡ θ ∂ 2 ∂ ϕ 2 + 1 sin ⁡ θ ∂ ∂ θ sin ⁡ θ ∂ ∂ θ

In the present case, r is fixed, so the derivatives with respect to r in the laplacian can be ignored and only the term Λ2/r2 survives. The Schrödinger equation then becomes

−(ℏ2/2mr2)Λ2ψ(θφ) = Eψ(θφ)

The term mr2 in the denominator can be recognized as the moment of inertia, I, of the particle, so the Schrödinger equation is

−(ℏ2/2I)Λ2ψ(θφ) = Eψ(θφ)

The function known as spherical harmonics, Yl,ml(θφ), are well known to mathematics and are the solutions of the equation

Λ2Yl,ml(θφ) = −l(l + 1)Yl,ml(θφ)   l = 0,1,2, .., ml = 0, ±1, ..., ±l

These functions satisfy teh two boundary conditions and are normalized in the sense that

∫ ϕ = 0 2 π Y l , m l ∗ ( θ , ϕ ) Y l , m l ( θ , ϕ ) sin ⁡ θ d θ d ϕ = 1

We can show by substitution that the spherical harmonics solve the Schrödinger equation

−(ℏ2/2m)Λ2Yl,ml(θφ) = l(l + 1)(ℏ2/2m)Yl,ml(θφ)   l = 0,1,2, .., ml = 0, ±1, ..., ±l

The spherical harmonics are therefore solutions of the Schrödinger equation with energies E = l(l + 1)ℏ2/2I. Note that the energies depend only on l but not on ml.
