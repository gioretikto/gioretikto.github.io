The Blackbody

The concept of a blackbody was introduced by Kirchhoff, who defined it to be a system which absorbs all EM radiation incident upon it. All bodies in the universe radiate EM energy of a spectrum of frequencies. Meaurement of this spectrum of frequencies shows two main things. First, for very low and very high frequencies the intensity of the radiation is low and at intermediate frequencies there is a frequency at which the intensity is at a maximum, νm. Secondly, the maximum νm changes as the temperature changes. When T is increased νm increases. Wien, also using thermodynamic reasoning, was able to demonstrate the so-called displacement law:

u(ν,Т) = ν3φ(Т/ν)  (1.1)

To determine the form of the function φ(T/ν), Wien supposed, folfollowing Michelson, that Maxwell's formula for the velocity distribution of molecules in an ideal gas was also applicable to the molecules of a solid which behaves as a blackbody; he further assumed that the wavelength and intensity of the radiation emitted by a molecule are completely determined by its velocity, thus enabling him to establish that

u(ν,Т) = aν3e(−βν/T) (1.2)

where α and β are universal constants. This formula is known as Wien's law. Even though we now know that the hypotheses which led to (1.2) are incorrect, the results obtained were able to account for the experimental data which existed at that time. In a series of new experiments, Paschen and Paschen and Wanner confirmed the validity of the above law for the range of frequencies corresponding to visible light (ν ~ 1015 s−1) and for temperatures up to 4000 K. The first discrepancies between Wien's law and experimental results were found by Lummer and Pringsheim, who, while studying radiation emission of wavelengths in the range 12-18 μm (ν ~ 1013 s−1), discovered that Wien's law is violated as frequency is decreased. A new attempt at obtaining u(ν, T) theoretically was carried out by Lord Rayleigh [RA 00]. Thus at the beginning of this century, two expressions were available, one valid at high frequencies (Wien's law) and another for low frequencies (the Rayleigh-Jeans law); it was necessary to find an expression for u(ν, T) which contained both of these as limiting cases. The first step towards obtaining the correct formula was made by Planck. Making use of thermodynamic arguments and of an ad hoc assumption in order to come up with an interpolation formula, he arrived at the celebrated expression which is now known as Planck's law, and which in modern notation is written

In that same work, Planck clearly established that classical dynamics, even together with relativity theory, constitutes a framework which is too narrow to explain microscopic phenomena.
