Stationary States

Per ottenere la Ψ(x,t) è necessario risolvere l'equazione di Schrödinger

i ℏ ∂ ∂ t ψ ( x , t ) = − ℏ 2 2 m ∇ 2 ψ ( x , t ) + V Ψ

for a specified potential V(x,t). It is tiresome to keep saying "potential energy function", so most people just call V the "potential", even though this invites occasional confusion with "electric" potential, which is actually potential energy per unit charge.

In this section we shall assume that V is independent of t. In this case the Schrödinger equation can be solved by separation of variables, We look for solutions that are simple products

Ψ(x,t) = ψ(x)φ(t)

ψ (lower-case) is a function of x alone, and φ is a function of t alone. At the end we will able to patch together the separable solutions in such way as to construct the most general solution. Substitution into Schrödinger equation, yields

1 ϕ ( t ) i ℏ ∂ ∂ t ϕ ( t ) = 1 ψ ( x ) H ϕ ( x )

since the left-hand side depends only on t and the right-hand side only on x, both sides can be equated to a constant that we should call E. For the left side we have

i ℏ ∂ ∂ t ϕ ( t ) = E ϕ ( t )

which is easy to solve, just multiply through dt and integrate to yield the solution

φ(t) = exp(−iEt/ℏ)

and for the side with spatial dependence

Ĥψ(x) = Eψ(x) (1.7.1)

with Ĥ,

H ^ = − ℏ 2 2 m d 2 d x 2 + V ( x )

The last equation is kwown as time-independent Schrödinger equation; we can go no further untill the potential V(x) is specified; Separation of variables has turned a partial differential equation into two ordinary differential equations. The next sections will be devoted to solving the time-independent Schrödinger equation, for a variety of simple potentials. But before I get to that you have every right to ask: What's so great about separable solutions? After all, most solutions to the (time depenndet) Schrödinger equation do not take the form ψ(x)φ(t). I offer three anwers - two of them physical, and one mathematical:

They are stationary states, Although the wave function itself,

Ψ(x,t) = ψ(x)e-iEt/ℏ

does (obviously) depend on t, the probability density,

|Ψ(x,t)|2 = Ψ*Ψ = ψ*e+iEt/ℏ ψe-iEt/ℏ =|ψ(x)|2

does not - the time-dependence cancel out. The same thing happens in calculating the expectation value of any dynamical variable; Every expectation value is constant in time; we might as well drop the factor f(t) altogether, and simply use ψ in place of Ψ (Indeed, it is common to refer to ψ as "the wave function" but this is sloppy language that can be dangerous, and it is important to remember that the true wave function always carries that exponential time-dependent factor). In particular <x> is constant, and hence <p>. Nothing ever happens in a stationary state.

They are states of definite total energy. In classical mechanics, the total energy (kinetic plus potential) is called the Hamiltonian.

A lot has happened in the past four pages, so let me recapitulate, from a somewhat different perspective. Here’s the generic problem: You’re given a (time-independent) potential V(x), and the starting wave function Ψ(x, 0); your job is to find the wave function, Ψ(x, t), for any subsequent time t. To do this you must solve the (time-dependent) Schrödinger equation . The strategy is first to solve the time-independent Schrödinger equation; this yields, in general, an infinite set of solutions, {ψn (x)}, each with its own associated energy, {En}. To fit Ψ(x, 0) you write down the general linear combination of these solutions:

Ψ(x, 0) = Σn cnψn(x)

the miracle is that you can always match the specified initial state by appropriate choice of the constants {cn }. To construct Ψ(x, t) you simply tack onto each term its characteristic time dependence (its “wiggle factor”), exp (−iEnt/ℏ):

Ψ ( x , t ) = ∑ n = 1 ∞ c n ψ n ( x ) e − i E n t / ℏ = ∑ n = 1 ∞ c n Ψ n ( x ) ( x , t )

The separable solutions themselves,

Ψn (x, t) = ψn (x)e−iEnt/ℏ

are stationary states, in the sense that all probabilities and expectation values are independent of time, but this property is emphatically not shared by the general solution (Equation 2.17): the energies are different, for different stationary states, and the exponentials do not cancel, when you construct |Ψ|2.
