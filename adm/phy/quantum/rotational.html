Rotational Spectra

Rotational energy, along with all other forms of molecular energy, is quantized; this means that molecule cannot have any arbitrary amount of rotational energy (i.e. any arbitrary value of angular momentum) but its energy is limited to certain definite values depending on the shape and size of the molecule concerned. The permitted energy value − so-called rotational energy levels − may in principle be calculated for any molecule by solving the Schrödinger equation for the system represented by that molecule. For simple molecules the mathematics involved is straightforward for tedious, while for complicated systems it is probably impossible without gross approximations. We shall not concent ourselves unduly with this, however, being content merely to accept the results of existing solutions and to point out where reasonable approximations may lead.
We shall consider each class of rotating molecule in turn, discussing the linear molecule in most detail, because much of its treatment can be irectly extended to symmetrical and unsymetrical molecules.

Diatomic molecules

The rigid Diatomic molecules

The simplest of all linear molecules, is composed of masses m1 and m2 joined by a rigid bar (the bond) whose length is

r0 = r1 + r2 (2.1)

The molecule rotates end-over-end about a point C, the centre of gravity; this is defined by the moment, or balancing, equation

m1 r1 = m2r2  (2.2)

The moment of inertia about C is defined by

I = m1 r1r2 + m2r1r2
= m1 r1r2 = m2r1r2  (from Eq. 2.2)
= r1r2(m1m2)

However, from Eq. (2.1) and 2.2:

m1r1 = m2r2 = m2(r0 − r1)

Therefore,

r 1 = m 2 r 0 m 1 + m 2 r 2 = m 1 r 0 m 1 + m 2 2.3

Replacing (2.3) in (2.2):

I = m 1 m 2 m 1 + m 2 r 0 2 = μ r 0 2 2.4

where we have written μ = m1m2/(m1 + m2) and μ is called the reduced mass of the system. Equation 2.4 defines the moment of inertia conveniently in terms of the atomic masses and the bond length.
By the use of the Schrödinger equation it may be shown that the rotational energy levels allowed to the rigid diatomic molecule are given by the expression:

E J = h 2 8 π 2 I J ( J + 1 ) J = 0 , 1 , 2 , …

h is Plank's constant, and I is the moment of inertia, either Ib or IC, since both are equal. The quantity J, which can take integral values from zero upwards, is called the rotational quantum number. We are usually interested in the frequencies related to transitions that we can express as ν = ΔE /h Hz, or wavenumber, ν̄ = ΔE /hc cm−1, of the radiation emitted or absorbed as a consequence of changes between energy levels. In the rotational region spectra are usually discussed in terms of wavenumbers, so it is useful to consider energies expressed in these units. We write:

ε J = E J h c = h 2 8 π 2 I c J ( J + 1 ) cm − 1 J = 0 , 1 , 2 , …

This equation can be abbreviated to

εJ = BJ(J + 1) cm−1 (J = 0, 1, 2, ..)

where B = h/8π2IBc, the rotational constant. In which we have used the moment of inertia IB. We might equally well have used IC.

As J increases in principle, there is no limit to the rotational energy the molecule may have. In practice, of course, there comes a point at which the centrifugal force of a rapidly rotating diatomic molecules is greater than the strength of the bond, and the molecule is disrupted, but this point is not reached at normal temperatures.

We now need to consider differences between the levels in order to discuss the spectrum. A rather sophisticated application of Schrödinger equation show that, for this molecule, we need only consider transitions in which J changes by one unit − all other transitions being spectropically forbidden. Such a result is called a selection rule, and we may formulate it for the rigid diatomic rotator as:

Selection rule:  Δ = ±1

If we imagine the molecule to be in the J = 0 state (the ground rotational state, in which no rotation occurs), we can let incident radiation be absorbed to raise it to the J = 1 state. Plainly the energy absorbed will be:

εJ = 1 − εJ=0 = 2B − 0 = 2B cm−1

and, therefore,

ν̄J = 0 ⟶ J = 1 = 2B    cm−1

In other words, an absorption line will appea at 2B cm−1. If now the molecule is raised from the J = 1 to the J = 2 level by the absorption of more energy, we see immediately:

ν̄J = 1 ⟶ J = 2 = 6B − 2B = 4B  cm−1

Thus a stepwise raising of the rotational energy results in an absorption spectrum consisting of line 2B,4B,6B, ... cm−1, while a similar lowering would result in an identical emission spectrum.
