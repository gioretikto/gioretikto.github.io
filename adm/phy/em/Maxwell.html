Maxwell's equations

In free space, Maxwell's equations take the form.

∇ ⋅ E = 0  8.1.1

∇ ⋅ B = 0 8.1.2

∇ × B = 1 c 2 ∂ E ∂ t  8.1.3 ∇ × E = − ∂ B ∂ t  8.1.4

where we have defined

1/c2 = μ0ε0

Our goal is to find a form of the equations in which the fields E and B appear separately, and not together in the same equation. As a first step, we take the curl of both sides of Eq. (8.1.4), and interchange the order of differentiation on the right-hand side (which we are allowed to do, since the space and time coordinates are independent). We obtain

∇ × ∇ × E = − ∂ ∂ t ∇ × B

Substituting for ∇ × B from Eq. (8.1.3), this becomes

∇ × ∇ × E = − 1 c 2 ∂ 2 E ∂ t 2

This second-order differential equation involves only the electric field E so we have achieved our aim of decoupling the field equations. However, it is possible to make a further simplification, using a mathematical identity. For any differentiable vector field a

∇ x ∇ x a ≡ ∇(∇⋅a) − ∇2 a

Using this identity 1.12.3, and also making use of Eq. (8.1.1), we obtain finally

∇ 2 E = − 1 c 2 ∂ 2 E ∂ t 2 = 0

Thi equation is the wave equation in three spatial dimensions. Note that each component of the electric field independently satisfies the wave equation. The solution, representing a plane wave propagating in the direction of the vector k, may be written in the form

E = E0 cos (k ⋅ r − ωt + φ0) (8.1.4)

where E0 is a constant vector, φ0 is a constant phase, ω and k are constants related to the frequency f and wavelength λ of the wave by

ω = 2πf
λ = 2π/|k|

Wave equation for the magnetic field

So far, we have only considered the electric field. But Maxwell’s equation (8.1.3) tells us that an electric field that varies with time must have a magnetic field associated with it. Therefore, we should look for a (non-trivial) solution for the magnetic field in free space. Starting with Eq. (8.1.3), and following the same procedure as above, we find that the magnetic field also satisfies the wave equation:

∇ 2 B = − 1 c 2 ∂ 2 B ∂ t 2 = 0

with a similar solution:

B = B0 cos (k ⋅ r − ωt + φ0)  (8.1.5)

Here, we have written the same constants ω, k, and φ0 as we used for the electric field, though we do not so far know they have to be the same. We shall show in the following section that these constants do indeed need to be the same for both the electric field and the magnetic field.

Relations between electric and magnetic fields in a plane wave in free space

As we commented above, although taking additional derivatives of Maxwell’s equations allows us to decouple the equations for the electric and magnetic fields, we must impose additional constraints on the solutions to ensure that the first-order equations are satisfied. In particular, substituting the expressions for the fields (8.1.4) and (8.1.5) into Eqs. (8.1.1) and (8.1.2), respectively, and noting that the latter equations must be satisfied at all points in space and at all times, we obtain

∇ ⋅ E = ∇ ⋅ E0 cos (k ⋅ r − ωt + φ0) = k ⋅ E0 cos (k ⋅ r − ωt + φ0)

So the following two conditions must be satisfied

E0 ⋅ k

B0 ⋅ k

Finally, substituting the expressions for the fields (8.1.4) and (8.1.5) into Eqs. (8.1.3) and (8.1.4), respectively, and again noting that the latter equations must be satisfied at all points in space and at all times, we see first that the quantities ω, k, and φ0 appearing in (8.1.4) and (8.1.5) must be the same in each case. Also, we have the following relations between the magnitudes and directions of the fields:

k x E0 = ωB0
k x B0 = −ωE0
