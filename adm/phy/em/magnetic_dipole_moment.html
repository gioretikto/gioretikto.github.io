The Magnetic Dipole Moment

As we have just discussed, a torque acts to rotate a current-carrying coil placed in a magnetic field. In that sense, the coil behaves like a bar magnet placed in the magnetic field. Thus, like a bar magnet, a current-carrying coil is said to be a magnetic dipole. A magnetic dipole can be theoretically described as an elementary pair of magnetic poles. But in electromagnetism, the magnetic pole in only a hyphotheical concept that has never been observed experimentally.

The torque, τ, in newon meters (N m ), exerted on a magnetic dipole of moment m put in a magnetic induction B is then given by the following equation

τ = μ x B = -μ0 m x H

which reminds us very much of the corresponding equation for the torque exerted by an electric field on an electric dipole-namely, Eq. x:

τ = p x E

this means that the magnetic induction tries to aling the dipole so that its moment lies parallel to the induction. In other words, the magnetic induction tries to aling the current loop such that the field produced by the current loop is parallel to it. If no frinctional forces are present, the work done by the turning force will be conserved. This gives rise to the following expression for the energy of interaction of the dipole moment m in the presence of the magnetic induction B

Epot(θ) = −m ⋅ B = μ0 m ⋅ H

This energy term is also known as the Zeeman energy. When the moment and induction are aligned parallel (θ = 0°), Epot = – m ⋅ B0, is a minimum. When the moment and field are orthogonal (θ = 90°), Epot = 0, is a maximum. In between, Epot = – m ⋅ B0 cosθ.

Orbital magnetic dipole moment

When it is in an atom, an electron has an additional angular momentum called orbital angular momentum, Lorb and associated with it, there is an orbital magnetic dipole moment μorb;

Consider an electron in orbit with angular velocity, ω. This is equivalent to a current, i = −eω/2π, and a magnetic moment of magnitude μorb = −eωr2/2. The orbital angular momentum of the electron has a magnitude, |L| = r mev = mer2ω, such that

μorb = −e/2me Lorb

The minus sign means that μorb and Lorb have opposite directions.

Orbital angular momentum cannot be measured (as well as μorb); only its component along any axis can be measured, and that component is quantized. The component along, say, a z aix can have only the values given by

Lorb,z = mlℏ,   ml = 0,±1,±2,..

in which ml is called the orbital magnetic quantum number and "limit" refers to some largest allowed integer value for ml.

We can write the z component μorb,z of the orbital magnetic dipole moment as

μorb,z = -ml eh/(4πm)

and, in term of the Bohr magneton, as

μorb,z = -mlμB
