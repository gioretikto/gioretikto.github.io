Capacitive Reactance

A circuit containing an ideal capacitor of capacitance C connected with a source of alternating emf is shown in Fig.1

The AC source supplies an alternating voltage v = V0sin (ωt). When alternating emf is applied across the capacitor a similarly varying alternating current flows in the circuit. The resistance of a circuit like this can be made so small that it has a negligible effect compared with the capacitor, and so we can assume negligible resistance. The two plates of the capacitor become alternately positively and negatively charged and the magnitude of the charge on the plates of the capacitor varies sinusoidally with time. Also

considering the instantaneous values q, v = V0sin (ωt) and knowing that

q = Cv

Differentiating w.r.t. time

dq / dt = C dv/dt
i = C dv/dt

Hence instantaneous current is proportional to the rate of change of voltage. Now, since v = V0sin (ωt)

i = C d d t V 0 sin ⁡ ω t

Thus

i = C ω V 0 cos ⁡ ω t = C ω V 0 sin ⁡ ( ω t + π 2 )

The current at t = 0, corresponds to I0 = ωCV0, so we can as well express the previous as

i = I 0 sin ⁡ ( ω t + π 2 )

In a circuit containing only capacitance (purely capacitive), the current and voltage are 90° out of phase.

In a resistive circuit, the resistance is due to the obstruction to the passage of electron through the resistor. But in a capacitive circuit, resistance to the flow of current is due to the charged capacitor.

V0 / I0 = 1/ωC = XC

The term XC, is called capacitive creactance (Ω). So the rms current i in the circuit containing only a capacitor C is given by another version of Ohm’s law to be

Irms = I0/√2 = V0/XC√2 = Vrms/XC

The above relation is similar to Ohm's law. The capacitive reactance is the opposition to alternating current by capacitance. The opposition tendered depends upon the rate of change of voltage across the capacitor, in formulae by

XC ≡ 1/2πωC

XC is inversely proportional to the capacitance C; the larger the capacitor, the greater the charge it can store and the greater the current that can flow. It is also inversely proportional to the frequency ω; the greater the frequency, the less time there is to fully charge the capacitor, and so it impedes current less.
