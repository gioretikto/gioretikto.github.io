Inductors

When a current is passed through a coil of wire, a magnetic field is generated. An inductor is a circuit component that is made of a conducting wire wrapped around a core in the form of a coil. There are different types of inductors that can be categorized base on the material composition of their core, which can be made of iron or ferrite, air, or any nonmagnetic material.
The current I in the circuit rises continuously from zero to its final value. This changing magnetic field acts just like a magnet being moved around near the coil − it induces another current in the coil. While the current is rising, an induced emf ℰL = dΦB/dt = −L(ΔI/Δt) appears across the inductor. This effect, in which a coil induces current in itself, is called self induction. The presence of an inductor impeeds the current to change instantaneously.

Although this result was obtained for the special case of a long solenoid, it is quite general and is valid for any point where a magnetic field exists in air or vacuum or in a nonmagnetic material. Thus, energy can be stored in a magnetic field, just as it can in an electric field.
