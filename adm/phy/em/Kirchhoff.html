Kirchhoff's laws

L'analisi dei circuiti si basa su due postulati fondamentali che prendono il nome dal fisico tedesco Gustav Robert Kirchhoff (1824 - 1887). The laws of conservation of energy and charge have been restated in a form suitable to lumped parameter circuits. Gustav Robert Kirchhoff arrived at the required restatements of these conservation laws in 1857: the junction rule and the loop rule. La loro formulazione richiede due convetti topologici, quello di nodo e quello di maglia.

The juction rule (it. legge dei nodi) states that, for any node (junction) in an electrical circuit, the sum of currents flowing into that node is equal to the sum of currents flowing out of that node; un nodo è un punto nel quale convergono almeno tre correnti. This juction rule is an application of the law of conservation of electric charge to the electric current in a circuit, since current intensity is but a measure of electrons passing through the wire cross section per unit time.

The algebraic sum of currents in a network of conductors meeting at a point is zero.

Recalling that current is a signed (positive or negative) quantity reflecting direction towards or away from a node, this principle can be succinctly stated as:

∑ k = 0 n ik = 0

Si tratta di una generalizzazione della (5.7 i1 = i2) conseguenza del principio di conservazione della carica.

Kirchhoff's voltage law

This law is also called Kirchhoff's second law, Kirchhoff's loop (or mesh) rule, and Kirchhoff's second rule. Una maglia è un cammino chiuso. This law states that

The directed sum of the potential differences (voltages) around any closed loop is zero.

Similarly to Kirchhoff's current law, the voltage law can be stated as:

∑k Vk = 0

It is a consequence of law of conservation of energy as voltage is energy or work per unit charge and the voltage drop is the energy, expended in transporting a unit charge through the electric field that exists inside the component. The law, then, says that the net energy change for a unit charge transported around a closed path is zero. The energy gained by the charge in the source of emf may decrease in the form of a potential drop −IR across a resistor.
