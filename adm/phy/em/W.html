Electric potential and Electric potential energy

Since work W is the component of force in the direction of movement times the distance traveled, there is no work done moving perpendicular to the field; as the electrical potential will be unchanged along this line, there is no change in the energy of the system.
The elementary work dW that is done by the electroc field E in moving a charge q by a displacement dℓ under the influence of the force Fe = qE is equal to

dW = Fe ⋅ dℓ = qE dℓ cos α

where α is the angle between the electric field vector E and the direction of the charge motion. This equation can be used to find the work performed by the electric field in moving charge q from point 1 to point 2 in the field generated by another stationary charge Q. Since the electric field of a point charge is E = (kQ/r2)er and erdℓ = dℓ cos α = dr, the total work done along the whole path is

From it follows that the work of the electrostatic field W12 does not depend on the shape of the path that the charge q follows. This work depends on the difference between the initial r1 and the final r2, positions of the charge q. In such a field, the work performed to move the charge q along any closed contour is equal to zero, i.e.

q ∮ E ⋅ dl = 0

The field is called a conservative field if the work along a closed contour is equal to zero; thus, the electrostatic field is a conservative field.
We know that for a conservative force F a scalar function U known as potential is defined and that

F = −∇U

Then we have that the work depends solely on the values of U at B and A:

W = U(B) − U(A)  1.1.1

The work W12 done by the electric field in moving a charge q is given by the difference between the potential energies at the beginning and at the end of the path, that is

W 12 = k q Q ( 1 r 1 − 1 r 2 ) = q ( k Q r 1 − k Q r 2 ) = q ( V 1 − V 2 ) = U 1 − U 2

Here, we have introduced the electric potential V of the charge Q: V = kQ/r. The electric potential is equal to the electric potential energy U of a probe charge q at a given point of the field divided by the value of this charge: V = U/q. Only differences of potential energies have physical meaning. As is true for gravitational potential energy, there is no absolute zero level of electric potential energy. If all charges were separated so that they were at infinite distance from one another, then the total electrical potential energy would be zero. Therefore, it is convenient to choose the potential energy of a charge at infinity to be zero. If r2 ⟶ ∞, we have

W1∞ = qV = U1 = kqQ/r1

This equation determines the work performed by the electric field in moving charge q from point r1 to infinity. The work is positive if both charges are of the same sign as the potential enegy U∞ of the charge q equals zero when charge q is at the infinite distance from the charge Q. It is worth noting here that if we bring charge q from infinity to point r1 we need to perform work against the electric fields, that is, W∞1 is negative if both charges are of the same sign: W∞1 = −W1∞.
To conclude, the potential energy of interaction between charges q and Q at a distance r is equal to

U = qV = kqQ/r

where the electric potential of a point charge Q at a distance r from that charge is V = kQ/r. The unit of electric potential in the SI is called volt (V). We could have considered directtly the scalar potential V, associated with the conservative vector field E:
