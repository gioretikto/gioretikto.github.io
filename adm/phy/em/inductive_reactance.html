Inductive Reactance

Let the inductor have no resistance. Thus, any voltage that appears across the terminals of the inductor must be due to the self-induced voltage in the coil by a changing current through it (self-inductance).
The changing current output of the generator produces a back emf that impedes the current in the circuit. The magnitude of this back emf is

At any instant, v = vL by Kirchhoff's voltage law, so

v L = L d I d t = V 0 sin ⁡ ω t

di/dt = (V0/L) sin(ωt)
i = (V0/L) ∫ sin(ωt) dt

Integrating the previous equation we get the current

i = (−V0/ωL) cos(ωt)
= (V0/ωL) sin(ωt − π/2)

Now, i will be a maximum I0 when sin(ωt − π/2) = 1.

Irms = I0/√2 = V0/XL√2 = Vrms/XL

The effective resistance of the coil in an AC circuit is measured by a quantity called the inductive reactance, XL:

XL ≡ 2πfL

The current reaches its maximum after the voltage does, and it is said that the current in an inductor lags behind the voltage by a phase of 90°.
In an inductor the 90° phase difference between current and voltage leads to the same reult for averge power that it does in a capacitor. An inductor alternately absorbs and releases energy for equal periods in time.

When f is in hertz and L is in henries, the unit of XL is the ohm. The inductive reactance increases with increasing frequency and increasing inductance. Contrast this with capacitors, where increasing frequency or capacitance decreases the capacitive reactance.
