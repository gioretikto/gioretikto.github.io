Magnetic Induction or Flux Density

When matter is placed in the region surrounding a current-carrying conductor, the magnetic field in the space around the conductor is different from that which exists when the conductor is in a vacuum due to the magnetisation of the material. The presence of material may concentrate the field.

This behaviour arises due to the interaction of the externally and the internal field generated by the orbiting (and also spinning) electrons within the material. Now, a magnetic field can be described by either of two vectors:

  • The magnetic induction B, (teslas, (Wb m−2)

  • The magnetic field intensity H (amperes⋅metre−1

B = μ0 H,    (μ0 permeability of free space (i.e., a vacuum) μ0 = 4π x 10−7 Wb A−1 m−1

B and H have different physical significance.

The magnetic induction B is a field vector which determines the magnetic force acting on a moving charged particle. a magnetic “effect”
The magnetic field intensity H is also a field vector vector, but describes the magnetic field generated by a moving charged particle. a magnetic “cause”

The constant μ0 shows that the resulting magnetic induction B produced by an external source of magnetic field (such as a permanent magnet or moving charge) depends upon the “ease” with which the surrounding material permits the creation of magnetic field lines.
μ0 is called the permeability of the material. Those materials which tend to facilitate the setting-up of magnetic field lines have a higher permeability than others.

Magnetic induction, B, also called the flux density, or flux per unit area, is defined as the response of the medium to a magnetic field, H. It is worth mentioning, at the outset, that there is a general lack of unanimity in the literature regarding the names for B and H, but not on what the symbols represent; for there is uniform agreement regarding the relationship between, B, H, and the magnetization, M. The ratio between B and H is called the permeability, μ of the material

B = μ H

In SI units, free space is also considered a medium with permeability, μ0 (the origin of this is explained below).

B = μ0 H

The unit of magnetic induction is the Tesla = [Wb m–2] = [V s m–2] and is defined in terms of the Lorentz force it imparts on a straight current wire, ℓ, carrying a current, i

F = iℓ x B

Definition: A magnetic induction of 1 Tesla imparts a force of 1 N on a 1 m length of conductor carrying a current of 1 A in a direction perpendicular to the direction of induction.

Magnetic Flux

If B the magnetic induction at a small patch of vector area dS, then the scalar product B⋅dS is defined as the magnetic flux dφ through the patch of area dS. That is

dφ = B ⋅dS

The magnetic flux through the entire designated are S of which dS is one of patch is,

φ = ∫S B ⋅dS

If B is measured in teslas (T) or webers per square meter (W/m2), the the magnetic flux should be in webers.

Relative permeability μr

Generally the permeability of different materials is defined relative to the permeability of free space μ0. The relative permeability is defined as the ratio of flux density, produced in a medium (other than free space) to the flux density produced in free space, under the influence of same magnetic field strenght and under identical conditions.

Thus if the magnetic field strenght is H which is producing flux density B in the medium while flux density B0 in free space then the relative permeability is defined as the ratio of the moduli of magnetic induction fields in the medium and free space

μr = B/B0   where H is the same

It is dimensionless and has no units. For free space, vacum μr = 1.

equivantely

μ = μrμ0

for example if relative permeability of the iron is 1000 means it is 1000 times more magnetic than the free space of air.

We will see later that the magnetic permeability of certain materials (i.e., diamagnetic and ferromagnetic materials) is constant over a wide range of values of magnetic field, while for other materials (e.g., ferromangetic and ferrimagnetic materials), the permeability varies considerably with the applied magnetic field. Hence the magnetic induction B is no longer a linear function of the magnetic field H.

If an electric current, i = 2A, circulates in a long toroidal solenoid with a number N = 1000 coils each having a diameter D of 10 cm, the magnetic field inside the solenoid is given by

H = Ni/2πr = Ni/πD = 6367 A ⋅ m–1

If the solenoid has just an air core (μair ≌ μ0), the resulting magnetic induction will be

B = μairH ≌ 0.08T

By contrast, if the solenoid has a core made of a soft magnetic material such as a rod of pure iron e.g., Armco iron) with a relative magnetic permeability of μr ≌ 1000, the magnetic induction will be greatly increased as follows

B = μair>H ≌ 8T

This example clearly shows how important it is for core materials to have a hing magnetic permeability for the manufacture of electromagnets because they provide high magnetic induction with moderate magnetic fields.

Magnetic susceptibility and Magnetization

The difference of the induction field due to the presence of the medium is

Bm = B − B0 = (μr − 1)B0

We define the magnetic susceptibility as the quantity

χm = (μr − 1)

hence we can write

Bm = χm B0 = μ0χmH

We define the mangetization vector as

M = χmH

The relationship between B, H, and M for linear isotropic media where a magnetic susceptibility χm can be defined is:

B = B0 + Bm = μ0(H + M)

The intensity of the magnetic induction field in the solenoid is equal to

B = μ0ni + μ0χmni

where the term μ0ni is the magnetic field produced by the current circulating in the solenoid. The second term can be assumed as the effect due to a solenoid equal to the first but with a circulating current equal to μ0χmi. As we will see this current is not fictitious. The current, however, is due to the movement of bound charges (orbital electrons, electron spin, and nuclear spin) that do not convey any flow of charge across the body, and the field generated, which has the dimensions of H, will be called the magnetization M. The current produced by the bound charges is called a bound current or Amperian current.

Anisotropic Materials

Just as we found for anisotropic dielectric materials, the permeability of an anisotropic magnetic material must be given as a 3 x 3 matrix, while B and H are both 3 x 1 matrices. We have

Bx = μxx Hx + μxy Hy + μxz Hz

By = μyx Hx + μyy Hy + μyz Hz

Bz = μzx Hx + μzy Hy + μzz Hz

For anisotropic materials, then, B = μH is a matrix equation; however B = μ0(H + M) remains valid, although B, H, and M are no longer parallel in general. The most common anisotropic magnetic material is a single ferromagnetic crystal, although thin magnetic films also exhibit anisotropy. Most applications of ferromagnetic materials, however, involve polycrystalline arrays that are much easier to make.

Our definitions of susceptibility and permeability also depend on the assumption of linearity. Unfortunately, this is true only in the less interesting paramagnetic and diamagnetic materials for which the relative permeability rarely differs from unity by more than one part in a thousand. Some typical values of the susceptibility for diamagnetic materials are hydrogen, 2 x 105; copper, 0.9 x 105; germanium, 0.8 x 105; silicon, 0.3 x 105; and graphite, 12 x 105. Several representative paramagnetic susceptibilities are oxygen, 2 x 106; tungsten, 6:8 x 105; ferric oxide Fe2O3, 1.4 x 103; and yttrium oxide Y2O3, 0:53 x 106. If we simply take the ratio of B to μ0H as the relative permeability of a ferromagnetic material, typical values of μr would range from 10 to 100 000. Diamagnetic, paramagnetic, and antiferromagnetic materials are commonly said to be nonmagnetic.
