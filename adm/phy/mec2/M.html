Momento della quantità di moto; Isotropy of Space

Passiamo ora alla deduzione della legge di conservazione che deriva dall'isotropia dello spazio.
Questa isotropia significa che le proprietà meccaniche di un sistema isolato non cambiano, qualunque sia la rotazione nello spazio di tutto il sistema in blocco. Consideriamo pertanto il caso di una rotazionae infinitesima del sistema ponendo la condizione che la Lagrangiana resti invariata.
Sia δφ il vettore diretto come l'asse di rotazione e modulo pari all'angolo della rotazione infitesima δφ; il verso della rotazione risponde alla regola della vite. Sia l'origine del sistema di coordinate collocata sull'asse di rotazione. Owing to the rotation, every particle in the system is displaced by some distance δrα and if a particle has velocity vα, the direction of the velocity vector will change by δvα. Figure 1.10 shows the effect of the rotation on the position vector rα.

Cechiamo anzitutto a che cosa è uguale δra, l'incremento del raggio vettore, in una tale rotazione. Dalla figura 2,

|δra| = r sen θ δφ.

But since δr is perpendicular to both r and δφ, we can write

δr = δφ × r  (2.4.1)

Similarly,

δv = δφ × v (2.4.2)

Se lo spazio è isotropo, la rotazione non avrà effetto sulla Lagrangiana, così che δL = 0,

δ L = ∑ a ( ∂ L ∂ r a ⋅ δ r a + ∂ L ∂ v a ⋅ δ v a ) = 0

(Note that in this situation the velocities of the particles are not constant). Again, we use Lagrange's equation to replae δL/δra con d/dt (&partial;L/&partial;va) = d/dt(pa) = ṗa and write

Σa (ṗa ⋅ δra + pa ⋅ va) = 0

Replacing (2.4.1) and (2.4.2), yields

Σa (ṗa ⋅ δφ x ra + pa ⋅ δφ x va) = 0

Interchanging the dot and the cross, using the property of the scalar triple product we obtain

Σa δφ ⋅ (ra x ṗa + ṙa x pa) = δφ ⋅ Σa d/dt(ra x pa)

Consequently, since δφ ≠ 0,

Σa d/dt(ra x pa) = dM/dt = 0

where M is the total angular momentum. That is, the isotropy of space leads to the conservation of angular momentum. Like the linear momentum, it is additive, whether or not the particles in the system interact.
There are no other additive integrals of the motion. thus every closed system has seven such integrals: energy, three components of momentum, and three components of angular momentum.
Since the definition of angular momentum involves the radius vectors of the particles, its value depends in general on the choice of origin. The radius vector ra and r'a of a given point relative to origins at a distance a apart are related by ra = r'a + a. Hence

M = Σa ra x pa
= Σa r'a x pa + a x Σa pa
= M' + a x P

It is seen from this formula that the angular momentum depends on the choice of origin except when the system is at rest as a whole (i.e. P = 0). This indeterminacy, of course, does not affect the law of conservation of angular momentum, since momentum is also conserved in a closed system.
We may also derive a relation between the angular momenta in two intertial frames of reference K and K', of which the latter moves with velocity V relative to the former. We shall suppose that the origins in the frames K and K' coincide at a given instant. Then the radius vectors of the particles are the same in the two frames, while their velocities are related by va = va' + V. Hence we have

M = Σa mara x va = Σa marav'a + Σa mara x V

The first sum on the RHS is the angular momentum M' in the frame K'; using in the second sum the radius vector of the centre of mass, we obtain

M = M' + μR x V

This relation gives the law of transformation of angular momentum from one frame to another, corresponding to formula (2.3.1) for momentum and (2.3.5) for energy.
If the frame K' is that in which the system considered is at rest as a whole, then V is the velocity of its centre of mass, μV its total momentum P relative to K, and

M = M' + R x P

In other words, the angular momentum M of a mechanical system consists of its "instrinsic angular momentum", M', in a frame in which it is at rest and the angular momentum R x P due to its motion as a whole.
Although the law of conservation of all three components of angular momentum (relative to an arbitrary origin) is valid only for a closed system, the of conservation may hold in a more restricted form even for a system in an external field. It is evident from the above derivation that the comonent of angular momentum along an axis aobut which the field is symmetrical is always conserved, for the mechanical properties fo the system are unaltered by any rotation about that axis. Here the angular momentum must, of course, be defined relative to an origin lying on the axis.
The most important important case is that of a centrally symmetric field, i.e. one in which the potential energy depends only on the distance from some particular point (the centre). It is evident that the component of angular momentum along any axis passing through the centre is conserved in motion in such a field. In other words, the angular momentum M is conserved provided that it is defined with respet to the centre of the field.
Another example is that of a homogeneous field in the z-direction; in such a field, the component Mz of the angular momentun is conserved, whichever point is taken as the origin.
The component of angular momentum along any axis (say the z-axis) can be found by differentiation of the Lagrangian:

M z = ∑ a ∂ L ∂ ϕ ˙ a

where the coordinate φ is the angle of rotation about the z-axis. This is evident from the above proof of the law of conservation of angular momentum, but can also be proved directly. In cylindrical coordinates r, φ z we have (substituting xa = ra cos φa, ya = ra sin φa)

M z = ∑ a m a ( x a y ˙ a − y a x ˙ a ) = ∑ a m a r a 2 ϕ ˙ a  (2.4.4)

From the expression of T and the definition of your coordinates:

x = r cos ⁡ ( ϕ ) , x ˙ = r ˙ cos ⁡ ( ϕ ) − r sin ⁡ ( ϕ ) ϕ ˙ , y = r sin ⁡ ( ϕ ) , y ˙ = r ˙ sin ⁡ ( ϕ ) + r cos ⁡ ( ϕ ) ϕ ˙ , z ˙ = z ˙ .

Squaring the dotted terms and summing over give the Lagrangian is, in terms of these coordinates,

L = 1 2 ∑ a ( r ˙ a 2 + r a 2 ϕ ˙ a 2 + z ˙ a 2 ) − U  (2.4.5)

and substitution of this in (2.4.3) gives (2.4.4).

Problems

Problem 1. Obtain expressions for the Cartesian components and the magnitude of the angular momentum of a particle in cylindrical coordinates r,φz.

Solution. Replacing the cylindrical coordinates in the caterian components of the angular momentum Mx = ypz − zpy, My = zpx − xpz, Mz = xpy − ypx.

M x = m ( r z ˙ − z r ˙ ) sin ⁡ ϕ − m r z ϕ cos ⁡ ϕ M y = − m ( r z ˙ − z r ˙ ) cos ⁡ ϕ − m r z ϕ sin ⁡ ϕ M z = m r 2 ϕ ˙ M 2 = m 2 r 2 ϕ ˙ 2 ( r 2 + z 2 ) + m 2 ( r z ˙ − z r ˙ ) 2

Problem 2. The same as Problem 1, but in spherical coordinates r,θ,φ.

Solution.

M x = − m r 2 ( θ ˙ sin ⁡ ϕ + ϕ sin ⁡ θ cos ⁡ θ cos ⁡ ϕ ) , M y = m r 2 ( θ ˙ cos ⁡ ϕ − ϕ sin ⁡ θ cos ⁡ θ sin ⁡ ϕ ) , M z = m r 2 ϕ ˙ sin 2 ⁡ θ M 2 = m 2 r 4 ( θ ˙ 2 + ϕ ˙ 2 sin 2 ⁡ θ )

Problem 3. Which components of momentum P and angular momentum M are conserved in motion if the following fields?

  1. the field of an infinite homogeneous plane;

  2. that of an infinite homogeneous cyclinder;

  3. that of an infinite homogeneous prism;

  4. that of two points;

  5. that of an infinite homogeneous half-plane;

  6. that of a homogeneous cone;

  7. that of a homogeneous circular torus;

  8. that of an infinite homogeneous cylindrical helix.

Solution. The projection of momentum and of angular momentum onto a symmetry axis of the given field are conserved because the mechanical properties (Lagrangian and equation of motion) are not changed by a translation along this axis, or by a rotation around it. For the component of angualr momentum, this is only valid if the angular momentum is defined with respect to the centre of the field, and not with respect to an arbitrary spatial point. The momentum or the component of momentum, respectively, remains conserved in the sense of Lagrangian mechanics, if, and only if, the potential of the field does not depend on the corresponding generalized coordinate. We can measure the field that such a distribution generates with a test particle.

  1. If the plane is the xy-plane, the rotation of the particle about the z-axis won't change its distance from the xy-plane, and the particle will expirience the same field. So we can affirm the z part of the angular momentum, Mz is conserved. A translation in a plane parallel to the xy-plane won't change the field experienced by the particle too, thus Px, Py are conserved as well.

  2. Mz, Pz (if the axis of the cylinder is the z-axis);

  3. As in b) Pz (if the edges of the prism are parallel to the z-axis), but there is no rotational symmetry around the z axis;

  4. Mz if the line joining the points is the z-axis;

  5. Py if the edge of the half-plane is the y-axis;

  6. Mz if the axis of the cone is the z-axis;

  7. Mz if the axis of the torus is the z-axis;

  8. the potential in the Lagrangian function does not change under a rotation through δφ about the z axdis, as long as one simultaneously shifts along the z axis by δz = (h/2π)δφ and a simultaneous rotation through δφ just conserves the symmetry of the potential; consequently, the variation of the Lagrange function vanishes, giving

    ∂L = 0 = (∂L/∂z)∂z + (∂L/∂φ) ∂φ

    From Lagrange's equation, we have

    (d/dt) pz = ∂L/∂z   and   (d/dt) Mz = ∂L/∂φ

    hence,

    (d/dt) (pzh/2π + Mz)∂φ = 0

    For arbitrary ∂φ it follows that

    (d/dt) (pzh/2π + Mz) = 0

    and, therefore

    (pzh/2π + Mz) = constant

    i.e. for the helix a certain linear combination of pz and Mz.
