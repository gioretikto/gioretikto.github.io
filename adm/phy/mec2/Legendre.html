Legendre transformation

A Legendre transform converts from a function of one set of variables to another function of a conjugate set of variables. Both functions will have the same units.

  1. Convert from the Lagrangian L(x, ẋ) to the Hamiltonian H(x, p). Here the velocity ẋ and the linear momentum p are conjugate variables, and both L and H have units of energy.

  2. Convert between the internal energy U, enthalpy H, Helmholtz free energy A, and Gibbs free energy G. The two conjugate pairs of variables are pressure p and volume V, and temperature T and entropy S. (Optionally, the chemical potential μ and number of particles N can be added in as another conjugate pair.) All of these thermodynamic potentials have units of energy.

    3. Suppose that f(x) is a twice-differentiable function that is strictly convex f'' > 0, one some interval. Then f' is a monotonic function. The equation m = f'(x) has a unique solution x = f'−1 (m) for any given values of m. We define g(m) = xm - f(x(m)) as the LT of f.

    We consider the intersection of the tangeto to f at the point (x0, f(x0)) with the y-axis. The tangent has the following equation:

    T(x) = f(x0) + f'(x0)(x − x0)

    The intersection with the y-axis g = T(0) therefore is

    g(x0) = -x0 f'(x0)

    and depends, of course on the point x0 under consideration. One calls the function g(x) for an arbitrary point x the Legendre transform of f<(x); it is

    g = f − xp with p = f'

    In other words, g(x) is the corresponding value of the intersection of the tangent to f at point (x, f(x) with the y-axis.

    Thermodynamic Potentials

    Total differentials of the different thermodynamics functions can be related to each other through the Legendre transformations:

    df = f1dx1 + f2dx2 + ... + fndxn

    wherer f is the dependent variable x1, x2, ..., xn are the independent variables, and f1 = (∂f/∂x1)x1, ... xn. Consider the new function

    g = f − f1x1

    one gets on differentiation:

    dg = df − f1dx1 -x1 df1

    or substituting the top equation:

    dg = x1df1 + f2 + ... + fndxn

    i.e., the independent variable x1 has changed to f1 and the dependent one from f to g.

    Applied to dU = −pdV + TdS the Legendre transformation leads to:

    H = U − (∂U/∂V)S V = U + pV

    and

    dH = V dp + TdS

    Similarly, since T = (∂U/∂S)V and also T = (∂H/∂S)p, one can write:

    F = U − (∂U/∂S)VS
    G = H − (∂H/∂S)pS

    This leads to the total differentials:

    dF = dU - TdS − SdT = −pdV − SdT
    dG = dH − TdS − SdT = VdP − SdT

    Mathematically H, F and G are thus Legendre transformations of the basi function of state U(S,V).

    Legendre transform from the Lagrangian L to the Hamiltonian H

    Suppose we have a mechanical system with a single generalized coordinate q and corresponding velocity !q . Then the Lagrangian is defined as the difference between the kinetic and potential energies, L(q, q̇) = K - U. We wish to transform to a new function H (q, p) where p is the momentum.

    The Legendre transformation is

    H = L − pq̇
