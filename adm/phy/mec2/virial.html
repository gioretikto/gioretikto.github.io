Mechanical similarity

Multiplication of the Lagrangian by any constant clearly does not affect the equations of motion. This fact (already mentioned in xx) makes possible, in a number of important cases, some useful inferences concerning the properties of the motion, without the necessity of actually integrating the equations.
Such cases include those where the potential energy is a homogeneous function of the coordinates, i.e., statisfies the condition

U(αr1,αr2, ..., αrn) = αkU(r1, r2, ..., rn)  (5.1)

where α is any constant and k the degree of homogeneity of the function.
Let us carry out a transformation in which the coordinates are changed by a factor α and the time by a factor β

rα → αrα,  t → βt

r' = αr,   t' = βt,   dr'/dt = (α/β) dr/dt

The similarity transformation, with the two positive constants α and β, transforms the system Σ into a system Σ', whose kinetic energy T' is related to the kinet energy T by

T' = (α/β)2T

Thus all the velocities va = dra/dt are changed by a factor α/β, and the kinetic energy by a factor α2/β2. The potential energy is multiplied by αk, thus to match the potential energy scaling, we must have

(α/β)2 = αk  or  β = α1−½k

and the Lagrangian T' − U' in the system Σ' is then just a multiple of the Lagrangian T − U in the system Σ. A change of all the coordinates of the particles by the same factor corresponds to the replacement of the paths of the particles by other paths, geometrically similar but differing in size. Thus we conclude that, if the potential energy of the system is a homogeneous function of degree k in the (Cartesian) coordinates the quations of motion permit a series of geometrically similar paths, and the times of the motion between corresponding points are in the ratio

t'/t = (l'/l)1−½k  (5.2)

where l'/l is the ratio of linear dimensions of the two paths. Not only the times but also any mechanical quantities at corresponding points at corresponding times are in a ratio which is a power of l'/l. For example, the velocities, energies and angular momenta are such that

v'/v = (l'/l)½k,   E'/E = (l'/l)k   M'/M = (l'/l)1+½k (5.3)

The following are some examples of the foregoing.
As we shall see later, in small oscillations the potentail energy is a quadratic function of the coordinates (k = 2). From (5.2) we find that the period of such oscillations is independent of their amplitude.
In a uniform field of force, the potential energy is a linear function of the coordinates, i.e. k = 1. From (5.2) we have t'/t = (l'/l)1/2. Hence, for example, it follows that, in fall under gravity, the time of fall is as the square root of the initial altitude.
In the Newtonian attraction of two masses tor the Coulomb interaciton of two charges, the potentail energy is inversely proportional to the distance apart, i.e. it is a homogeneous function of degree k = −1. Then t'/t = (l'/l)3/2, and we can state, fro instance, that the square of the time of revolution in the orbit is as the cuve of the size of the orbit (Kepler's third law).

The virial theorem

If the potential energy is a homogeneous function of the coordinates and the motion takes place in a finite region of space, there is a very simple relation between the time average values of the kinetic and potential energies, known as the virial theorem.
Since the kinetic energy T is a quadratic function of the velocities, we have by Euler's theorem on homogeneous functions Σ va ⋅ ∂T/∂va = 2T, or, putting ∂T/∂va = pa, the momentum,

Let us average this equation with respect to time. The average value of any function of time f(t) is defined as

It is easy to see that, if f(t) is the time derivative dF(t)/dt of a bounded function F(t), its mean value is zero. For

Let us assume that the system executes a motion in a finite region of space and with finite velocities. Then Σ pa ⋅ ra is bounded, and the mean value of the first term on the right-hand side of (5.4) is zero. In the second term we replace ṗa by −∂U/∂ra in accordance with Newton's equations, obtaining

2T = Σa ra ⋅ ∂U/ra (5.5)

If the potential energy is a homogeneous function of degree k in the radius vector ra, then by Euler's theorem equation (5.4) becomes the required relation

2T̄ = kU (5.6)

Since T + U = E = E, the relation (5.6) can also be expressed as

U = 2E/(k+2),   T = kE/(k + 2)

which expresses U and T in terms of the total energy of the system.
In particular, for small oscillation (k = 2) we have T = U, i.e. the mean values of the kinetic and potential energies are equal. For a Newtonian interaction (k = − 1) 2T = −U, and E = − T, in accordance with the fact that, in such an interaction, the motion takes place in a finite region of space only if the total energy is negative.
