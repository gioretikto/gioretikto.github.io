Equazioni differenziali ordinarie

An ordinary differential equation (ODE) is an equation that relates a function y(x) to some of its derivatives y(n) (x) = dn y/dxn . It is usual to call x the independent variable and y the dependent variable, and to write the most general ordinary differential equation as

F(x,y,y',y'',..., y(n)) = 0  (11.1.1)

The number n in (11.1.1) is called the order of the ordinary differential equation, and it is the order of the highest derivative of y that occurs in the equation. For example

y′(x) = f (x) (11.1.2)

with f a known function. Equation (11.1.2) is called homogeneous if f (x) ≡ 0 otherwise nonhomogeneous. For example, the harmonic equation mẍ + kẋ = 0 is inhomogeneous, but mẍ + kx = sin(ωt) is inhomogeneous.

The fact that there is only one independent variable means that we are dealing with ordinary (as opposed to partial) differential equations. We further assume that the coeffiencts (a,b,c) are independent of time, although d is either zero or time-dependent.
If each derivative occurs linearly, (i.e. term like ÿ2 or ÿ3) then the DE is said to be linear otherwise is nonlinear.
A solution of an ordinary differential equation is a function y = f(x) that, when substituted into the equation, makes it identically zero over the interval on which the equation is defined. A solution of an nth order equation that contains n arbitrary constants is called the general solution of the equation. If the arbitrarygeneral and particular solutions, and integral curves constants in the general solution are assigned specific values, the result is called a particular solution of the equation.

Mostriamo un primp esempio elementare, ma storicamente significativo, di modello differenziale.

Example 1. Modello di Malthus per la dinamica delle popolazioni, 1798. Si considera una popolazione che evolve isolata e i cui unici fattori di evoluzioni sono fertilità e mortalità. Indicheremo con N(t) il numero di individui presenti al tempo t, con λ (rispettivamente, μ) il numero di nuovi nati (morti) per individuo nell'unità di tempo, cosicché in un tempo di durata h il numero di nuovi nati e di morti sarà rispettivamente λhN(t) and μhN(t). Perciò, la variazione del numero di individui in un tempo h sarà:

N(t + h) − N(t) = λhN(t) − μhN(t) (11.1.4)

Dividing both sides of the previous equation by h, yields:

[N(t+h) − N(t)] / h = (λ − μ) N(t) (11.1.5)

Assuming 11.1.5 valid for any time interval h; taking the limit of both sides for h → 0 (considering the function N(t) differentiable

Ṅ(t) = (λ − μ) N(t)  11.1.6

This is a first order linear differential equation; the quantity ε = λ − μ si chiama potenziale biologico. Writing 11.5 in the form N'/N = ε, we observe that the increase (or decrease) ration of N is constant. The previous 11.5 equation does not admit a single solution but infinite. As we shall see soon, they are all of the form

N(t) = N0eεt  11.1.7

where c is an arbitrary real constant. The fact that 11.1.7 is a solution of 11.1.6 means that if we replace back 11.1.7 into 11.1.6 we obtain an identity.
