Euclidean Spaces

Definition 12.1.1. For each positive integer k, let ℝk be the set of all ordered k-tuples

x = (x1, x2, ..., xk)

where x1, ..., xk ∈ ℝ, called coordinates of x. The elements of ℝk are called points, or vectors, especially when k > 1. We shall denote vectors by boldfaced letters. If y = (y1, y2, ..., yk) and if α ∈ ℝ, put

x + y = (x1+y1, ..., xk+y1)
αx = (αx1, ..., αxk)

so that x + y ∈ ℝk and αx ∈ ℝk. This defines addition of vectors, as well as multiplication of a vector by a real number (a scalar). These two operations satisfy the commutative, associative, and distributive laws (the prood is trivial, in view of the analogous laws for the real numbers) and make ℝk into a vector space over the real field. The zero element of ℝk (sometiemd called the origin or the null vector) is the point 0, all of whose coordinates are 0.
We also define the so-called "inner product" (or scalar product) of x and y by

x ⋅ y = ∑ i = 1 k x i y i

and the norm of x by

| x | = x ⋅ x = ∑ i = 1 k x i y i

The structure now defined (the vector space ℝk with the above inner product and norm) is called euclidean k-space.

Theorem 12.1.2. Suppose x,y,z ∈ ℝk, and α ∈ ℝ. Then

  1. |x| ≥ 0;

  2. |x| = 0 iff x = 0;

  3. |αx| = |α||x|;

  4. |x ⋅ y| = |x| |y|;

  5. |x + y| = |x| + |y|;

  6. |x − z| = |x − y| + |y − z|.

Proof. a), b), and c) are obvious, and d) is an immediate consequence of the Schwarz inequality. By d) we have

|x + y|2 = (x + y)⋅(x + y)
= x ⋅ x + 2x ⋅ y + y ⋅ y
≤ |x|2 + 2|x||y| + |y|2
= (|x| + |y|)2

so that e) is proved. Finally f) follows from e) if we replace x by x −y adn y by y − z. □

12.1.3 Remarks Theorem 12.1.2 (a), (b), and (f) will allow us to regard ℝk as a metric space.
ℝ1 (the set of all real numbers) is usually called the line, or the real line.
Likewise, ℝ2 is called the plane, or the complex plane (compare Definitions 1.11.1 and 12.1.1). In these two cases the norm is just the absolute value of the corre­sponding real or complex number.
