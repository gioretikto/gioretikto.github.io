Fundamentals theorems on limits

Teorema 6.15 (Uniqueness of the limit). Suppose f admits (finite or infinite) limit ℓ for x → x0. Then f admits not other limit for x → x0.

Proof. We employ a proof by contradiction, showing that the existence of a second limit ℓ' ≠ ℓ will lead to an absurd. We consider the case of finite ℓ' and ℓ: the other cases can be derived in a similar manner. Since ℓ' ≠ ℓ, there exists a neighborhood I(ℓ') for ℓ' and one I(ℓ) for ℓ which are disjoint, i.e.

I(ℓ') ∩ I(ℓ) = ∅

It is enough to consider for each point a neighborhood of ray ε less or equal to the semidistance between the centers, i.e.

ε ≤ |ℓ - ℓ'|/2

Considering the neighborhood I(ℓ) of ℓ, from the hypothesis limx → x0 f(x) = ℓ follows there exists a neighborhood I'(x0) of x0 such that

∀x ∈ dom f,   x ∈ I(ℓ) \ {x0} ⇒ f(x) ∈ I(ℓ)

Similarly considering a neighborhood I(ℓ') of ℓ', from limx → x0 f(x) = ℓ', follows

∀x ∈ dom f,   x ∈ I(ℓ') \ {x0} ⇒ f(x) ∈ I(ℓ')

The intersection of the two neighborhoods I(ℓ') e I(ℓ) is still a neighborhood of x0, containing infinite elements of dom f, because f is defined across the whole neighborhood of x0 (not necessarily in x0). Thus if x* ∈ dom f is a generic element belonging to both neighborhoods and different from x0, it results

f(x*) ∈ I(ℓ') ∩ I(ℓ)

thus the two neighborhoods I(ℓ') and I(ℓ) are disjoint. But this is contradiction because I(ℓ') ∩ I(ℓ) = ∅.□

Corollary 6.15 Let f be continuos on the closed interval [a,b], then f is bounded in [a,b].

The following corollary precices what's the sign of f in the neighborhood of x0.

Corollary 6.16 (permanennce of sign) Suppose f admits (finite or infinite) limit ℓ for x → x0. If ℓ > 0 or ℓ = +∞, there exists a neighborhood I(x0) of x0 such that f is striclty positive in I(x0) \ {x0}. An analogous result is found for the negative sign.

Proof. Suppose ℓ to finite and positive. Consider the neighborhood Iε(ℓ) of ℓ with ray ε ℓ/2 > 0. By the definition of limit, there exists a neighborhood I(x0) of x0 such that

∀x ∈ dom f,   x ∈ I(x0) \ {x0} ⇒ f(x) ∈ Iε(ℓ)

Observing that Iε(ℓ) = (ℓ/2, 3ℓ/2) (0, +∞), we conclude that all values of f are strictly positives.

If ℓ = +∞, it is enough to single out a neighborhood IA (+∞) = (A, +∞) of +∞ (with A > 0) and apply the definition of limit.□
