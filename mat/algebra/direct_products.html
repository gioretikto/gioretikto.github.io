Direct Products

The Cartesian product, with operations defined ooordinatewise, allowed us to construct new rings from known ones. The same is true for groups. Direct products are an easy way to construct larger groups from smaller ones. This construction yields all finite abelian groups.

Given two groups G and H, the (direct) product group G x H is the cartesian product with the operation

(g,h) ⋅ (g',h') = (gg', hh')

It is easy to verify that G x H is a group. For G and H abelian, with groups operations written as addition, often the direct product is written instead as a (direct) sum.

G ⊕ H

Theorem 7.17.1 Let H and K finite cyclic groups of order m and n respectively. Then H x K is cyclic iff gcd(m,n) = 1.

Proof. Assume H x K is cyclic. Suppose gcd(m,n) = d and (h,k) is a generator of H x K. Now

(h,k)mn/d = ((hm)n/d, (kn)m/d) = (e,e) ⇒ mn = |(h,k)| ≤ mn/d ⇒ d = 1

Thus |H| and |K| are relatively prime.

For the converse suppose H = ⟨h⟩ and K = ⟨k⟩ of order m an n respectively, and gcd(m,n) = 1. Let d be a positive integer such that (h,k)d = (e,e). Then hd = e and kd = e. Since o(h) = m and o(k) = n, we have m|d and n|d. Now gcd(m,n) = 1. hence mn| d and so mn ≤ d (see Corollary 2.19). Thus it follows for definition of order that o(h,k) = mn and hence H x K is a cyclic group.   □

|(h,k)| = lcm(m,n) = mn = |H x K|

so (h,k) is a generator of H x K which implies that H x K is cyclic.   □

Proposition 7.17.2. Let H and K be normal subgroups of a group G, such that H ∩ K = {e} and the product HK = {hk: h ∈ H, K ∈ K} = G. Then G is isomorphic to H x K. Conversely, if G is isomorphic to a direct product J1 x J2 of groups, then there are normal subgroups H and K of G with H ∩ K = {e}, HK = G, H ≃ J1, and K ≃ J2.

Proof. Let H and K be normal subgroups of a group G with H n K = {e} and HK = G. First by Exercise xx every element of G can be written in a unique way in the form hk. Define φ : G ⟶ H x K by φ(hk) = (h,k). The conditions H n K = {e} and HK = G ensure that φ is one-to-one and onto, respectively. The “morphism” aspect of an isomorphism depends on H and K being normal subgroups. Let’s start with the right side of the equality φ(ab) = φ(a)φ(b): φ(h1 k1)φ(h2k2) = (h1, k1 )(h2, k2) = (h1 h2, k1 k2)= φ(h1h1k2k2). For φ(h1k1h2k2) to equal this quantity, we need h1k1h2k2 = h1k1h2k2. We can cancel h1 on the left of each side of the equation and k2, on the right. Thus to finish the isomorphism we need commutativity for elements of H with those of K: h2k1 = k1h2. Let’s start with the right side k1h2. By the definition of K being normal (aK = Ka), there is k3 so that k1h2 = h2k3. Similarly since H is normal, there is h3 so that k1h2 = h3k1. So h2k3 = h3k1. By uniqueness from Exercise 6.4.10(a) h2 = h3 and k1 = k3, showing commutativity and finishing the isomorphism. See Exercise 6.4.10(b) for the converse.  ■
