Modular Arithmetic

The finite set ℤn, is closely related to the infinite set ℤ. So it is natural to ask if it is possible to define addition and multiplication in ℤn and do some reasonable kind of arithmetic there. To define addition in ℤn, we must have some way of taking two classes in ℤn and producing another class− their sum. Because addition of integers is defined, the following tentative definition seems worth investigating: The sum of the classes [a] and [c] is the class containing a + c or, in symbols,

Definition 4.6. Let [a] and [b] be elements of ℤn, then By proposition 4.2 the following operations can be defined

[a]n + [b]n := [a + b]n

[a]n ⋅ [b]n := [a x b]n

Example 1. In ℤ5; we have [3] + [4] = [3 + 4] = [7] = [2] and [3] ⋅ [2] = [3 ⋅ 2] = [6] = [1].

Proposition 4.7. Addition and multiplication ℤn x ℤn → ℤn are well defined functions: that is independent of which class representative is chosen.

Proof. It is clear that the rule [a]n + [b]n yields an element of ℤn (closure of the operation), but the uniqueness of this result needs to be verified. In other words, closure is obvious, but we need to show that the operation is well-defined. To do this, suppose that [a] = [c] and [b] = [d]. Then

[a] = [c] ⇒ a ≡ c (mod n)  and   [b] = [d] ⇒ b ≡ d (mod n).

By Proposition 4.0.5,

a + b ≡ c + d (mod n),

and therefore [a + b]n = [c + d]n. A similar argument holds true for multiplication.□

New Notation

We have been very careful to distinguish integers in ℤ and classes in ℤn, and have even used dilferent symbols for the operations in the two systems. By now, however, you should be reasonably comfortable with the fundamental ideas and familiar with arithmetic in ℤn. So we shall adopt a new notation that is widely used in mathematics, even though it has the flaw that the same symbol represents two totally different entities. Whenever the context makes clear that we are dealing with ℤn we shall abbreviate the class notation "[a]" and write simply "a". In ℤ6 for instance, we might say 6 = 0, which is certainly true for classes in ℤ6 even though it is nonsense if 6 and 0 are ordinary integers. We shall use an ordinary plus sign for addition in ℤn, and either a small dot or juxtaposition for multiplication. For example, in ℤ5 we may write things like

4 + 1 = 0   or   3 ⋅ 4 = 2   or   4 + 4 = 3

On those few occasions where this usage might cause confusion, we will returo to the brackets notation for classes.

Molto semplice: nella prima tabella, incrociando una riga, intestata da i, con una colonna, intestata da j, otteniamo un numero che rappresenta la classe [i + j]5. Notice the different structure of ℤ4 and ℤ5, the first set has zero divisors (see Definition 2.4) whilst ℤ5 is not. Thus ℤ4 is not an integral domain, but ℤ5 is. If we compare Cayley's table for ℤ5 and ℤ6, we note that the addition table of ℤ6 ℤ5 is similar to that of ℤ5 but the multiplicative tables are different. In ℤ5, esclusa la riga intestata da zero, che contiene solo zeri, in ogni riga sono rappresentati (una ed una sola volta) tutti gli elementi di ℤ5. Nel caso di ℤ6, invece, questo accade solo per la seconda e l’ultima riga, quelle intestate da 1 e 5, non per le altre. Questa differenza è molto significativa: ci dice che ℤ5 e ℤ6 sono strutture algebriche molto diverse tra loro. In ℤ5 any element different from zero is invertibile, i.e. multiplied for a specific element yields [1]5, which is, the neuter element with regards to multiplication in ℤ5. Questo si vede dalla tavola di Cayley per la moltiplicazione di ℤ5: in ogni riga, esclusa quella intestata da 0, appare 1. Ad esempio, nella riga intestata da 2 abbiamo trovato 1 in corrispondenza della colonna intestata da 3, perché [2]5 · [3]5 = [1]5; possiamo esprimere questo fatto dicendo, appunto, che [2]5 è invertibile in ℤ5 e che [3]5 is the inverse of [2]3. In questo ℤ5 behaves as the ring ℚ of rational numbers or ℝ of real numbers (rings of this kind are known as fields), in cui tutti gli elementi diversi da zero hanno inverso (il reciproco) o, equivalentemente, in cui è sempre possibile dividere un elemento per un elemento diverso da zero, e non come ℤ, in cui queste proprietà non valgono. Al contrario, ℤ6, ha proprietà più deboli di quelle che valgono in ℤ. Ad esempio, in ℤ (come in ℚ ed in ℝ, ed anche in ℤ5) vale la legge di annullamento del prodotto: se il prodotto tra due numeri è zero, uno dei due numeri deve essere zero. Questo non vale in ℤ6, come si vede anche dalla tabella; ad esempio [2]6· [3]6 = [0]6, benché [2]6 ≠ [0]6 ≠ [3]6. Vediamo quindi che ℤ, ℤ5 e ℤ6 sono strutture algebriche di natura molto diversa tra loro. Senza entrare in dettagli che qui sarabbero fuori luogo, diciamo che la natura dei singoli anelli ℤn è determinata dalle proprietà aritmetiche dei numeri n che li definiscono. For example, we can prove that ℤn is a field only if n is a prime number.

Multiplicative Inverses in ℤn

Thorem 4.7. An element [a] of ℤn has a multiplicative inverse in ℤn if and only if a and n are relatively prime.

Proof Suppose first that [a] has a multiplicative inverse [b] in ℤn. Then

[a] [b] = [1]

This means that

[ab] = [1]   and   ab ≡ 1   (mod n)

Therefore,

ab − 1 = nk

for some integer k, and

a(b) + n(−k) = 1.

By Corollary 4.21, we have (a,n) = 1.

Converserly, if (a,n) = 1, then Theorem 2.16 guarantess the existence of a solution s to the congruence

as ≡ 1   mod n

Thus

[a] [s] = [1]

and [a] has a multiplicative inverse [s] in ℤn.□

Proposition 4.8. Every nonzero element of ℤn has a multiplicative inverse if and only if n is a prime.

Proof. The corollary follows from the fact that n is a prime if and only if every integer a such that 1 ≤ a < n is relatively prime to n.

Example 4.9. The elements of ℤ15 that have multiplicative inverses can be listed by writing down those [a] that are such that (a, 15) = 1. These elements are [1], [2], [4], [7], [8], [11], [13], [14].
