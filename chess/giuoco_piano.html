Giuoco piano

The Italian Game is a family of chess openings beginning with the moves:

    1. e4 e5
    2. Nf3 Nc6
    3. Bc4

The name Giuoco Piano, is often wrongly considered synonymous with the Italian Game. The term Giuoco Piano for the lines where Black replies 3...Bc5.
The other option for Black is 3...Nf6, the Two Knights Defense. They are about equally popular, but the resulting positions usually have a very different character.

The Giuoco Piano (Italian: "Quiet Game"; also called the Italian Opening, is a chess opening beginning with the moves:

    1. e4 e5
    2. Nf3 Nc6
    3. Bc4 Bc5

Main line

Wilhelm Steinitz vs Curt von Bardeleben
"The Battle of Hastings" (game of the day Feb-23-2016)
Hastings (1895), Hastings ENG, rd 10, Aug-17

Both sides have brought out a Knight and a Bishop, the Bishops bear down on the vulnerable f-Pawns, and White is ready to castle. The most exciting play can follow if White now continues with 4 c3. White prepares to form a big centre of Pawns with 5 d4, which would also gain time by attacking the Black Bishop. Note too that 4 c3 opens a diagonal for the Queen and introduces possibilities of Qb3 adding to the attack on f7. Let us continue with the main line of analysis:

... Nf6

Black brings out another piece, attacks the e-Pawn and prepares to castle. Note that now 5 Ng5 would get White nowhere since 5 ... O-O defends the f-Pawn adequately.

Common alternatives to 3...Bc5 include 3...Nf6 (the Two Knights Defence) and 3...Be7 (the Hungarian Defence). Much less common are 3...d6 (the Semi-Italian Opening), 3...g6, 3...Nd4 (the Blackburne Shilling Gambit), and 3...f5 (the Rousseau Gambit).

Variations

The main continuations on White's fourth move are:

  • 4.c3, the Main line, see below.

  • 4.b4, the Evans Gambit, in which White offers a pawn to speed his development. This opening was popular

  • in the 19th century, more than the standard Giuoco Piano.

  • 4.d3, the Giuoco Pianissimo, see below.

  • 4.0-0, often with the intention of meeting 4...Nf6 with 5.d4, the Max Lange Gambit, with similar ideas to the Italian Gambit but with some transpositional differences.

    • Other continuations are:

    • 4.Nc3 Nf6, the Four Knights Variation.

    • 4.d4, the Italian Gambit, in which White opens up the centre, avoiding the quiet lines of the Giuoco Piano and Giuoco Pianissimo.

    • 4.Bxf7+? Kxf7 5.Nxe5+ Nxe5, the Jerome Gambit, an unsound opening where White sacrifices two pieces in the hope of exposing Black's king and obtaining a mating attack.

    1 e4 Excellent opening move. White anchors a pawn in the center and opens lines for his queen and bishop.

    1 .. e5 Very Good! Black insists on a fair share of the centre and liberates two of his pieces.

    2 Nf3! The Knight comes into play early in the game, in compliance with the precept: develop knights before bishops!

    3 Bc4 "The best attacking piece is the king's bishop" says Tarrash, so White puts this pieces to work and clears the way for early castling.

    3 Bc5 On this excellent square the bishop commands an important diagonal, exerts pressure on the centre and attacks a weak pawn. This deployment conforms to two golden rules for opening play:

    Place each piece as quickly as possible on the square where it is most effective.
    Move each piece only once in the opening.

    4 c3 White's primary object is to establish two pawns in the centre and with this move he intends to support an advance of the d-pawn. In advancing to c3, the pawn occupies a square that should be reserved for the b1-knight.

    4 ... Qe7 Very good! Black develops a piece while parrying the threat. If White persists in playing 5 d4, the continuation 5 ..exd4 cxd4 Qxe4+ wins a pawn.

    5 0-0 White postpones the advance of the d-pawn and moves his king to a safer place.

    5 ... d6 Strengthens the centre and supports the e-pawn and bishop. Now the c8-bishop can get into the game.

    Castle early in the game, preferably on the kingside.

    6 d4 With the hope that Black will exchange paws. This would leave White with an impressive line-up in the centre, while the c3-square is then available for his knight. If now 6...exd4 7 exd4 Qxe4, White punishes the pawn-snatching by 8 Re1 pinning the queen.

    6 ... Bb6 But Black need not capture! Now that his e-pawn is secure, the bishop simply retreats, still bearing down on the white centre from its new position. Despite its formidable appearance. White's pawn-centre is haky. The d-pawn is attacked three times, and White must keep a triple guard on it while trying to comple his development.

    9 h3 A coffe-house move! Weak players make this move instinctively in dire dread of having a piece pinned. Playing h3 or g3 after clastling creates an organic weakness that can never be remedied.

    9 ... Nf6 The knight swing into the fray with an attack on the e-pawn. This move conforms with a useful principle.

    10 dxe5 White exchanges, and opens up line for his pieces. Unfortunately this reacts in Black's favour, in accordance with the rule in these cases:

    Ruger - Gebhard - Dresden 1915

    1. e4 The most important target in the opening is to develop the pieces as fast as possible in order to occupy and control the centre. White's first move fixes a pawn in the centre and, as the first step in getting his pieces off the back rank, opens avenues for his queen and bishop.

    1 ... e5 Black equalizes the pressure in the centre and releases two pieces for action. He must be content with this, as he cannot hope at this early stage to wrest the initiative away from White by force.

    2. Nf3 It is good strategy to make developing moves that embody threats, as it cuts down the choice of reply.

    2 ... Nc6 Black saves his e-pawn and develops a piece. The knight comes into play efficiently: its action is directed toward infcluencing the centre. It defends the e5-pawn and attacks d4.

    3 Bc4 The bishop comes out, taking up a post where it commands a diagonal leading towards Black's king. It attacks a pawn which is peculiarly vulnerable as it is defendend only by the king.

    3 ... Bc5 Black follows suit, bringing his bishop out to the most suitable square. In the opening, a bishop is best placed for attack when it controls a diagonal passing through the centre or when it pins a hostile knight and renders it immobile.

    4. c3 Supporting an advance of the d-pawn with a view to controlling the centre. White postpones castling as his king is in no danger.

    4 ... Nf6 A powerful counter to the threat: Black develops a piece and attacks a pawn.

    5. d4 White meets this by attacking a piece.

    5 ... exd4 Pratically forced, since 5... Bd6 guarding the e-pawn is clumsy, since it blocks the d-pawn, while 5... Bb6 permits 6 dxe5 Nxe4 7. xf7+ Kxf7 8. Qd5+ and White regains his piece.

    6. cxd4 White's array of paswn looks impressive, but can the centre be maintained?

    6... Bb4+

    7 Nc3 White prefers this, which offers a pawn, to 7 Bd2 Bxd2, when the e-pawn is protected.

    7... O-O "Chess is not for timid sould" wrote Steinitz in a letter to Bachmann. Castling early in the game is generally sound strategy. In this case it is inappropriate, as White's centre is formidable and must be destroyed. Black must play 7... Nxe4, accepting the offer of a pawn, and take his chances of surviving Whatever attack might follow. It cannot be worse than this passive castling, which result in both his knights being evicted from their fine posts. Pillsbury gave as his rule, which he sais was absolute and vital: "Castle because you want to or because you must, but not because you can".

    8 d5 Ordinarly, such moves are susperct, as d5 should be occupied by a piece, but here the pawn dislodges the knight and prevents Black forever from freeing his game by ...d5

    8 ... Ne7 As good as there is. The shift to a5 is counted by 9. Bd3, and the knight is stranded at the side of the board.

    9. e5 Now a shot at the other knight!

    9 ... Ne4 Retreating to e8 does not look attractive, so Black plays to exchange knights.

    10. Qc2 Protects his own knight, which was twice attacked, and threatens the enemy's.

    10 ... Nxc3 Pratically forced, as defending the knight by 10...f5 allows 11 d6+ and the other knight falls.

    11 bxc3 This recapture gains a tempo: Black's bishop must lose a move retreating, while White's advantages multiply. Now he enjoys an open b-file for his rook and an extra diagonal for his dark-squared bishop.

    11. Bc5 Naturally this offers better chances tha moving to a5, the only other flight square.

    12. Ng5! ostensibly to scare Black by the threat of mate; actually, the purpose is to induce a weakining advance of one of the king's retinue of pawns.

    12 ... Ng6 The only efence. The kingside pawns remain intact, but Black has been compelled to place his pieces where White wants them. Under such circumstances the chances of Black putting up a good fight are not too bright

    13 h4 Threatening to move on to h5, dislodging the knight, and then mate with the queen.

    13. ... h6 What else is there? Black triees to drive off the menancig knight. He cannot do this by 13 ... f6 as 14 d6+ Kh8 15 Nf7+ wins the exchange.

    14 d6! A poweful stroke! Black's bishop is cut off from the defence, while a line is opened for White's bishop. White now threatens 15. Qxg6, as the pinned f-pawn could not capture the queen.

    14 ... hxg5 Black might as well take whatever material he can.

    15. hxg5! The knight will not run away (the penality being 16 Qh7#). Meanwhile, the rook has lovely open file, and the queen still threatens to capture.

    15 ... Re8 The king needs more room!

    16 Qxg6 Regaining the piece without relaxing the pressure. White threatens one mate in one, and two mates in two.

    16 ... Rxe5 Black consoles himself by equalazing the material and gives one despairing check before he yields.
