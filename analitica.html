In its original form Lagrange's equation made it possible to set up Newton's equations of motion, F = dp/dt, easily in terms of any set of genelarized coordinates, that is, any set of variable capable of specifying the positions of all particles in the system. The generalized coordinates subsume the rectangular Cartesian coordinates, of course, but also include angular coordinates such as those in the plane polar or spherical polar coordinates. The generalized coordinates also allow us to deal easily with constraints of motion, such as a ball constrained to move always in contact with the interior surface of a hemisphere;

We have two categories of constraints:

  • Geometric constraint: Only the positions of the particles of a system are restricted. For example, the coordinates of the particle m, constrained by a rigid rod, x,y,z should satisfy the following equation

    x2 + y2 + z2 = l2

    Therefore the coordinates of the particle m at any instant of time t, x(t),y(t), z(t), are not independent. Only two of them are independent.

  • Kinematic constraint: Both the position and velocity of the particles of a system are restricted. For example if a cylinder moves along the positive direction of the x axis, then the position of th center of the cylinder C must satisfy the following relationship (a geometric constraint)

    zC = R

    where zC is the position of the center of the cylinder along the z axis, and R is the radius of the cylinder.
